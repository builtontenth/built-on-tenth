Insurance replacement is the highest-ticket, highest-margin work in residential roofing. A retail replacement in most markets runs $14,000 to $22,000. An insurance replacement on the same house, with supplements properly filed, often runs $18,000 to $28,000 -- because the full scope includes every code-required component, every material at current pricing, and overhead and profit that retail jobs often don't capture.

Most roofing companies handle insurance jobs with the same process they use for retail: inspect, quote, send the estimate, follow up. That's the wrong process. Insurance claim jobs operate on a different timeline, involve a third party who controls the payout, and require documentation and negotiation skills that have nothing to do with your ability to install a roof.

The contractors who consistently get full payment on insurance jobs understand three things most don't:

Being present at the adjuster meeting changes the close rate from 35 to 45% to 65 to 75%. The supplement process -- going back to the insurance company after the initial scope -- is where most contractors leave $1,500 to $4,000 per job. And a low adjuster estimate isn't a final number; it's an opening position.

This article covers the full workflow, from the first call through final payment.

## Insurance vs. Retail: A Different Job With a Different Process

Before the workflow, the framing.

On a retail replacement job, the homeowner is the decision-maker and the checkwriter. Your job is to convince them the work is worth the price and get a signed contract.

On an insurance claim job, the homeowner is still the customer but the insurance company controls what gets paid and how much. The homeowner's decision is which contractor to use -- not whether to do the work. The work is happening because the insurance company has agreed it should. Your job is to document the full scope of damage, advocate for the homeowner's claim, and make sure the payout reflects actual repair costs and current pricing.

That distinction changes everything: your sales approach, your documentation process, your timeline, and what happens after the adjuster visit.

One more thing that separates these jobs: **insurance replacement close rates are higher than retail when you work the process correctly.** The homeowner isn't weighing cost against budget -- the insurance company is paying most or all of it. The variable is which contractor they trust to execute the claim and the work. Trust-building is the sale, not price-comparison.

## Step 1: The Initial Inspection -- Documentation First

When a homeowner calls after a storm event, your first visit has one primary purpose before anything else: documentation.

An adjuster who shows up later will spend 20 to 45 minutes on the roof. You have time to do a thorough inspection that the adjuster won't replicate. Everything you document before the adjuster visit becomes your argument for full scope.

**What to document on the initial inspection:**

Photos of every face of the roof. Close-up photos of shingle impact marks, bruising, or granule loss on representative samples. Photos of all roof penetrations -- pipe boots, vents, skylights, chimney flashing. Photos of gutters and downspouts for hail impacts. Photos of any visible decking issues if accessible. Measurements and a sketch of the roof layout.

Document damage beyond just the shingles: step flashing at walls, valley liner condition, drip edge, ridge cap, ridge ventilation. These are the components adjusters most commonly exclude from initial scopes, and documented evidence of their condition is your basis for including them later.

**The written inspection report:**

A reputable contractor provides the homeowner with a written report from the initial inspection. This serves two purposes: it demonstrates professionalism and thoroughness relative to competitors who just eyeball it, and it creates a documented record before the adjuster visit that can't be challenged as post-adjuster inflation.

If your company doesn't have an inspection report template, build one. It doesn't need to be elaborate -- a one-page summary of findings with photos attached establishes the record.

## Step 2: The Adjuster Meeting -- Why You Need to Be There

The adjuster meeting is the highest-leverage moment in an insurance claim job. **Roofing contractors present at adjuster meetings close at 65 to 75% on those jobs, compared to 35 to 45% for contractors who aren't present.**

The gap exists for two reasons.

First: you see what the adjuster includes and excludes in real time. An adjuster working fast on a catastrophe event may miss drip edge, ice and water shield, pipe boots, ridge ventilation, or code-required upgrades. If you're there when they're writing the scope, you can point out what's missing before the estimate is finalized. If you're not there, you're working from a completed document and arguing after the fact.

Second: your presence signals commitment to the homeowner. Most contractors hand off to the homeowner and say "let us know what the adjuster says." The ones who show up are making a visible investment in the outcome of the claim. Homeowners notice. When they're choosing between two contractors after the adjuster leaves, the one who showed up is almost always the one they call.

**How to be present at the adjuster meeting:**

When you schedule the inspection, ask the homeowner to let you know when the adjuster appointment is set and to request your presence. Your framing to the homeowner:

> "I'd like to be there when the adjuster comes out. Not to pressure anyone -- just so I can answer technical questions about the roof system if they come up, and make sure nothing gets missed. It protects your claim."

That framing is accurate and homeowner-serving. You're not there to argue with the adjuster. You're there as the homeowner's technical resource.

**At the adjuster meeting:**

Bring your inspection report and photos. Let the adjuster lead -- they're running the meeting. When they finish their inspection, ask if you can walk them through a few items you noted. Point out specific components with documentation: "The pipe boots are cracked and show impact damage -- here's the photo from our inspection. Are those included in your scope?" Ask about code upgrades: ventilation requirements, drip edge, ice and water shield if local code requires it. Document which items they agree to include and which they don't.

You're not arguing. You're presenting evidence. There's a significant difference in how adjusters receive those two approaches.

## Step 3: Reading the Adjuster's Estimate

After the adjuster visit, you'll receive an estimate -- typically prepared in Xactimate, the industry-standard claims estimating software used by nearly every major insurance carrier. Understanding this document is non-negotiable for any roofing company doing significant insurance volume.

**What Xactimate is:**

Xactimate is software developed by Verisk Analytics that generates line-item estimates for property claims using regional pricing databases. Every item in the estimate has a specific code, a unit of measurement, and a price pulled from Xactimate's regional database. A properly written Xactimate roof estimate includes 40 to 80 individual line items covering every component of the roofing system.

The key insight from United Policyholders: Xactimate estimates look authoritative and complete, but they are frequently inaccurate or incomplete. The software is generic, and an adjuster spending 30 minutes on your roof will miss things that someone spending two hours with prior knowledge of the system won't. The estimate is a starting point, not a final settlement.

**How to read it:**

Compare the adjuster's scope line by line against what you documented in your inspection. Check for these common omissions:

- Drip edge (frequently excluded, often code-required)
- Ice and water shield (required in many markets, often written as optional)
- Pipe boot replacements (commonly excluded if not visibly failed)
- Step flashing (often not broken out as a separate line item)
- Ventilation upgrades to meet current code
- Decking replacement if spacing or condition requires it
- Waste factor allowances on complex roof geometries
- Current material pricing (Xactimate databases can lag actual market prices, especially post-storm)
- Overhead and profit (applicable on jobs meeting general contractor scope criteria)

Also check unit pricing against current material costs. Construction material prices were up 42.4% above pre-pandemic levels as of late 2025. Xactimate's regional databases update monthly but may lag actual current market pricing, particularly in the weeks after a major storm event when local material supply is constrained.

## Step 4: The Supplement Process

A supplement is a formal request to the insurance company for additional funds beyond the initial estimate. It's submitted when the contractor's scope of work -- legitimately and accurately described -- exceeds what the adjuster included in the initial estimate.

**Supplements are standard practice.** Most roofing insurance claims require at least one supplement to capture the full scope of necessary repairs, according to multiple industry sources. Filing a supplement is not aggressive or adversarial -- it's the normal mechanism for ensuring the approved scope matches the actual work required. Contractors who don't file supplements either absorb the missing costs themselves or skip components the homeowner is entitled to under their policy.

**What gets supplemented most often:**

Code upgrades are the most consistently missed category. Local building codes may require drip edge, ice and water shield at eaves and valleys, specific ventilation, or decking replacement if existing decking doesn't meet spacing requirements. Adjusters may not know local code, and code-required items are covered under most homeowner policies as part of bringing the roof to current compliance.

After tear-off supplements cover damage that wasn't visible from the surface during the initial inspection: decking condition, compromised flashing that looked intact from outside, additional layers of shingles not visible until the top layer is removed. These are legitimate supplements because the damage genuinely couldn't be assessed until work started.

Material pricing supplements address the gap between Xactimate's database pricing and actual current market costs on materials. This is most applicable in the weeks after a major storm event or during periods of elevated material pricing.

**How to submit a supplement:**

Document everything. Photos of the condition requiring the supplement, references to local building code where applicable, and written justification for each line item requested. The supplement should be submitted in Xactimate format -- the same language the insurance company uses -- so comparisons are apples to apples.

Contractors without Xactimate familiarity have two options: learn it (Xactware offers training and certification, and a Level 2 certification means writing estimates in the same language adjusters use) or outsource supplements to a specialty service. Supplement companies typically charge 1 to 1.5% of the supplement amount or a flat fee, and can increase approval rates significantly on complex or heavily documented supplements.

**The supplement follow-up cadence:**

Submit the supplement with a complete documentation package and follow up with the adjuster or their desk team at 5 business days. Many supplements sit in a queue and don't move without a follow-up call. Keep a simple log of supplement submission dates and follow-up dates for every insurance job. Companies that track this systematically see faster approvals than ones that submit and wait.

## Step 5: How to Handle a Low Adjuster Estimate Without Losing the Homeowner

A low adjuster estimate is the most common friction point in insurance claim jobs. Handled correctly, it's an opportunity to demonstrate your expertise and deepen the homeowner's trust. Handled wrong, it creates confusion, damages the relationship, and sometimes loses the job.

**What not to do:**

Don't tell the homeowner the insurance company is lowballing them or acting in bad faith. Even if that's accurate, it puts the homeowner in the middle of a dispute they didn't sign up for. It also risks making them feel anxious or adversarial about a process they'd prefer to resolve smoothly.

Don't agree to do the work for whatever the insurance company approved if the approved amount doesn't cover the full legitimate scope. That either means absorbing costs yourself or cutting corners on the installation. Neither is a sustainable business practice.

**The right framing:**

Present the gap as a normal, solvable part of the process:

> "The adjuster's estimate is a good starting point, but it's missing a few items that are standard on this type of repair. That's pretty common -- adjusters are working fast and sometimes miss things. We'll put together a supplement that documents exactly what's needed and why, and submit it to your carrier. Most of the time this gets resolved within a couple of weeks. You don't need to do anything -- we handle the back and forth."

This framing does three things: it normalizes the situation, it takes the burden off the homeowner, and it positions you as an advocate rather than a critic of the insurance company.

**If the supplement gets denied:**

Ask for the specific reason in writing. Most denials are either documentation-related (the carrier needs more evidence for a specific line item) or scope-related (the carrier disputes whether a particular component needs replacement). Both are resolvable with additional documentation or a re-inspection.

If the gap is significant and the denial persists, the homeowner has the right to request a re-inspection or invoke the appraisal process under their policy. This is where a public adjuster can add value for large, heavily disputed claims -- though public adjusters typically charge 10 to 15% of the claim payout, so the math needs to support it.

## Step 6: The Follow-Up Timeline Through the Claims Process

Insurance claim jobs require a different follow-up cadence than retail jobs because the homeowner's decision timeline tracks the claims process, not just their personal preference.

**Week 1 to 2 after initial contact:** Confirm the adjuster appointment and your attendance. Send the inspection report. Keep communication warm and informative -- not sales-focused.

**Adjuster meeting day:** Follow up with the homeowner within 24 hours. "The adjuster looked thorough -- we'll review their estimate once it comes in and let you know if anything needs to be addressed."

**When the estimate arrives:** Review within 48 hours and contact the homeowner. If the scope is complete, move toward signing the contract. If there are gaps, explain what the supplement will cover and what the timeline looks like.

**Supplement submission:** Let the homeowner know it's submitted and give a realistic timeline -- typically two to four weeks for standard supplements. Set a follow-up date so they know when to expect an update.

**Supplement approval:** Contact immediately. Move toward scheduling. The homeowner has been in a holding pattern and is ready to move forward.

**Throughout the process:** One check-in per week if the process extends beyond three weeks. You're the advocate -- staying present and informed is part of the service.

This follow-up cadence mirrors the storm season follow-up structure covered in the [roofing phone operations article](/insights/why-roofing-companies-miss-60-percent-inbound-calls-storm-season). The channel and timing differ between an inbound storm call and an active insurance claim, but the principle is the same: consistent presence wins the job against competitors who go quiet between touchpoints. If the homeowner's last friction point is cash for the deductible, [roofing financing](/insights/how-offering-financing-closes-more-roofing-jobs) is often what gets the contract signed after scope is approved.

## Tracking Insurance Jobs Separately From Retail

Insurance replacement jobs and retail replacement jobs should be tracked separately in your CRM from the first contact. They have different close rates, different timelines, different revenue profiles, and different process requirements.

**Why it matters:**

If you're mixing insurance and retail in the same pipeline, your close rate numbers are blended in a way that hides which process is working and which isn't. A 42% close rate on retail and a 68% close rate on insurance blend to an apparent 55% that tells you nothing useful about either.

Separate tracking also lets you see your supplement success rate -- what percentage of supplements you file get approved, and what the average additional revenue per approved supplement is. That number, tracked over 12 months, tells you whether your documentation process is improving and what the supplement practice is worth to your business annually.

**Minimum CRM fields for insurance jobs:**

Job type (insurance / retail), adjuster visit date, adjuster present (yes/no), initial scope amount, supplement submitted (yes/no), supplement amount, supplement approved amount, total approved scope, and final contract amount. That data, run quarterly, shows you exactly where your insurance claim process is generating or losing revenue.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is a roofing insurance supplement?

A supplement is a formal request to the insurance company for additional payment beyond the initial adjuster's estimate. It covers legitimate costs that were missed, undervalued, or discovered after work began -- including code-required upgrades, hidden damage found after tear-off, and items the adjuster excluded. Supplementing is standard practice on most roofing insurance claims, not aggressive or confrontational. Most claims require at least one supplement to capture the full scope of necessary repairs.

### Why should roofing contractors attend the adjuster meeting?

Contractors present at adjuster meetings close 65 to 75% of those jobs compared to 35 to 45% for contractors who aren't present. Being there lets you point out missed line items in real time before the estimate is written, demonstrate technical expertise to the homeowner, and position yourself as an advocate for their claim rather than just another bidder. Homeowners choosing between contractors after the adjuster visit almost always select the one who showed up.

### What is Xactimate and why does it matter for roofing contractors?

Xactimate is property claims estimating software developed by Verisk Analytics and used by nearly every major insurance carrier to write adjuster estimates. A properly written roofing estimate in Xactimate includes 40 to 80 individual line items. Contractors who can read Xactimate estimates can identify missing line items immediately and write supplements in the same format the carrier uses -- which improves approval rates significantly. Contractors who can't read Xactimate are reviewing a document in a language they can't fully interpret.

### What are the most commonly missed line items on insurance roofing estimates?

The most consistently missed items: drip edge (often code-required but excluded), ice and water shield at eaves and valleys, pipe boot replacements, step flashing at walls, ventilation upgrades required by current code, decking replacement if spacing or condition requires it, waste factor on complex roof shapes, and current material pricing where Xactimate's database lags actual market costs. After tear-off supplements frequently add decking, underlayment condition, and flashing that wasn't assessable from the surface.

### How long does the supplement process take?

Standard supplements with complete documentation typically resolve in two to four weeks. Supplements with inadequate documentation, or at carriers running high claim volume after a major storm event, can take four to eight weeks. Following up with the adjuster or desk team at five business days after submission and every week thereafter moves the process faster than submitting and waiting. Keep a tracking log with submission dates and follow-up dates for every open supplement.

### Should I do the work for whatever the insurance company approves if it's less than my actual cost?

No. Either absorb the costs yourself -- which isn't sustainable -- or skip components the homeowner is entitled to under their policy, which isn't ethical. The correct approach is to file a supplement documenting why the approved amount doesn't cover the full legitimate scope, and to hold the start date until the supplement is resolved or the homeowner understands and accepts the scope the approved amount covers. Frame this to the homeowner as protecting their claim, not holding up their project.

### What's the difference between a supplement and a new claim?

A supplement is an adjustment to an existing approved claim -- it reopens or revises the estimate to include items missed or discovered after work began. It is not a new claim, does not typically affect the homeowner's deductible, and doesn't reset the claims process. A new claim would be required for a separate, unrelated damage event. Supplements are legal, common, and often necessary to ensure the original claim is fully and accurately paid.

### Should roofing contractors learn Xactimate or outsource supplements?

Both are viable. Companies with significant insurance claim volume (20+ insurance jobs per year) typically benefit from having someone in-house trained on Xactimate -- either an office staff member or an estimator. Xactware's Level 2 certification is the relevant credential. Companies with lower insurance volume often find it more efficient to outsource to supplement specialty companies that charge 1 to 1.5% of the supplement amount or a flat per-job fee. The ROI calculation is straightforward: if a supplement specialist recovers an average of $2,500 per job and charges $100 to $200 per supplement, the math works regardless of your volume.

### How do I handle a homeowner who wants to start work before the supplement is approved?

Explain the timing clearly: "We can schedule your start date now and plan around the supplement timeline. If it's approved before we start, we're good to go on the full scope. If it's still pending, we'll discuss whether to proceed on the initial approved scope and add the supplemented items once approved, or wait for the full approval. Most supplements resolve before we'd be ready to start anyway." This gives the homeowner a clear path forward without creating a commitment you can't deliver on.
