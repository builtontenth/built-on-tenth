You're spending somewhere between $3,000 and $15,000 per month on marketing. An agency sends you a report every month. The report has graphs. It shows your impressions went up. Your click-through rate improved. Cost per lead came down from $148 to $131.

The phone is ringing. Some jobs are getting booked. Revenue feels roughly where it was last year, maybe slightly up.

But you can't tell whether the agency is the reason for that, a partial reason, or irrelevant to it. You don't know what your cost per booked job is by channel. You don't know which of the leads the agency generated actually became revenue. You don't know if the $8,500/month you're paying is producing $8,500 in recoverable value or $85,000.

That uncertainty is not a knowledge problem. It is a reporting problem. And in most cases, it is a reporting problem the agency has no structural incentive to fix.

This article covers what legitimate marketing accountability looks like for HVAC companies, why the metrics most agencies report are the wrong ones, and the 10 questions that determine in about five minutes whether your agency is measuring what actually matters.

## The Metric That Changes Everything

Cost per lead is the number almost every HVAC marketing agency reports. It is also, according to people who run marketing for HVAC companies at scale, the most misleading number in the relationship.

Chris Lollini, owner of Reputation Igniter and an industry advisor cited by ACHR News, is direct about it: "Cost per lead is often misleading in HVAC. Cost per booked job tells the real story. Clicks, impressions, and rankings are inputs. Revenue is the outcome contractors should evaluate."

Chris Hunter, principal industry advisor at ServiceTitan, frames it the same way: "The biggest marketing myth in HVAC is that more leads mean more revenue. Quality and booking rate matter far more than volume. A $30 lead that never books is expensive. A $150 lead that turns into a replacement, a membership, and five-star reviews is profitable."

The gap between cost per lead and cost per booked job is where most of the confusion lives. A channel producing 80 leads at $45 each looks better than one producing 25 leads at $120 each. But if the first channel books at 28% and the second books at 70%, the math inverts: the "expensive" channel produces booked jobs at $171 each, the "cheap" channel produces them at $161 each -- and that's before accounting for average ticket differences by channel. The full math is broken down in the [cost per lead vs. cost per booked job article](/insights/cost-per-lead-vs-cost-per-booked-job-contractor), which is the conceptual layer most agency reports skip.

Cost per lead cannot tell you any of that. It can only tell you what the agency spent to generate a contact. What happened to the contact after it arrived is invisible in a cost-per-lead report.

## Why Agencies Report the Wrong Numbers

This is not primarily a competence problem. It is a structure problem.

Most marketing agencies are compensated as a percentage of ad spend, a flat monthly retainer, or a combination. Their revenue goes up when your ad budget goes up. Their revenue stays the same whether the leads they generate book at 30% or 70%.

That structure creates an incentive to report the metrics that make spend look productive -- impressions, clicks, call volume, cost per lead -- rather than the metrics that show whether spend produced revenue. Cost per booked job requires connecting ad data to booking data to revenue data. That connection lives partly in the ad platform (which the agency controls), partly in your call tracking (which may or may not be set up), and partly in your CRM or field service software (which the agency usually can't see).

Bridging that gap takes work the agency isn't paid to do and produces a number that may not make the agency look good. So it doesn't get bridged.

This is not an accusation. It is a description of how the incentive structure works. As Hunter noted in the ACHR News analysis: "When contractors connect marketing, call center, sales, and revenue data inside platforms like ServiceTitan, they move from 'How many leads did we get?' to 'How much revenue did this channel actually produce?' That's where real ROI lives." The operative word is "when." Most contractors haven't made that connection. Most agencies aren't helping them make it.

The result is that roughly 70% of HVAC companies report dissatisfaction with their marketing providers, according to industry data compiled by Amra and Elma. That dissatisfaction is rarely about the agency doing obviously wrong things. It is almost always about a persistent inability to answer the one question that matters: is this producing revenue?

## What Legitimate Reporting Looks Like

Legitimate marketing accountability for an HVAC company connects every dollar spent to every job booked. Not approximately. Not directionally. Specifically, by channel, monthly.

The report you should be receiving every month includes:

**Cost per booked job by channel.** Not cost per lead. Cost per booked job: total spend on the channel divided by jobs that were sourced from that channel and appeared on the dispatch board as completed work. This requires call tracking connected to your scheduling system. If your agency says this can't be done, what they mean is they haven't set it up.

**Booking rate on leads generated.** Of the inbound calls or form submissions the agency's campaigns generated, what percentage became scheduled appointments? This number sits at the intersection of marketing quality and CSR performance. A booking rate below 55% on paid leads usually indicates either low-intent traffic, CSR handling issues, or both. Your agency should be helping you identify which.

**Revenue attributed by channel.** How much total invoiced revenue traces back to leads generated by each channel this month? This is the number that turns a marketing conversation into a business conversation. It requires your field service platform to tag jobs by lead source -- which is a one-time setup, not ongoing work. If it isn't set up, ask why.

**Channel-level trend over 90 days.** Any channel can have a good month or a bad month. The 90-day trend shows whether performance is improving, stable, or declining. Agencies that only report monthly snapshots without trend context are hiding variance behind timing.

**Your ad account ownership.** This isn't a reporting metric, but it determines whether you can verify everything above independently. If your Google Ads account, your LSA account, your call tracking data, and your website analytics are in your name with you as administrator, you can check the numbers anytime. If they're in the agency's account, you are receiving a summary of data you don't have direct access to -- which changes the nature of the relationship.

## The Conflict of Interest in Plain Language

Here is the structural reality most agency conversations avoid:

An agency paid on ad spend percentage is financially incentivized to recommend higher ad budgets. An agency paid a flat retainer has no financial incentive to reduce your spend when channels stop performing -- their revenue stays the same. An agency that controls your ad accounts, your website, and your call tracking has created switching costs that keep you in the relationship regardless of performance.

None of these arrangements make an agency malicious. They make them human. They are responding rationally to the incentives they've been given.

The consequence for you is that the relationship lacks natural accountability. In a well-structured agency relationship, the agency only gets paid when you win. In the standard structure, the agency gets paid when you spend. Those are different relationships producing different behavior.

The questions that expose the structure quickly are the ones most owners never ask:

- "Do I own my ad accounts, website, and call tracking data?"
- "What happens to my accounts and my data if I stop working with you?"
- "Can you show me cost per booked job by channel, not cost per lead?"
- "What is my booking rate on the leads your campaigns generate?"
- "What is your recommendation for reducing my spend if a channel stops performing?"

An agency that answers all five of those questions directly and confidently is a different agency from one that deflects, hedges, or changes the subject. The answers tell you more about the relationship than any monthly report.

## The Numbers That Show Up Before the Revenue Problem Does

Marketing problems in home services almost never appear in the revenue line first. They appear in the leading indicators that agencies don't typically track and don't typically report.

The sequence is predictable: campaign quality declines, lead intent drops, booking rate falls, cost per booked job rises, revenue per marketing dollar falls, revenue stagnates. By the time revenue stagnation is obvious in the P&L, the marketing problem has been compounding for three to six months.

The indicators that show the problem early:

**Booking rate on paid leads, month over month.** If your booking rate on Google Ads leads drops from 62% to 44% over three months, something in your traffic quality or your lead handling has changed. Your agency should flag this. Most don't.

**Average ticket on booked jobs by channel.** A channel generating high volume at low ticket is producing a different result than one generating lower volume at high ticket. HVAC replacement leads from Google LSAs produce different economics than HVAC repair leads from shared directories. If your agency isn't showing you average ticket by channel, they're showing you lead volume in a way that obscures what the lead volume is actually worth.

**Revenue per marketing dollar, rolling 90-day.** Total attributed revenue divided by total marketing spend over the same period. This number should be stable or improving. If it's declining, your marketing is becoming less efficient. Your agency should tell you that and explain why before you notice it in the P&L.

Tracy Paul, founder of Cornerstone Advertising and a contributor to ACHR News, frames the monitoring standard well: "Marketing reporting should remain simple and tied directly to outcomes. Contractors should closely monitor marketing opportunities alongside booked jobs. Marketing creates opportunities, the customer service team converts them, and sales generates revenue. If opportunities are strong but bookings are weak, call handling requires attention."

That's the accountability standard. Opportunities generated, jobs booked, revenue produced. Not clicks and impressions.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What should my marketing agency report every month?

At minimum: cost per booked job by channel, booking rate on leads generated, total revenue attributed to marketing by channel, and 90-day trend for each metric. Impressions, clicks, and cost per lead are inputs that show channel activity -- they don't show whether that activity produced revenue. An agency that reports only activity metrics is giving you an incomplete picture of whether marketing is working.

### How do I know if my HVAC marketing agency is actually working?

The most direct test: can you calculate cost per booked job from the data your agency provides? If you can, compare it against the 10% of average ticket threshold for each job type. If you can't because the attribution doesn't exist, your agency isn't giving you what you need to answer that question. Secondary test: do your ad accounts, website, and call tracking sit in your name with admin access? If not, you can't independently verify the numbers they report.

### What is a red flag with a marketing agency?

The clearest single red flag for home service contractors: the agency can't tell you your cost per booked job. That number requires connecting their ad data to your booking data -- a straightforward integration that any agency serious about accountability sets up in the first 30 days. Agencies that report cost per lead without cost per booked job are either missing the measurement infrastructure or deliberately reporting the metric that makes their work look better than it is. Other significant flags: your ad accounts are in the agency's name rather than yours, guaranteed rankings or lead counts without discussing booking rate or close rate, and resistance to showing you raw platform data.

### Should I fire my marketing agency?

Not without running the numbers first. The question isn't whether your agency is "good" in the abstract -- it's whether your current cost per booked job by channel is producing revenue at or below your threshold (8 to 12% of average ticket per job type). An agency producing booked jobs at $180 on a $1,800 water heater job is working. An agency producing booked jobs at $740 on a $380 service call is not, regardless of what the impressions report says. Run the cost per booked job math by channel before making any change.

### Why does my agency only report cost per lead and not cost per booked job?

Three reasons, in order of frequency. First, the measurement infrastructure to connect ad data to booked jobs isn't set up -- call tracking isn't flowing into the CRM or field service platform, so the agency genuinely can't calculate the number without your help. Second, the agency reports what the standard industry report template produces, which is activity metrics rather than revenue metrics, because that's what most clients accept. Third, the cost per booked job would reveal underperformance that cost per lead obscures. The fix for the first two is technical and takes a few days. The fix for the third is a different agency or a different relationship structure.
