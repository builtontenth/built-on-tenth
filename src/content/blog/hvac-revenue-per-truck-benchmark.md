Most HVAC owners know their total revenue. Few know their revenue per truck.

That gap matters more than most owners realize. Total revenue tells you how big your business is. Revenue per truck tells you how efficiently it's running -- and whether adding the next truck will actually grow revenue or just spread the same inefficiency across a larger fleet.

Here's the spread in the industry: the top 10% of HVAC service companies generate **$180,000 to $280,000 in annual revenue per technician.** The bottom 25% generate **$80,000 to $120,000** from the same technician working the same 40 to 50 hours per week, according to operational data across hundreds of HVAC companies.

Same labor market. Same customer base. Same equipment brands. A 2 to 3x difference in output.

The gap isn't about working harder. It's about average ticket, jobs per day, what happens on every call, and whether trucks are rolling toward the right jobs in the right order. Most of this is within the owner's control. Most owners have never looked at it by truck.

---

## The Number and How to Calculate It

**Revenue per technician (annual):**
Total revenue for the year divided by number of field technicians. This is your blended company number.

**Revenue per technician (monthly):**
Total invoiced revenue for the month divided by field tech headcount. Track this monthly -- it shows seasonal patterns and whether operational changes are actually moving the needle.

**Revenue per technician by individual:**
Pull invoiced jobs by assigned technician for the last 90 days. Divide by working days. Multiply by 250 for an annual run rate. This is the number that tells you who is performing and who isn't -- and whether the gap is a training issue, a dispatch issue, or a pricing issue.

Most field service platforms -- ServiceTitan, Housecall Pro, FieldEdge -- generate this report natively. If yours doesn't, pull job revenue by assigned tech from your invoicing system. It takes 20 minutes the first time.

**The wage-to-revenue ratio check:**

A useful parallel calculation: what percentage of revenue is each technician generating relative to their compensation? The target in healthy HVAC operations is **14 to 20% of revenue going to technician wages.** A tech earning $52,000 per year should be generating roughly $260,000 to $370,000 in annual revenue. If they're generating $140,000 at $52,000 in wages, you're paying 37 cents of wages for every dollar of output -- more than double your target.

This ratio, run by individual technician, surfaces overpaying relative to output before the annual P&L shows it as a margin problem.

## What Driving Actually Costs You

Before getting into the performance drivers, one number frames everything: **the cost to roll a truck.**

According to ACCA's 2025 financial analysis, when you factor in wages, benefits, vehicle expenses, insurance, and non-billable windshield time, the average cost to roll an HVAC truck is **$84.40 per hour.** That's before a single dollar of profit.

At $84.40/hour, a 30-minute service call that you charge $89 for produces $4.60 per hour in margin. Not per job. Per hour. For the call duration only -- not accounting for the drive there and back.

The implication: HVAC trucks need to be running high-value work to cover their operating cost and generate meaningful margin. Service calls priced at emergency diagnostic minimums with nothing added aren't building a business -- they're treading water at $84.40/hour in the background.

The same ACCA analysis found that parts runs -- a technician leaving a job to pick up a part -- cost roughly **one hour of billable time per run.** At $84.40/hour in truck cost plus the lost billable revenue from the job sitting idle, a single parts run costs $150 to $300 depending on the job. On a company running 8 trucks with one parts run per truck every three days, that's **$9,600 to $19,200 per month** in parts-run losses before accounting for customer dissatisfaction from extended waits.

These are operational costs that don't show up as line items. They show up as revenue per truck being 30 to 40% lower than it should be.

## The Four Variables That Drive Revenue Per Truck

### Variable 1: Average Ticket

A truck running 4 jobs per day at a $220 average ticket generates $880 in daily revenue. The same truck at $380 average generates $1,520. Same hours. Same fuel. $163,200 annual difference per technician.

Average ticket in HVAC is driven by three things: job mix (replacement and installation calls produce 5 to 10x more revenue than tune-ups), flat-rate pricing discipline, and upsell behavior on every call.

On job mix: a tech running 4 tune-ups per day at $150 each generates $600. A tech running 3 service calls and one replacement estimate per day averages well over $1,000 in daily revenue -- even before the replacement closes. Companies that route replacement-leaning calls to their strongest closers and tune-up calls to newer techs are managing job mix deliberately. Companies that randomize dispatch are mixing high-value and low-value calls without tracking the revenue difference by technician.

On flat-rate pricing: techs who quote from memory introduce inconsistency that almost always skews low. An owner who reviewed technician quotes last year found the same job being quoted at $340, $290, and $410 depending on who ran the call. The ones quoting low weren't worse technicians -- they were undertrained on pricing. A price book, used consistently, removes the variable and brings every technician toward your intended margin.

On upsell behavior: a tech who replaces a capacitor and leaves has generated the minimum from that truck roll. A tech who replaces the capacitor, inspects the refrigerant charge, checks the contactor, and presents findings honestly when something is marginal generates significantly more per call without dishonesty or pressure. The system check is a service. Most homeowners whose technician never mentions the condition of adjacent components don't know what they're missing.

**The diagnostic:** Pull average ticket by technician for the last 90 days. A spread of more than $80 between your highest and lowest producer is almost always a pricing or job mix problem, not a work ethic problem.

### Variable 2: Jobs Per Day

Top-performing HVAC technicians complete **3 to 5 jobs per day** on service and repair work, according to ServiceTitan's industry benchmarks. Companies averaging fewer than 3 jobs per tech per day have a scheduling, routing, or job-type problem.

Technicians spend **28% of their workday driving**, according to the same ServiceTitan dataset. On a 9-hour day, that's 2.5 hours of non-billable windshield time. In a tightly dispatched urban operation with clustered routing, that drops to 18 to 20%. In a sprawling service area with ad-hoc dispatch, it runs 32 to 35%.

The math on improving dispatch routing from 28% to 22% drive time on a 6-tech operation running 9-hour days:

- Recovered time per tech per day: 0.54 hours
- At $84.40/hour truck cost and one additional job opportunity per recovered hour: $272 per tech per day
- Annually: $272 x 250 days x 6 techs = **$408,000 in recoverable capacity** -- without hiring anyone, without changing your service area, without a single additional marketing dollar

ServiceTitan's benchmarks for a 50-tech operation estimate $2.38M annually in windshield time. Scaled to a 6-tech shop, that's $285,000. Better dispatch routing is one of the highest-ROI operational investments available to an HVAC company at any revenue level.

**The diagnostic:** How many jobs per tech per day did you average last month? If it's below 3 on service and repair work, pull your dispatch data and map the geographic spread of each tech's daily route. The ones running fewest jobs per day are almost always covering the most ground.

### Variable 3: First-Call Resolution Rate

First-call resolution -- the percentage of jobs completely resolved on the initial visit -- directly drives revenue per truck in two ways: it protects against non-billable callback visits that consume truck capacity, and it reflects tech competence that translates to higher average ticket and customer trust.

The industry target is **first-call resolution of 80% or above**, with a callback rate at or below **2.75%**, according to FieldEdge's operational benchmarks. Companies below 75% first-call resolution are filling tech calendars with non-revenue-generating repeat visits.

The primary driver of low first-call resolution is parts availability. A tech who arrives at an emergency call without a capacitor or contactor on the truck makes two trips: one to diagnose, one to fix. Both trips carry the $84.40/hour truck cost. Only the repair trip generates revenue. The diagnosis call was a cost center.

Truck stock optimization -- standardizing which parts every truck carries in what quantities, based on your most frequent repair calls by system type -- is the operational fix. It requires analyzing 90 days of parts usage data by job type, which most field service platforms can generate. Companies that go through this process typically reduce parts runs by 60 to 70% within 60 days of restocking to the optimized list.

**The diagnostic:** What percentage of your service calls last month were resolved on the first visit? If you don't know this number, your CRM has it -- pull callbacks that were assigned to the same address within 14 days of the original call and check what percentage of jobs they represent.

### Variable 4: Service Agreement Revenue Per Tech

Technicians who sell service agreements on every eligible call generate meaningfully more revenue per year than those who don't -- not just from the agreement fees, but from the downstream jobs those agreement customers produce.

The mechanism: a tech who converts one caller per week to a service agreement at $185/year is adding $9,620 in annual recurring revenue from that single habit. That's before accounting for the agreement customer's higher average annual spend, which runs 2 to 3x a one-time service caller for most HVAC companies.

The [HVAC service agreements breakdown](/insights/how-hvac-service-agreements-add-200k-annual-revenue) covers the three moments to sell and the exact scripts. The per-truck implication: a technician selling 1.5 agreements per week vs. a technician selling 0.3 agreements per week produces a revenue per truck difference that compounds annually. Track agreement sales by technician alongside revenue -- they're related metrics.

## The Spread Between Your Best and Worst Technician

Every HVAC company with three or more technicians has a performance spread. Understanding what's driving it determines whether the fix is training, dispatch, pricing, or staffing.

**A normal spread:** 20 to 30% revenue difference between your top and bottom producer. Largely explained by experience level and job mix assignment. Closable with pricing training and better job routing.

**A problem spread:** 40 to 60% revenue difference. This indicates a systems failure -- either your dispatch is routing better jobs to certain techs, your pricing varies by who quotes the job, or one technician has a specific behavior gap that hasn't been identified.

**How to read the data:**

Pull the last 90 days of revenue by technician. For each tech, calculate: total revenue, days worked, average daily revenue, jobs per day, and average ticket.

Low average ticket with normal job count: pricing gap or job mix skew. Check whether they're using the price book and whether their callbacks are being assigned back to them.

Low job count with normal average ticket: dispatch and routing problem. Map their daily routes and compare drive time against higher-performing techs.

Low job count and low average ticket: this technician needs direct observation and coaching. Listen to recorded dispatches, ride-along on calls, and review their last 10 work orders for patterns.

Most performance gaps have a specific cause. Finding it takes 30 minutes with the right data. Not finding it costs $40,000 to $80,000 per year per underperforming technician in foregone revenue.

## The Hiring Decision: When Revenue Per Truck Tells You Yes or No

The most expensive mistake in HVAC growth is adding a truck to solve a revenue problem that's actually a productivity problem.

If your 6-truck fleet is averaging $130,000 per truck and you add a seventh, you now have 7 trucks averaging $130,000. You've added a truck payment, insurance, tools, and a technician salary -- and generated the same output per head you had before. Total revenue increases but margin per truck stays flat or declines if the new tech underperforms the average.

**The hiring signal you actually want:**

Your existing trucks are running at or above 72% billable utilization consistently, AND you're turning down or significantly delaying calls because you don't have available field capacity, AND your revenue per truck is above $200,000 annually.

If all three are true, a new truck generates close to its full revenue potential from month one. If any of those three are broken, adding headcount compounds the problem.

**The two-week test before committing to a hire:**

Track billable hours per technician for the next two weeks. If your top three techs are running at 75% utilization or above and still declining calls, you need capacity. If they're running at 55 to 60% utilization and declining calls, you have a scheduling or dispatch problem, not a headcount problem.

The two scenarios produce materially different ROI numbers -- and the gap is usually what changes an owner's mind about whether to hire or to fix.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much revenue should an HVAC technician generate per year?

Top-performing HVAC companies generate $180,000 to $280,000 per technician annually. Industry average runs $120,000 to $180,000. The bottom quarter generates $80,000 to $120,000. Your target depends on your service mix -- replacement-focused operations with higher average tickets can support higher revenue per tech than pure service-and-repair shops. The benchmark check is the wage-to-revenue ratio: technician wages should represent 14 to 20% of the revenue they generate.

### What is the wage-to-revenue ratio for HVAC technicians?

A healthy HVAC operation targets technician wages at 14 to 20% of the revenue that technician generates. A tech earning $52,000 per year should be generating $260,000 to $370,000 in annual revenue. If wages exceed 25% of revenue generated, the technician is either priced wrong, under-performing, or running too low a job mix. Calculate this ratio for each technician individually -- the team average hides individual misalignments.

### How many jobs per day should an HVAC technician complete?

Three to five jobs per day on service and repair work is the top-performing range per ServiceTitan's benchmarks. Fewer than 3 jobs per day on standard service calls indicates a dispatch, routing, or job-type problem. Higher-ticket installation days may produce fewer jobs at significantly higher revenue -- judge technicians in installation mode by revenue per day, not job count per day.

### What does it actually cost to roll an HVAC truck?

According to ACCA's 2025 financial analysis, the average fully-loaded cost to roll an HVAC truck -- including wages, benefits, vehicle, insurance, and non-billable drive time -- is approximately $84.40 per hour. That's the floor your billing rate and job volume need to clear before generating any margin. It's why a $89 diagnostic on a 30-minute call barely breaks even, and why parts runs that cost one billable hour apiece are a significant hidden drain on profitability.

### How do I improve average ticket per technician?

Three approaches that work: implement flat-rate pricing consistently so all techs quote the same job at the same price regardless of who runs the call, train techs to perform a system check on every call and document findings (not push upgrades -- document findings), and route higher-ticket call types to your higher-average-ticket technicians until the lower-ticket techs develop the same habits. Most average ticket gaps between technicians are a pricing consistency and call protocol issue, not a competence issue.

### When should I add another HVAC truck?

When your existing trucks are running at or above 72% billable utilization consistently, AND you're actively declining or significantly delaying calls, AND your revenue per truck is above $200,000 annually. If any of those three conditions isn't met, adding a truck generates new overhead without proportional revenue. Fix revenue per truck first -- a company moving from $130,000 to $200,000 per truck on its existing fleet generates more net revenue than adding a new truck at $130,000.

### How do I find which technician is dragging down my revenue per truck average?

Pull 90 days of invoiced revenue by assigned technician from your field service platform. Calculate average daily revenue, jobs per day, and average ticket for each technician. The technician with low average ticket and normal job count has a pricing gap. The one with low job count and normal average ticket has a routing or scheduling problem. The one with both low metrics needs direct observation -- listen to dispatches, review work orders, and ride along on two calls. Most gaps have a specific, identifiable cause.

### Does revenue per truck include the owner if they're running calls?

Only if you want an accurate picture of your business's scalable capacity. Owner-run calls generate revenue that disappears the moment you stop running calls. For capacity planning and hiring decisions, calculate revenue per truck using only your employed technicians. Owner revenue can be tracked separately but shouldn't be used to inflate the team benchmark -- it creates a false picture of what your fleet produces without you in it.
