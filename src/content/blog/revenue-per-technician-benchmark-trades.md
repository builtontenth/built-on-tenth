There are two HVAC companies in the same mid-size metro. Both run 8 field technicians. Both focus on residential service and replacement. Both are spending roughly 8% of revenue on marketing.

Company A does $1.4M annually. Company B does $2.6M.

They are not in different markets. They are not running different job types. The difference is almost entirely in what happens between when the truck leaves the shop and when the invoice is collected. Average ticket. Job mix. Pricing discipline. Dispatch efficiency. How often the tech is running calls versus sitting idle.

Revenue per technician is the metric that shows that difference in a single number. It is also the metric most owners don't track -- because tracking total revenue feels like the same thing, and it isn't.

This article covers what revenue per technician means across HVAC, roofing, and plumbing, what the benchmarks actually look like in each trade, and the four variables that explain nearly every gap between a company's current number and where it should be.

---

## Why Total Revenue Is the Wrong Scorecard

When a trades business grows from $1.5M to $2.1M while adding two technicians, the owner sees growth. The P&L shows it. The bank account shows it. Revenue went up.

But if those two new technicians produced $150,000 each -- and your existing team was producing $250,000 each -- you didn't grow the business. You diluted it. You added headcount, overhead, insurance, vehicle costs, and management load for $300,000 in new revenue that should have been $500,000 if the new people matched the productivity of the people already there.

Revenue per technician catches this. Total revenue doesn't.

The same logic applies in reverse. A company holding headcount flat while implementing better dispatch, pricing discipline, and service agreement programs can grow from $1.8M to $2.4M with the same team. That's a 33% revenue increase with zero incremental labor cost. Revenue per tech moved from $180,000 to $240,000. Margin expanded. Capacity wasn't the constraint -- efficiency was.

This is what FieldEdge's 2026 industry outlook means when it states: "True scaling in 2026 means increasing revenue per technician while protecting margin, culture, and customer experience." Not adding headcount. Increasing output per head first.

## The Benchmarks by Trade

These ranges reflect the spread between top-quartile and bottom-quartile operations across residential home services. They are not aspirational targets -- they are the observed range of what companies in similar markets, running similar services, actually produce per technician annually.

### HVAC

**Bottom quartile:** $80,000 to $120,000 per technician per year
**Industry average:** $150,000 to $180,000
**Top quartile:** $180,000 to $280,000

The top-quartile number comes from operational data across hundreds of HVAC companies, documented consistently in trade industry sources including ACCA's financial benchmarks. The spread -- $80K to $280K -- is not explained by market size, market competitiveness, or which equipment brands the company carries. It is explained by what happens on the call.

A tech running 4 tune-ups per day at $150 each generates $600 in daily revenue. A tech running 2 service calls and converting one into a system replacement at $8,500 generates $10,000+ in a single day. Job mix is the single largest driver of the HVAC revenue per tech gap. The [HVAC revenue per truck article](/insights/hvac-revenue-per-truck-benchmark) covers the full mechanics of what moves this number for HVAC companies specifically.

### Roofing

**Bottom quartile:** $120,000 to $180,000 per crew member per year
**Industry average:** $230,000 to $300,000 per installer
**Top quartile:** $340,000+ per production employee

The NRCA benchmark, documented through workshop data published in Professional Roofing, finds that roofing business owners calculating their revenue per installer number for the first time typically discover each installer is responsible for $230,000 to $300,000 per year. This is the industry midpoint, not the ceiling.

The same source makes a striking observation: contractors estimate that only 10% of untrained installers are fully effective -- reducing their revenue contribution to roughly $23,000 per year. That means an untrained installer is producing roughly one-tenth the revenue of a trained one. "For every year an installer's talent goes undeveloped, your company could be losing $207,000 of potential income ($796 per day)."

Roofing's revenue per crew member calculation differs from the service trade calculation in one important way: roofing includes significant materials cost embedded in each job. A $14,000 replacement generates $5,000 to $6,000 in materials cost alone. The revenue per installer figure reflects gross revenue, not gross profit -- which is why the benchmark looks higher than HVAC or plumbing even though net margins in roofing are often lower. When comparing across trades, use a consistent definition (gross revenue per field employee) to avoid misleading comparisons.

ProLine's analysis of roofing revenue benchmarks sets the revenue-per-employee target at $340,000 for efficiently run operations. Below this, the company is either underperforming on pricing, close rate, or job mix -- or is overstaffed relative to its current volume.

### Plumbing

**Bottom quartile:** $100,000 to $150,000 per technician per year
**Industry average:** $180,000 to $240,000
**Top quartile:** $280,000 to $380,000

Plumbing revenue per tech is heavily influenced by job mix -- perhaps more than any other trade. A plumber running primarily emergency service calls at $380 average ticket and 4 jobs per day generates roughly $380,000 in annual revenue at 250 working days. A plumber running emergency service plus water heater replacement, repipe, and remodel work at $1,200 to $6,500 average tickets can push well above that on the same hours.

The bottom-quartile plumber is typically running high-frequency, low-ticket service calls without upselling to maintenance plans, higher-ticket repairs, or replacement work. The top-quartile plumber is running a mix of service calls, installation work, and recurring service agreement customers who represent predictable scheduled revenue.

Brentwood Growth's operational data, referenced in the [plumbing revenue per tech article](/insights/revenue-per-technician-plumbing-business-scaling), supports the $200,000 to $300,000 benchmark for plumbing companies running an efficient mix of service and installation work.

## How to Calculate Your Number

The calculation is straightforward. Most owners who haven't done it explicitly are surprised by the result.

**Company level:**
Total invoiced revenue for the last 12 months divided by total number of field technicians (not including office staff, management, or apprentices who aren't running their own calls).

**Individual level:**
Pull invoiced jobs by assigned technician for the last 90 days. Divide total invoiced revenue by working days. Multiply by 250 for an annual run rate.

That individual calculation is where the actionable information lives. Most field service platforms -- ServiceTitan, Housecall Pro, FieldEdge, Jobber -- generate this report natively. If yours doesn't, pull revenue by assigned technician from your invoicing system.

When you have both numbers -- company average and individual by technician -- two things typically become visible immediately:

**The spread.** In most companies, the top-producing technician generates 2 to 3x the revenue of the bottom-producing one. They are working the same hours, running the same types of calls, in the same market. The output difference is not explained by luck or territory -- it's explained by pricing, average ticket, and how they handle the estimate conversation on the call.

**The pattern.** When you break the individual numbers down further -- revenue per job and jobs per day, not just total revenue -- the gap's source usually becomes clear. High revenue/low job count means high average ticket (good). Low revenue/high job count means low average ticket (pricing or job mix problem). Low revenue/low job count means dispatch, routing, or parts availability is throttling the tech's capacity. Each pattern has a specific fix.

## The Four Variables Behind Every Gap

Revenue per technician is produced by exactly four inputs. Improving any one of them moves the number. Understanding which one is driving your gap tells you where to focus.

### Variable 1: Jobs per day (utilization)

How many completed, invoiced jobs does each technician finish on an average working day?

Industry averages for completed jobs per day run 3 to 5 in service-focused HVAC and plumbing, lower in roofing where single jobs are full-day commitments. Below 3 completed jobs per day in a service trade almost always indicates dispatch or routing inefficiency: too much windshield time between calls, parts runs pulling the tech off productive work, or call scheduling that's creating morning-heavy loads with empty afternoon slots.

The ACCA 2025 financial analysis found that parts runs -- a tech leaving a job to pick up a needed part -- cost roughly one full hour of billable time per run. At an average truck cost of $84.40 per billable hour, plus the revenue missed from the job sitting idle, a single parts run costs $150 to $300. On a company running 6 trucks with one parts run per truck every two days, that's $3,000 to $5,400 per month in parts-run losses before counting the revenue impact.

### Variable 2: Average ticket

What does the average completed job invoice for?

This is the most directly controllable variable and the most underoptimized in most operations. Pricing inconsistency -- different techs quoting the same job at different prices -- is the most common cause of average ticket being lower than it should be. A tech who quotes a capacitor replacement at $240 and another who quotes $385 for the same job are not pricing based on different cost realities. They're pricing based on different confidence levels and different training.

Flat-rate pricing removes the variable. Good / Better / Best option presentation on replacement and installation work increases average ticket by presenting customers with a tier choice rather than a yes/no decision. Service agreement attachment at the end of service calls converts a one-time interaction into a recurring revenue relationship and increases the annual revenue attributed to that customer.

### Variable 3: Job mix

What percentage of calls are high-ticket (replacement, installation, repipe, roof replacement) versus low-ticket (tune-up, minor repair, emergency patch, inspection)?

Job mix is the multiplier on everything else. A tech completing 4 jobs per day at $200 average generates $800 daily. The same tech completing 3 jobs per day at $1,200 average generates $3,600. Deliberately routing high-value calls to your best closing techs while routing tune-ups and inspections to newer techs or during their ramp-up period is one of the highest-ROI dispatch decisions available.

In HVAC, the conversion from a service call to a system replacement recommendation -- and whether that recommendation is made confidently and professionally -- is often the single biggest driver of the revenue-per-tech spread between top and bottom performers. In plumbing, the equivalent is the conversion from an emergency repair call to a water heater inspection, or from a service call to a service agreement discussion.

### Variable 4: Revenue-to-compensation ratio

Is each technician generating enough revenue to justify their total cost?

The target in healthy home service operations is 14 to 20% of revenue going to technician wages. A tech earning $55,000 per year should be generating $275,000 to $390,000 in annual revenue. At $140,000 in annual revenue, that tech is costing you 39 cents in wages per dollar of output -- more than double the target.

This ratio, run by individual technician quarterly, shows you which members of your team have compensation that has outpaced their productivity, and where coaching or job mix changes would bring the ratio back into range. It surfaces overpaying relative to output before the annual P&L shows it as a margin problem.

## The Headcount Trap

The most common mistake in trades business scaling is treating a revenue gap as a headcount problem.

A company doing $1.8M with 8 technicians is producing $225,000 per tech. The owner wants to reach $2.5M. The instinct is to hire two more technicians.

If hiring those two technicians brings total revenue to $2.1M -- because the new techs produce $150,000 each while the existing team's per-tech output stays flat -- the company went from 8 people producing $225,000 each to 10 people producing $210,000 each. Revenue grew but efficiency fell. Overhead went up. Margin compressed.

The alternative sequence: improve the four variables above with the existing team first. What would revenue per tech look like at $250,000 company average instead of $225,000? With 8 existing technicians, that's $2M in revenue from the same headcount. With $270,000 average: $2.16M. With $290,000 average: $2.32M.

When those productivity improvements are in place and the techs are running at 72%+ billable utilization and still turning down calls, that's when adding a technician makes sense. At that point, the new tech enters an operation with working systems and is likely to produce at or near the company average from month one. Adding headcount to a low-utilization, low-average-ticket operation adds the cost without adding the systems that would make the cost worth it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is a good revenue per technician for HVAC?

Top-quartile HVAC companies generate $180,000 to $280,000 per technician annually. Industry average runs $150,000 to $180,000. Below $120,000 per technician indicates significant operational gaps -- typically in pricing consistency, job mix, or utilization -- that are worth diagnosing before adding headcount. The range within a single market can be wide: two HVAC companies with the same technician count in the same city routinely show 2 to 3x revenue per tech differences driven entirely by internal operations.

### What is a good revenue per technician for plumbing?

Top-quartile plumbing operations generate $280,000 to $380,000 per technician annually. Industry average is $180,000 to $240,000. Plumbing has the widest ticket variance of the three trades -- emergency service at $280 to $450 and repipe work at $5,000 to $8,000 -- so job mix drives a larger percentage of the revenue per tech gap in plumbing than in HVAC or roofing.

### What is a good revenue per crew member for roofing?

NRCA workshop data shows that most roofing business owners find their installers generating $230,000 to $300,000 in revenue per year when they calculate it for the first time. Top-performing operations push toward $340,000 per production employee. Note that roofing figures are gross revenue, not gross profit -- material costs are 35 to 50% of roofing revenue, so the net margin picture is different from HVAC or plumbing even at the same revenue per crew number.

### How do I calculate revenue per technician?

Divide total invoiced revenue for the last 12 months by your number of field technicians. For individual-level calculation, pull invoiced revenue attributed to each technician for the last 90 days, divide by the number of working days in that period, and multiply by 250 for an annual run rate. Most field service platforms including ServiceTitan, Housecall Pro, and FieldEdge generate this report by technician natively.

### Why is my revenue per technician low?

Four causes account for the majority of cases: low jobs per day (utilization or dispatch problem), low average ticket (pricing inconsistency or job mix skewed toward low-ticket work), high headcount relative to current demand (the operation is overstaffed for its call volume), or high new-technician percentage (newer techs producing below company average while ramping up). The individual tech analysis -- revenue per job and jobs per day, not just total revenue -- usually identifies which cause is primary within 20 minutes of pulling the data.

### Is it better to improve revenue per tech or hire more technicians?

Improve revenue per tech first, unless your existing technicians are consistently running at 72%+ billable utilization and still declining or delaying calls due to capacity constraints. Adding a technician to an operation where existing techs are running below that threshold adds cost without addressing the underlying efficiency gap. When utilization is genuinely constrained and the operation is running well, a new technician enters a working system and can produce near the company average from month one -- making the hire immediately accretive rather than dilutive.

### Does revenue per technician work the same way across HVAC, plumbing, and roofing?

The calculation is the same but the interpretation differs by trade. Roofing revenue per crew member is a gross revenue figure that includes significant material cost, so it should not be compared directly to HVAC or plumbing figures without adjusting for that difference. Plumbing has a wider ticket range than HVAC, making job mix a more powerful lever in plumbing than in HVAC. Roofing is more seasonally concentrated, meaning revenue per tech in any given month can be misleading -- use annual figures or at least 6-month rolling averages to assess roofing productivity accurately.
