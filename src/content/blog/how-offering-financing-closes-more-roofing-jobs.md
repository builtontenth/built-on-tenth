The average roofing company closes **27% of its estimates.**

That means 73 out of every 100 homeowners who let you on their roof, listened to your scope, and sat through your price presentation said no -- or said nothing and never called back.

Most roofing owners assume those losses are price-related. The competitor came in lower. The homeowner is shopping around. The estimate was too high for the market.

Sometimes that's true. But in a significant number of those cases, the homeowner wanted the roof. They needed the roof. They just didn't have $15,000 sitting in their checking account, and nobody offered them a way to say yes without writing that check.

That's the financing gap. And it's worth more than most roofing companies realize.

Contractors who offer financing at the point of estimate close **30 to 50% more jobs** than those who don't, according to data from the Consumer Federation of America and industry benchmarks from home improvement lending platforms. On a company running 80 estimates per month at a $16,000 average ticket, moving from a 27% close rate to a 38% close rate is **$140,800 in additional monthly revenue** from the same estimate volume.

This article covers how roofing contractor financing actually works, what it costs you in dealer fees, how to run the math to see if those fees are worth it, and how to present financing without it feeling like a desperation move.

## Why the $15,000 Check Is a Bigger Problem Than Your Price

Most roofing objections that sound like price objections are actually liquidity objections.

The homeowner isn't saying your price is too high relative to what the work is worth. They're saying they don't have access to $15,000 right now without selling something, pulling from savings, or taking out a loan they'd have to arrange themselves.

The distinction matters because the solutions are different. If it's a price objection, you're adjusting scope or margins. If it's a liquidity objection, you're creating a payment structure that makes the same price accessible.

Construction material prices were up 42.4% above pre-pandemic levels as of December 2025, according to NRCA data. Iron and steel prices -- core roofing components -- jumped 12.3% year-over-year. Roofing replacements that cost $10,000 three years ago routinely run $14,000 to $18,000 now. Homeowners' savings haven't kept pace with that increase. The liquidity gap is wider than it was in 2020 or 2021, which means financing is doing more work in 2026 than it did before.

When you offer financing, you're not lowering your price. You're changing the question from "do you have $16,000?" to "can you handle $189 per month?" Those are two different questions, and most homeowners can answer yes to the second one when they can't answer yes to the first.

## How Contractor Financing Actually Works

Most roofing contractors who don't offer financing assume it requires becoming a lender or carrying financial risk. Neither is true.

Here's the actual structure:

You partner with a third-party lender -- GreenSky, Hearth, Service Finance, Synchrony, or Goodleap are the most common for roofing. You apply as a dealer, which typically takes one to five business days and requires your business license, insurance certificate, and basic company information.

When a homeowner wants to finance, they apply through the lender's portal or mobile app -- usually on a phone or tablet at the kitchen table during your estimate presentation. Approval decisions come back in minutes. Once approved, the homeowner signs a loan agreement with the lender, not with you.

You complete the job. The lender pays you directly, in full, typically within two to five business days of job completion. The homeowner then makes monthly payments to the lender.

**You carry zero financial risk on the loan.** If the homeowner stops making payments six months later, that's the lender's problem. Your payment was already in your account. The only cost to you is the dealer fee, which is deducted from the loan amount at funding.

## What Dealer Fees Actually Cost You

The dealer fee is the cost of offering financing. It's a percentage of the financed amount that the lender deducts before paying you.

Roofing contractors typically pay dealer fees of 3 to 15% depending on the financing terms offered. Standard rate loans carry lower dealer fees of 3 to 6%, while 0% promotional financing carries higher fees of 8 to 15%. The 0% option costs you more because the lender is absorbing the interest that would normally be collected over the loan term.

Here's what that looks like on a $16,000 roof:

| Financing Type | Dealer Fee | Fee Amount | You Receive |
|---|---|---|---|
| Standard rate loan (12.99% APR) | 4% | $640 | $15,360 |
| 18-month 0% promo | 9% | $1,440 | $14,560 |
| 12-month same-as-cash | 7% | $1,120 | $14,880 |

On the standard rate loan, you net $15,360 instead of $16,000. That's a $640 cost to close a job you otherwise wouldn't have closed. Against a $16,000 ticket with typical roofing margins of 35 to 45%, you're giving up roughly 4% of revenue to recover a job that would have been $0.

The math only breaks down if the homeowner was going to write the full check anyway and you're offering financing unnecessarily. That's the one scenario where dealer fees cost you without returning anything.

**How to handle dealer fees in pricing:**

There are two approaches. The first is to build the typical dealer fee into your estimate price -- add 4 to 6% to your standard price and offer financing as a standard option at no additional cost to the homeowner. The second is to quote your standard price and note that financing is available, absorbing the fee on financed jobs.

The first approach is cleaner operationally because you don't have to track which jobs were financed at a margin reduction. The second approach is more transparent. Both are used by working roofing companies. The wrong approach is to offer financing without accounting for dealer fees at all -- that's where margins get quietly compressed job by job without the owner noticing.

## The Platform Comparison

Not all lenders serve roofing contractors the same way. Here's how the main options compare:

**GreenSky**

The most widely used financing platform in home improvement. Currently owned by Goldman Sachs, GreenSky offers fixed-rate installment and promotional loans up to $100,000 and approves homeowners based on creditworthiness, not just job amount -- meaning if an install is worth $10,000, a homeowner might get approved for $15,000, giving the contractor credit to upsell into. Dealer fees range from 0% to 26.6% depending on the loan. Integrates with ServiceTitan and Leap. Approval decisions in under a minute in most cases. The most common complaint among roofing contractors is inconsistent customer service and occasional account closures without notice -- worth knowing if you plan to rely on it as your primary option.

**Hearth**

Hearth is a technology platform that connects customers to multiple lenders through one application, with no per-project dealer fees -- instead charging a monthly or annual subscription. The multi-lender model means higher approval rates because customers see options from several lenders simultaneously rather than a single yes/no. Good for contractors who want to maximize the percentage of customers who get approved. Less useful if you want predictable per-job fee math.

**Service Finance**

Strong for 0% promotional financing programs. Service Finance is considered best-in-class for same-as-cash deals. Promotional 0% offers are effective sales tools, particularly for retail replacement customers who are price-sensitive and motivated by the "no interest if paid in full" framing. Higher dealer fees on promo products, lower on standard rate loans.

**Synchrony**

Major consumer lender with strong brand recognition -- homeowners may already have Synchrony accounts from home improvement retailers. Dealer fees range from 0.99% to 15% depending on the loan. Better suited for larger projects and customers with strong credit profiles. Integration with some field service platforms.

**The practical recommendation:**

Start with one platform -- GreenSky or Hearth are the most common entry points for roofing -- and get comfortable with the application process before adding a second. Once your team is fluent in presenting financing and running applications, adding a second lender as a backup improves approval rates when a homeowner doesn't qualify with the primary.

## The Close Rate Math: Is Financing Worth It?

Run these numbers on a company doing 80 estimates per month:

**Without financing:**

- 80 estimates x 27% close rate = 21.6 booked jobs
- 21.6 x $16,000 average ticket = $345,600/month
- Annual revenue from estimates: $4,147,200

**With financing (conservative: 35% close rate, 40% of jobs financed at 5% average dealer fee):**

- 80 estimates x 35% close rate = 28 booked jobs
- 6.4 additional jobs vs. without financing (the gap)
- 40% of 28 jobs = 11.2 financed at $16,000
- Dealer fees: 11.2 x $16,000 x 5% = $8,960/month
- Revenue increase from additional jobs: 6.4 x $16,000 = $102,400
- Net monthly gain after dealer fees: **$93,440**
- Annual net gain: **$1,121,280**

The dealer fees cost $8,960 per month. The close rate improvement generates $102,400 per month in additional revenue. The ROI on offering financing is not close.

Even in a conservative scenario where financing only adds 5 percentage points to close rate instead of 8, and dealer fees run 7% instead of 5%, the math still produces $40,000 to $60,000 per month in net additional revenue on an 80-estimate operation.

The dealer fee is not a cost you're trying to minimize. It's a margin you're paying to unlock a job that otherwise doesn't happen.

That same logic shows up in your channel math too. A lead source that looks expensive at the top of funnel often becomes completely workable once financing lifts close rate at the estimate stage. ## How to Present Financing Without It Feeling Like a Pressure Tactic

Most roofing contractors who offer financing present it wrong. They get to the end of the estimate, the homeowner hesitates on the price, and the salesperson pivots to: "We do have financing available if that helps." Said as an afterthought. As a rescue move. As something that signals the price was too high and the company knows it.

That framing kills the effectiveness of financing before the application even opens.

**The right approach: present financing before you present the price.**

Early in the estimate presentation, before the number is on the table:

> "One thing I'll mention -- a lot of our customers use our financing options so they don't have to come out of pocket all at once. We work with [lender], approvals take about two minutes, and you can do the whole thing on your phone. Just something to keep in mind when we go through the numbers."

This does three things. It normalizes financing -- "a lot of our customers" means it's not a sign of financial difficulty. It removes the stigma of applying by making it sound simple and routine. And it sets the expectation before the price lands, so when the homeowner hears $16,000 they're already thinking in monthly payment terms, not lump sum terms.

Then when you present the price, lead with the monthly:

> "The full replacement comes to $16,400. On our standard financing that works out to around $194 per month over 10 years, or there's a 12-month same-as-cash option if you want to pay it off faster."

You've given them the full price and the monthly option in the same sentence. The homeowner compares $16,400 to $194, not to what they have in their checking account.

**What not to do:**

Don't offer financing only to customers who flinch at the price. That makes it feel like a consolation product. Don't ask "would you like to apply for financing?" as a yes/no question -- that requires the homeowner to actively opt into something that feels like admitting they can't afford cash. Don't wait until after they've said no to bring it up. By then the decision is made.

The contractors who get the most out of financing present it to every customer as a standard option -- not as a rescue, not as a fallback, but as one of two normal ways people pay for roofing work.

If your office is still missing calls or taking too long to work storm leads, financing won't rescue the economics by itself. ## The Insurance Job Question

Insurance replacement jobs change the financing dynamic. The homeowner already has an insurance payment coming. They're not writing the full check out of pocket -- they're covering a deductible, typically $1,000 to $2,500, and the insurance company covers the rest.

Financing the deductible is a different product than financing the full job. Some lenders -- GreenSky included -- support deductible financing specifically. A homeowner who doesn't have $1,500 for their deductible is a different situation than one who doesn't have $16,000 for a full cash replacement.

On insurance jobs, the financing conversation is simpler:

> "Your out-of-pocket is your deductible -- looks like that's $1,500 on your policy. We can finance that if you'd rather not come out of pocket all at once. It's about $18 a month."

Most homeowners who've just been through the stress of a storm claim will say yes to $18 a month rather than adding a $1,500 bill to everything else they're dealing with. It also removes the last friction point between a signed contract and a started job. ## Getting Started This Week

Setting up contractor financing with a single lender takes one to five business days. The application asks for your business license, insurance certificate, and basic company information. Most roofing contractors are approved within 48 hours.

**What to do:**

1. **Choose one platform to start with.** GreenSky or Hearth are the most common entry points. Apply as a dealer this week -- the application is free and the clock doesn't start on your dealer account until you activate it.
2. **Run the close rate math on your own estimates.** Pull last month's estimates sent and jobs closed. Calculate your current close rate. Then run the scenario above with your actual ticket size and estimate volume. That number is what financing is worth to your business specifically.
3. **Write your presentation script.** Customize the language above for your market and your company name. Practice it before the next estimate so it doesn't sound new.
4. **Decide how to handle dealer fees in pricing.** Build them in upfront or absorb them on financed jobs -- either works, but make the decision deliberately before the first financed job rather than discovering the fee after the fact.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the typical dealer fee for roofing contractor financing?

Standard rate loans (fixed APR around 9 to 14%) carry dealer fees of 3 to 6% of the financed amount. Promotional financing (0% for 12 to 18 months, same-as-cash) carries higher dealer fees of 7 to 15% because the lender is absorbing the interest. On a $16,000 job, a 5% dealer fee costs you $800. On a 10% dealer fee for a 0% promo product, you give up $1,600. Whether the promo product is worth the higher fee depends on your market and how much your close rate improves when you offer 0% vs. a standard rate.

### Do I carry any risk if the homeowner stops making payments?

No. Once the lender funds you -- typically within two to five business days of job completion -- your payment is complete. The homeowner's repayment obligation is entirely with the lender. If they default, that's the lender's loss, not yours. The only risk you carry is if a job dispute results in a chargeback before funding, which is governed by your dealer agreement with the lender.

### How do I handle dealer fees in my pricing?

Two approaches work. The first is to build the typical dealer fee (4 to 6% covers most standard loan products) into your standard estimate price and offer financing as a no-additional-cost option. This is operationally simpler because margins are consistent across all jobs. The second is to quote your standard price and absorb the dealer fee on financed jobs. This is more transparent but requires tracking which jobs carry the margin reduction. Either is valid -- the wrong approach is to offer financing without accounting for dealer fees at all.

### What's the best financing platform for roofing contractors?

GreenSky is the most widely used and has the most integrations with field service software. Hearth's multi-lender model produces higher approval rates. Service Finance is strong for 0% promotional products. The right answer depends on your job mix and whether you prioritize approval rate, integration capability, or promo product strength. Start with one. Add a second only after your team is fluent with the first.

### Should I offer financing on insurance replacement jobs?

Yes, specifically for deductible financing. The homeowner's out-of-pocket on an insurance job is their deductible -- typically $1,000 to $2,500. Financing $1,500 at $18 per month removes the last cash friction between a signed contract and a started job. It's a smaller loan than a full retail replacement and most homeowners who've just been through a claim are relieved to handle the deductible over time rather than all at once.

### Does offering financing change how I present the estimate?

Yes, and the timing matters more than the wording. Present financing before you present the price -- not after the homeowner flinches. Normalize it early ("a lot of our customers use our financing") so it doesn't feel like a rescue product when the number lands. Then lead with the monthly payment alongside the full price. Homeowners who hear "$194 per month" process it differently than homeowners who hear "$16,400" and then are offered financing as an afterthought.

### What close rate should I expect after adding financing?

Industry data suggests a 30 to 50% improvement in close rate for contractors who add financing and present it correctly. That translates to roughly 8 to 15 additional percentage points on close rate -- from an industry average of 27% toward 35 to 42%. The improvement is larger in higher-cost markets where replacement tickets regularly exceed $18,000 and the liquidity gap for homeowners is wider. It's smaller in markets with lower average tickets where more homeowners can manage a cash payment. Your actual improvement depends on how consistently your team presents the option.

### Is there a minimum job size for contractor financing?

Most platforms set a minimum of $1,000 to $2,500 per financed job. For roofing, this rarely matters since even repair jobs typically exceed that threshold. For companies that also do small maintenance work, check your dealer agreement for the minimum before presenting financing on a $400 repair -- it may not qualify under your lender's terms.
