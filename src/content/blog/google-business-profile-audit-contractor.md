## How Google Decides Who Ranks: The Three Pillars

Google's local ranking algorithm evaluates every business in a search query against three factors. Understanding these is not academic -- each one maps directly to specific actions you can take.

### Relevance

Relevance is how closely your profile matches what the homeowner searched for. When someone types "HVAC repair near me," Google looks at your primary category, secondary categories, services listed, business description, and the content on the website linked to your profile. Every element either reinforces or dilutes the signal that you're the right answer for that search.

The Whitespark 2026 Local Search Ranking Factors survey -- the most comprehensive annual study of local SEO signals, now including inputs from hundreds of SEO professionals -- identifies primary category selection as the single most powerful GBP ranking factor for the local pack. More than reviews. More than photos. More than any other element you control. If your primary category is wrong or suboptimal, everything else you do is fighting an uphill battle.

For an HVAC company, the primary category should be "HVAC Contractor" or "Air Conditioning Contractor" -- not a generic version that doesn't match the high-value searches. For a roofing company, "Roofing Contractor." For plumbing, "Plumber." Then secondary categories expand eligibility: an HVAC company adding "Heating Contractor," "Air Conditioning Repair Service," and "Furnace Repair Service" as secondaries appears for a broader set of relevant searches.

One insight from the Advice Local analysis of the 2026 Whitespark data: HVAC companies should consider shifting their primary category seasonally -- "Heating Contractor" in winter, "Air Conditioning Contractor" in summer -- to match the searches that have the most demand and competition at each time of year. Most don't do this. The ones who do capture better relevance alignment during peak demand.

### Distance

Distance is the physical proximity of your business location to the person searching. This is the ranking factor you have the least control over -- you can't move closer to every searcher in your market. But you can make sure Google has accurate, unambiguous data about where you are and where you serve.

For service area businesses (which most HVAC, roofing, and plumbing companies are), your service area settings define where you're eligible to appear. Most companies either set the service area too narrowly -- cutting themselves out of adjacent zip codes they service daily -- or set it so broadly that proximity signals are diluted everywhere.

The right service area setup covers every zip code where you regularly complete work, defined specifically enough that Google can match you to searches in your true coverage zone. Pulling your last 90 days of completed jobs by zip code and comparing against your current service area settings usually reveals gaps.

### Prominence

Prominence is how established, trusted, and well-known your business appears to Google. It's the most controllable and most contested of the three factors. It's driven primarily by reviews, behavioral signals, and web authority -- and it's where most of the competitive differentiation happens.

The 2026 Whitespark survey found that review signals have grown from 16% to 20% of local pack ranking weight since 2023. Reviews are becoming more important, not less, as Google's AI increasingly uses review language to match profiles to conversational queries. A plumber with 80 reviews mentioning "emergency repairs," "burst pipes," and "fast response" matches conversational queries better than one with 10 reviews that say "great service." The language in your reviews matters, not just the count.

## The 35-Factor Audit: What Actually Gets Checked

The [GBP Scorecard](/tools/gbp-scorecard) scores your profile across 35 specific factors organized into five sections. Here's what each section covers and what separates top-ranked profiles from average ones in competitive home service markets.

### Section 1: Profile Completeness (7 factors)

This section checks whether every field in your GBP is filled in accurately. It sounds basic. Most profiles fail on at least two or three points.

The factors: primary category accuracy, secondary category count and relevance, business description keyword quality, services section completeness, hours accuracy (including special hours and holiday hours), phone number format and consistency, and website URL pointing to a relevant service page rather than a generic homepage.

The most common failures: services section left at three or four entries when the platform allows dozens; business description using generic copy ("We provide quality HVAC services with customer satisfaction as our priority") instead of service-specific, service-area-specific language that matches actual homeowner searches; website URL pointing to the homepage when a service-specific landing page would produce better engagement signals.

One specific point from the 2026 ranking factors research: businesses that are listed as "open" at the time of search rank higher than those showing as closed. Accurate hours that reflect your actual availability -- including emergency availability if you offer it -- directly affect your eligibility for searches happening outside standard business hours.

### Section 2: Visual Content (6 factors)

Photo count, photo recency, photo diversity, video presence, cover photo quality, and logo clarity.

The threshold that consistently separates top-ranked profiles from mid-pack ones is not a specific number -- it's the recency signal. A profile with 200 photos added in 2022 and nothing since sends a different signal than one with 60 photos added consistently over the last 12 months. Google's behavioral data shows that profiles with recently uploaded photos generate higher click-through rates, which feeds the engagement signal that reinforces ranking.

The photo types that produce the most engagement in home service GBPs: trucks with visible branding parked at active job sites, before-and-after shots of completed work, and photos of technicians in uniform at work. Stock photos and generic office images produce lower engagement and lower click-through rates.

### Section 3: Review Performance (8 factors)

This is the highest-weighted section because it covers the signals that have grown most in importance. The eight factors: total review count, average star rating, review velocity (new reviews per month), recency of most recent review, response rate to reviews, response quality (specific vs. generic), percentage of reviews that mention services or neighborhoods, and Q&A section activity.

The most common mistake contractors make on reviews is optimizing for total count rather than velocity. A profile with 180 reviews, the last one posted six months ago, is being outpaced by a competitor with 85 reviews adding six new ones per month. BrightLocal's analysis found that a local business that had 150 five-star reviews slipped in rankings after six months without new feedback, then rebounded within days of restarting review requests and gaining ten new reviews in two weeks.

Velocity matters. Recency matters. And the language in reviews matters more than most contractors know -- Google's AI uses review content to match profiles to specific conversational queries. A plumber with reviews mentioning "water heater replacement," "emergency service," and specific neighborhoods appears for those searches more reliably than one with generic praise.

### Section 4: Activity Signals (7 factors)

Google Posts frequency, post content quality, Q&A section completeness, business description update recency, special offers or promotions posted, service area accuracy, and booking/appointment link functionality.

Most home service GBPs in this section have the same profile: zero or one Google Post in the last 90 days, an empty Q&A section, and no active offers. The companies consistently ranking in the top three are posting weekly and answering Q&A proactively.

The Fitz Designz practitioner case study from 2026 documented a 21% increase in local search impressions for a landscaping client after three months of weekly GBP posts and Q&A updates. The mechanism is engagement: posts that generate views and clicks produce behavioral signals that reinforce prominence.

For contractors, the easiest post cadence: one post per week featuring a completed job from that week. Photo from the job, brief description of what was done, the neighborhood or city where it happened, and a soft CTA ("Call us for yours"). That post takes three minutes and produces three things: fresh activity signal, relevant service and location language, and a new photo.

### Section 5: Website and Citation Signals (7 factors)

This section covers the web presence signals that extend beyond the GBP itself: NAP consistency across major directories, website mobile loading speed, website linking structure to the GBP, local backlink presence, schema markup implementation, social profile citations, and whether the website linked from the GBP is the same domain as appears in citations across the web.

The most common failure here is NAP inconsistency -- the business name, address, or phone number appearing differently on Yelp, HomeAdvisor, the BBB, or contractor-specific directories than it appears on the GBP. Google cross-references these to validate business legitimacy. Inconsistencies introduce doubt and suppress prominence signals.

The highest-value citation for home service contractors is Chamber of Commerce membership. A link from the local Chamber is one of the most trusted, locally-relevant backlinks Google recognizes -- and unlike most local SEO tactics, it takes a single action (join the Chamber) that produces a permanent citation. One Chamber link, in the practitioner data from Fitz Designz, produced better results than dozens of generic directory citations.

## What Competitors Ahead of You Are Almost Certainly Doing

Based on the ranking factor research and practitioner data, here is the typical profile of a company consistently in the top two map pack positions for competitive home service searches:

**They have a review system, not a review hope.** They ask every customer at job close via text with a direct review link. They're generating 6 to 12 new reviews per month, not 1 to 2. Their most recent review is from this week or last week, not last month.

**They post to GBP weekly.** Usually a job photo with a brief description, the service type, and the neighborhood. It takes three minutes and they've been doing it for 12+ months. Their profile shows recent activity when Google evaluates freshness.

**Their primary category is precisely right.** Not "Contractor" or "Service Company" -- the specific category that matches their highest-value service and the searches they most want to win.

**Their services section is complete.** Every service they offer is listed, each with a short description. This is what Google reads when deciding whether your profile is relevant to a specific service search.

**They own their website.** Their GBP links to a website on a domain they control, with a service page (not just a homepage) that reinforces the services and service area they've listed in the profile. The website loads in under three seconds on mobile.

**Their hours are accurate and include emergency availability.** If they answer calls until 9pm, they're listed until 9pm. If they offer after-hours emergency service, that's in their description and their attributes.

None of this is secret knowledge. It's documented in the ranking factor research, visible on the top-ranked profiles in your market, and available to any contractor who wants to implement it. The gap between top-ranked and mid-ranked profiles is almost never a technical advantage -- it's a consistency advantage.

## The Fastest Wins

If you're currently ranked 4th through 8th in your market and want to move up, three actions produce the fastest measurable improvement:

**1. Fix your primary category first.** Log into your GBP and verify your primary category matches your highest-value service exactly. This is the single highest-weighted factor in the Whitespark 2026 survey. It takes two minutes to change if it's wrong.

**2. Restart review velocity immediately.** Whatever your current monthly review rate is, double it. Set up a post-job text template with a direct review link and send it within an hour of every job close for the next 60 days. Review velocity improvements show up in ranking within weeks, not months.

**3. Start the weekly post habit today.** Post one job photo with a brief service description this week. Schedule a 15-minute calendar block for every Friday to repeat it. Ninety days of consistent weekly posts is one of the most documented drivers of GBP prominence improvement in home services.

Those three actions, implemented immediately, address the relevance signal (category), the prominence signal (reviews), and the activity signal (posts) -- covering the three pillars of Google's ranking decision.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the most important factor for ranking in the Google map pack?

Primary category selection is the single most important GBP factor according to the Whitespark 2026 Local Search Ranking Factors survey -- the most comprehensive annual study of local SEO signals. It determines the searches you're eligible to appear for. Getting it wrong means competing with a disadvantage that reviews, photos, and posts can't fully compensate for. Beyond category, review signals have grown from 16% to 20% of overall local pack ranking weight from 2023 to 2026, making review velocity the most important ongoing activity.

### How many Google reviews do I need to rank in the map pack?

Review count is less predictive than review velocity and recency. A profile adding 8 new reviews per month consistently will typically outrank one with a higher total count but no recent activity. Focus on building a system that generates consistent new reviews from every completed job rather than trying to reach a specific total. In most mid-size markets, being competitive requires adding at least 4 to 6 new reviews per month. In dense metros, top-ranked profiles are often adding 10 to 15.

### Does posting on Google Business Profile help with rankings?

Yes, through the behavioral signal it creates. Regular posts generate profile views and engagement, which Google tracks as an activity signal indicating the business is active and relevant. Practitioners have documented meaningful ranking improvements after sustained weekly posting campaigns. The direct ranking effect of posts is smaller than reviews or category accuracy, but the cumulative effect of 12 months of weekly posting is significant compared to a profile that hasn't posted in six months.

### Why am I not showing up in the map pack even though I have good reviews?

Three common causes: distance (you may simply be too far from where searchers are located for specific queries), category mismatch (your primary category doesn't align precisely with what's being searched), or website signals (your linked website may be slow, mobile-unfriendly, or targeting the wrong keywords). The GBP Scorecard helps identify which section is dragging your score. Distance is the one factor you can't fix without physical location changes -- everything else is addressable.

### How long does it take to improve map pack rankings?

Category fixes can show impact within days to a couple of weeks. Review velocity improvements typically show ranking movement within 30 to 60 days of sustained new reviews. Activity signals (posts, Q&A) compound over 60 to 90 days. Website and citation signals take longer -- typically 90 to 180 days for meaningful ranking movement. The fastest wins are category accuracy and restarting review velocity, which is why those are the first two actions recommended above.

### What is NAP consistency and why does it matter for GBP?

NAP stands for Name, Address, and Phone number. Google cross-references your business information across dozens of online directories and databases to validate that your business is legitimate and located where you say it is. Inconsistencies -- a different phone number on Yelp, a slightly different business name on the BBB, an old address on HomeAdvisor -- introduce doubt into that validation process and can suppress your prominence signals. Fixing NAP inconsistencies across major directories is typically a one-time cleanup that produces lasting ranking improvement.

### Is a Google Business Profile audit something I can do myself?

Yes, and the GBP Scorecard makes it structured. The five-section framework covers the categories most owners don't systematically check: profile completeness, visual content, review performance, activity signals, and website and citation signals. Most owners who run through the full audit identify gaps they weren't aware of -- even ones who thought they'd optimized their profile. The tool provides benchmark context so you can see where your score sits relative to what top-ranked profiles look like on the same rubric.
