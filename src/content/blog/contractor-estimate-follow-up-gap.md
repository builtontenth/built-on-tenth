Here is the story most HVAC, roofing, and plumbing owners tell themselves about an estimate that didn't close:

They weren't really serious. They were just price shopping. They probably went with the cheaper guy. There's nothing more I could have done -- if they wanted the job done, they would have called back.

That story is almost always wrong.

ServiceTitan's 2025 Residential Trades Report, which surveyed more than 1,000 contractors across 20 trades, found that businesses consistently following up on unsold estimates generate between 1 and 15% additional revenue from that activity alone. Among thriving contractors, 39% reported generating that lift from follow-up. Among struggling contractors, only 23% did -- not because follow-up doesn't work for them, but because most of them weren't doing it consistently.

The homeowner who got your estimate and went quiet wasn't necessarily uninterested. They were often overwhelmed, comparison shopping, waiting to see if their spouse agreed, or simply busy. They expected you to follow up. When you didn't, they either called someone else who did or let the project slide until a different contractor reached out first.

The revenue sitting in your unsold estimate backlog is recoverable. Most of it doesn't require new leads, new marketing spend, or new technicians. It requires a follow-up system -- and in most companies, that system doesn't exist.

## What the Numbers Say About Why Estimates Don't Close

The default assumption when an estimate goes cold is that the homeowner decided no. The data consistently shows something different.

According to research on sales follow-up behavior across service businesses, more than 80% of sales require five or more follow-up attempts. The average salesperson makes two attempts and stops. In home services, where the average company makes fewer than one follow-up attempt per unsold estimate, the gap is even wider.

The Blue Collar Success Group's 2025 KPI benchmarks found that nearly half of high-revenue contractors ($10M+) reported follow-up on unsold estimates accounting for 11 to 15% of their total income. That's not a marketing metric -- that's a pipeline management metric. Revenue that was already generated once in the form of an estimate sent, then recovered through the discipline of following up.

The reason the gap persists isn't that contractors don't believe follow-up matters. It's that no one owns it. The tech who ran the call sent the estimate and moved to the next job. The CSR who booked the original call isn't tracking the outcome. The owner is reviewing total revenue but not the conversion rate from estimate sent to job booked. Nobody is watching the outbox.

## Why "I Already Tried Calling Them" Isn't a Follow-Up System

The most common version of follow-up in home service companies: the tech or office staff calls the homeowner once, gets no answer or a "we're still thinking about it," and logs the estimate as pending. Two weeks later, it's written off.

That's not a follow-up system. That's one touch that happens to involve a phone call.

What makes the difference between a company that recovers 5% of cold estimates and one that recovers 25% is not effort -- it's structure. Specifically, it's three things:

**1. Timing of the first touch**

Research on lead response rates -- applicable to estimate follow-up as well as initial lead contact -- consistently shows that contact rates drop sharply after 24 hours. A follow-up call or text sent the morning after an estimate is delivered reaches a different homeowner than one sent four days later. The job is still fresh. The quote is still on their mind. They haven't made a competing decision yet.

Most contractors send estimates and follow up when they get around to it, which in practice means three to seven days later. By then, the homeowner has moved on mentally even if they haven't formally declined.

The top-quartile behavior: a text sent the day after the estimate, every time, without exception. Not a call that requires someone to pick up -- a text that they read when they're available and can respond to when they're ready.

**2. Multiple touches across different channels**

One call, no answer, written off. That sequence recovers almost nothing.

Three touches across text, call, and email over seven to ten days recovers 20 to 30% of cold estimates according to documented contractor results. Each touch serves a different purpose and reaches the homeowner in a different moment of their day.

The [follow-up sequence article](/insights/the-follow-up-sequence-that-moved-close-rate-to-40-percent) covers the exact language, timing, and channel for each of the four touches that moved one company's close rate from 28% to 40% without changing a dollar of marketing spend. The mechanics translate directly to estimate follow-up: the sequence is the same, the trigger is the sent estimate rather than the initial call.

**3. Someone who owns the outbox**

The estimate backlog has no natural owner in most home service companies. It's everyone's responsibility and therefore nobody's.

The fix is simple but requires deliberate assignment: one person checks the estimate pipeline daily, identifies estimates that have been cold for 48 hours with no follow-up, and makes or triggers the first touch. That role can sit with a CSR, an office manager, or an owner who builds a 15-minute daily habit. What it cannot be is unassigned.

Companies that implement this single operational change -- one person, one daily check, one consistent trigger for the first follow-up touch -- typically see close rate improvement within 30 days. Not because the homeowners who were uninterested suddenly became interested, but because the homeowners who were waiting for someone to follow up finally got the call they were waiting for.

## The Estimate Pipeline Problem Most Owners Can't See

Here is the visibility gap that lets the follow-up problem compound undetected:

Most home service owners know their total revenue. Some know their monthly booking count. Very few know their estimate-to-close rate by job type, by technician, or by time period -- and almost none know how many estimates went cold this month without a single follow-up attempt.

Without that visibility, the follow-up gap is invisible. Revenue looks like a marketing problem (not enough leads) or a market problem (homeowners aren't spending) when it's actually a pipeline problem (leads are coming in, estimates are going out, and nobody is working the outbox).

The diagnostic question is simple: of the estimates you sent last month that didn't become booked jobs, how many received at least one follow-up contact from your company?

If you don't know that number, run it. Pull your estimate history for the last 60 days. Find the ones that didn't close. Check how many received a call, text, or email after the estimate was delivered. That count is your follow-up rate. For most companies running this exercise for the first time, the number is below 30%.

The ServiceTitan 2025 data shows this isn't unusual. It's the industry baseline. The companies recovering 11 to 15% of additional revenue from follow-up aren't doing anything exotic -- they're just doing what most companies skip.

## Why This Beats Buying More Leads

The math on follow-up versus paid lead generation is worth making explicit, because most owners instinctively reach for more leads when revenue is flat rather than examining their estimate pipeline.

Say your roofing company sends 50 estimates per month at a $14,000 average ticket and closes 27% of them. You're booking 13 to 14 jobs. You want 18 jobs per month.

**Option 1: Buy more leads.**
To book 4 to 5 more jobs per month at 27% close rate, you need 15 to 19 more qualified estimates. At $80 per LSA lead and a 55% booking rate on contacted leads, you need roughly 27 to 35 additional leads per month. At $80 each that's $2,160 to $2,800 in additional monthly spend to generate the 4 to 5 jobs you want.

**Option 2: Follow up on existing cold estimates.**
You're already generating 50 estimates per month and closing 27%. The 36 to 37 that go cold each month contain the same jobs you're trying to buy. Recovering 15% of those cold estimates is 5 to 6 additional booked jobs per month. Recovery rate of 20% is 7.

The follow-up path generates more jobs from the same call volume at zero incremental lead cost. The math is not close.

This is the same calculation behind the whole Built on Tenth framework: before adding spend to the top of the funnel, find out how much is leaking in the middle. The estimate pipeline is one of the two largest leaks. The other is missed calls -- and the [phone revenue calculator](/tools/phone-calculator) runs the same exercise on that gap.

## The Three-Touch Sequence That Works for Replacement Work

The specific follow-up sequence matters. Vague check-ins ("just following up on that estimate!") produce lower response rates than touches that add value or create gentle urgency. Here is the sequence that produces the best documented results for replacement-ticket work in HVAC, roofing, and plumbing:

**Touch 1 -- Day 1 after estimate, text:**
> "Hi [name], just wanted to make sure you received the estimate for [job]. Happy to answer any questions or walk through the options. Just reply here or call us at [number]."

No pressure. Confirms receipt. Opens a channel. Reaches them when they're likely at work or going about their day in a way a call would interrupt.

**Touch 2 -- Day 3, call:**
> "Hi [name], this is [name] from [company]. I sent over the estimate for your [job] a couple days ago and wanted to follow up and see if you had any questions. We do have some availability [this/next week] if you want to get on the schedule."

The scheduling mention creates soft urgency without pressure. The homeowner who's been meaning to call back now has an easy opening.

**Touch 3 -- Day 7 to 10, email or text:**
> "Hi [name] -- reaching out one more time on the [job] estimate. Happy to adjust scope, talk through financing options, or schedule a time to go over it together. Just let me know what would be helpful."

Introducing financing at Touch 3 recovers a specific subset of cold estimates: homeowners who want the job done but hesitated on the total price. The connection between financing availability and close rate is significant in all three trades -- adding it as an explicit offer at Touch 3 converts leads that would otherwise stay cold indefinitely.

After three touches with no response, the estimate goes to a 30-day dormant file. It doesn't get worked off, but it doesn't get deleted. Thirty days later, a single "checking in" text goes out. A meaningful percentage of dormant estimates reactivate at that touch -- the homeowner's situation changed, the season shifted, or they simply forgot they'd gotten a quote and are starting the process again.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many times should I follow up on an unsold estimate?

Three times over seven to ten days, then a single touch at 30 days if there's been no response. The first touch is a text the day after the estimate is delivered. The second is a call on day three. The third is an email or text on day seven to ten, often introducing financing as an option. Most recoveries happen within the three-touch window. The 30-day touch recovers a smaller but meaningful percentage of estimates where the homeowner's situation has changed.

### What percentage of cold estimates can I realistically recover?

Based on documented contractor results, a structured three-touch follow-up system recovers 15 to 30% of estimates that didn't close on first contact. The lower end reflects higher-ticket replacement work where the homeowner needs more decision time. The upper end reflects service and repair estimates where the problem is acute and the homeowner was simply waiting for someone to confirm the next step. At any recovery rate, the revenue recovered exceeds what additional marketing spend on the same call volume would produce.

### Who should own the estimate follow-up process?

One specific person -- not "whoever has time." The most common assignment is a CSR or office manager with a defined daily check: any estimate sent more than 48 hours ago with no logged follow-up gets a Touch 1 today. At companies above $3M revenue, a dedicated pipeline coordinator handles this exclusively. Below that, it folds into an existing CSR role with a scheduled daily block rather than treated as an on-demand task.

### Is following up too aggressively going to annoy homeowners?

Three touches over seven to ten days is not aggressive. The homeowner who finds that excessive was not going to book regardless. The far more common scenario is a homeowner who was waiting for you to reach back out, interpreted silence as disinterest, and chose someone else who did follow up. The risk of following up too much is real but much smaller than the risk of not following up at all -- which the data consistently shows is the default behavior in this industry.

### How is this different from the follow-up sequence article already on the site?

The [follow-up sequence article](/insights/the-follow-up-sequence-that-moved-close-rate-to-40-percent) focuses on the four-touch sequence for inbound leads that didn't book on the first call. This article focuses specifically on estimates -- the later stage of the pipeline where a homeowner has already received a price and gone quiet. The mechanics overlap significantly, but the trigger point and the conversation framing differ. An estimate follow-up is confirming a decision the homeowner is already close to making. An inbound lead follow-up is building enough trust to get them to the estimate stage.

### What if I don't know my current estimate-to-close rate?

Pull your invoicing software or CRM for the last 90 days. Count estimates sent and jobs completed that started as those estimates. The ratio is your close rate. If your software doesn't make this easy, use a simple tally: take last month's completed jobs, trace each one back to whether it started as an estimate, and divide. It's worth 20 minutes of manual work because this number -- more than any marketing metric -- tells you how healthy your pipeline actually is.
