A hailstorm hits your market on a Tuesday afternoon.

By Wednesday morning your phone is ringing before your CSR gets to the office. By noon you've missed 40 calls. By the end of the week your voicemail is full and your competitors have already inspected half the roofs in the neighborhoods you service.

This is not a hypothetical. It's the pattern in every roofing market that gets a significant storm event. The demand is sudden and compressed. The companies that capture it are the ones built to handle surge volume. Most roofing companies are not.

The average roofing company answers **44% of inbound calls during a storm event.** On a normal week that same company might answer 70 to 75%. The storm doesn't break their phone system. It breaks the capacity behind it -- the number of people available to pick up, the process for handling call overflow, and the follow-up system for the calls that still get missed.

This article covers what the gap costs, how to staff for surge without adding permanent headcount, and how to handle the two call types that storm season produces -- insurance claims and retail replacements -- because they require completely different scripts and different timelines.

## What Storm Season Actually Does to Call Volume

Most roofing companies see 60 to 90 calls per week in their normal operating rhythm. A significant hailstorm or wind event in their market can produce 180 to 350 calls in the first 72 hours.

That's not a 20% increase. It's a 3 to 5x surge that hits without warning and compresses itself into a short window -- because homeowners who notice damage all start calling contractors at the same time, and the ones who don't get answered quickly move on.

The compression is the problem. By day four or five after a storm, call volume drops back toward normal even though the repair demand hasn't changed. Homeowners found someone else. Or they gave up for now and will call again when the ceiling starts leaking in six months.

**The cost of missed storm calls is higher than missed calls in any other trade.**

Here's why: roofing replacement tickets run $12,000 to $28,000 on a residential job. Insurance replacement jobs often run higher. At a $16,000 average replacement ticket and a 35% close rate on answered calls, each missed call during a storm event represents **$5,600 in potential lost revenue** before you've said a word.

Run that on a company that misses 120 calls during a three-day storm surge:

120 missed calls x 35% close rate x $16,000 ticket = **$672,000 in potential revenue** that went to whoever answered their phone.

## Why Roofing Offices Break During Surge

The failure is structural, not personal. A CSR who handles 40 calls per week with competence and calm is not a different person when 140 calls hit. The office just isn't built for what storm season demands.

**Single-point staffing.** Most roofing companies have one CSR or office manager handling all inbound calls. On a normal week that works fine. During a storm surge, one person physically cannot answer two lines simultaneously, return voicemails, schedule inspections, and handle the homeowners who walk into the office. The calls pile up faster than they can be worked.

**No overflow routing.** When the primary line is busy, calls go to voicemail. Not to a second line. Not to an answering service. Not to a mobile backup. To voicemail, which fills up, and then to a ring-with-no-answer that the homeowner interprets as "this company is unavailable."

**No surge protocol.** There's no documented process for what happens when volume spikes. Who picks up the second line? Who handles walk-ins? Who works the callback queue? Without answers to those questions, everyone improvises and the result is inconsistent and slow.

**No pre-built follow-up system.** The calls that do get answered but don't immediately book get logged in someone's memory or a sticky note. The follow-up that should happen within two hours doesn't happen until the next day or not at all -- because the CSR is too busy handling new inbound volume to work the backlog.

The timing and scripts apply directly to roofing -- including a section specifically on storm season and insurance claim follow-up.

## The Staffing Math During a Storm Event

You don't need to hire two permanent CSRs to handle storm season. You need a plan that adds temporary capacity during the surge window and routes calls intelligently.

**What a working surge setup looks like:**

**Answering service as overflow, not replacement.** A roofing-trained answering service ($300 to $700/month for an active account) takes calls when your primary line is engaged. They capture name, number, address, and damage description. They do not attempt to schedule -- they triage and log. Your CSR works the callback queue the same day, not the next morning.

The distinction matters: an answering service that tries to schedule inspections without knowing your availability creates double-booking and frustrated homeowners. Their job is to answer, capture, and confirm that someone will call back within two hours. That alone keeps the lead alive long enough for your CSR to close it.

**A dedicated storm callback number.** During a known storm event, route a secondary number to a mobile that a second person -- the owner, the office manager, a trusted admin -- monitors specifically for callbacks. Homeowners who leave a voicemail and get a call back within 30 minutes from a real person convert at a dramatically higher rate than ones who wait until the next day.

**Pre-built callback scripts.** The person working surge callbacks doesn't need to know how to run the full appointment-booking process. They need one job: confirm the damage, confirm the address is in your service area, and schedule the inspection. Write that script before storm season. Practice it once. Anyone in your office can run it.

**What this costs vs. what it recovers:**

A roofing-specific answering service with surge coverage runs $400 to $600/month. If it helps you capture two additional replacement jobs per storm event at $16,000 average, the service pays for itself on the first call it answers during the first storm.

## Insurance Claim Calls vs. Retail Replacement Calls

Storm season produces two fundamentally different types of inbound calls. They require different scripts, different timelines, and different follow-up cadences. Most roofing offices handle them identically, which underserves both.

### Insurance Claim Calls

A homeowner calling about storm damage who hasn't filed a claim yet is not ready to book an inspection in the same way a retail replacement customer is. They're in a different part of their decision process. They may not know whether their damage is claimable. They may have already called their insurance company and are waiting to hear back. They may have filed a claim and are waiting for an adjuster.

**What they need from your first call:**

Clarity on what happens next -- from their side and yours. Your CSR's job is to set an inspection, not to wait for the claim to resolve. The inspection is what determines whether they have a claimable loss and what the scope looks like.

**The script for insurance claim calls:**

> "The best thing to do right now is get one of our guys out to look at it before the adjuster comes. We document everything -- photos, measurements, all the damage -- and we can actually be there when the adjuster shows up so nothing gets missed. When are you home? We can usually get out within 48 hours."

Three things this script does: it positions the inspection as serving the homeowner's interest in the claim (not just the company's interest in the job), it introduces the idea of being present at the adjuster meeting (a real differentiator), and it moves directly to scheduling without waiting for the homeowner to resolve the insurance question first.

**The follow-up timeline for insurance claim calls:**

These leads have a longer window than retail calls but require more touchpoints because the homeowner's decision process takes longer. Follow up at 48 hours to ask if the adjuster has been in touch. Follow up again when the adjuster appointment is scheduled. Be present at the adjuster meeting if at all possible -- roofing companies that show up at adjuster meetings close at 65 to 75% on those jobs vs. 35 to 45% when they're not present.

### Retail Replacement Calls

A homeowner calling because their roof is 18 years old and they noticed granules in the gutter is a retail replacement lead. No insurance claim involved. They're shopping, comparing, and will likely get two or three estimates.

This call has a shorter decision window and a higher urgency to move quickly -- because without the insurance process creating a natural follow-up structure, the homeowner makes their decision and moves on. Speed matters more here than with insurance calls.

**The script for retail replacement calls:**

> "We can have someone out to take a look and give you a full estimate -- no obligation. We're typically in [neighborhood] on [day] -- does [morning or afternoon] work better for you?"

Direct. Moves to scheduling without detour. Offers a specific day (which reduces the "I'll think about it" response) and asks a choice question rather than a yes/no.

**The follow-up timeline for retail replacement calls:**

Use the same 4-touch sequence as any estimate follow-up -- call at 2 hours if they don't book immediately, text at 4 hours, call again at 48 hours, text at 7 days. ## What Top Roofing Companies Do Differently

The roofing companies that capture storm season at the highest rate share four operational characteristics:

**They have a storm protocol written down before storm season.** Not in anyone's head. Written down, practiced, and accessible. Who handles overflow. What number calls route to when the main line is engaged. What the callback priority order is. What script the person working callbacks uses. This document takes two hours to write and prevents $200,000 in missed revenue per storm event.

**They track their answer rate during storm events separately from normal operations.** Normal-week answer rate and storm-week answer rate are different numbers with different solutions. A company that averages 72% answer rate annually may be running at 38% during the 72 hours after a hailstorm. You can't fix a problem you can't see. Pull call data after every storm event and compare answer rate to your normal baseline.

**They separate insurance and retail in their CRM from first contact.** Insurance leads and retail leads have different follow-up timelines, different conversion rates, and different revenue profiles. Companies that tag them separately from the first call can track close rate by type and see where their process is breaking down. The ones that don't mix everything together and can't explain why their storm-season close rate looks the way it does.

**Their CSRs know the insurance process.** A CSR who can walk a homeowner through what to expect from the claims process -- what the adjuster looks for, what documentation matters, what the supplement process is if damage is missed on the first assessment -- creates a trust relationship on the first call that a competitor who just books the inspection doesn't. That trust is why homeowners don't call three other companies after speaking with yours.

## The Angi and Lead Platform Question During Storm Season

Storm season is when roofing companies most commonly increase their Angi or lead aggregator spend. That's usually the wrong move.

Here's why: the problem during storm season is not lead volume. It's capacity to handle the leads that are already coming in organically from Google, GBP, and referrals. Adding paid lead volume to an office that's already missing 60% of its inbound calls produces more missed leads at higher cost per lead.

The math: if your office is running at 40% answer rate during storm surge and you add $3,000 in Angi leads, you're answering 40% of those paid leads too. You're paying $130 per lead and booking 1 in 5 -- a cost per booked job that doesn't work at any ticket size.

Fix the phone operation first. Answer rate above 75% before adding paid volume. 2. Do you have a documented surge protocol -- written down, not just understood?
3. Do you have overflow call routing active right now, before the next storm?
4. Does your CSR use different scripts for insurance claim calls vs. retail replacement calls?
5. How many calls from your last storm event never received a follow-up attempt?

## What to Do Before the Next Storm

1. **Install call tracking if you don't have it.** You need to know your answer rate during surge, not estimate it. CallRail or your CRM's call tracking feature. Set it up this week.
2. **Write your surge protocol.** One page. Who handles overflow. What number calls route to when the main line is engaged. What script the overflow handler uses. Where the callback queue lives. Print it and put it where your CSR can find it.
3. **Set up overflow routing.** Whether that's an answering service, a second mobile line, or a forwarding rule -- the main line should never ring to voicemail while your office is staffed. Route it somewhere that captures the lead.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is a typical answer rate for roofing companies during storm season?

During a surge event, the average roofing company answers 40 to 50% of inbound calls -- significantly lower than their normal-week answer rate of 68 to 75%. The gap is almost entirely explained by single-point staffing and no overflow routing. Companies with documented surge protocols and overflow handling in place typically maintain 70 to 80% answer rates even during peak surge windows.

### How should a roofing CSR handle insurance claim calls differently?

Insurance claim calls require a script that positions the inspection as serving the homeowner's interest in the claim -- not just booking a sales visit. The CSR should explain that documenting damage before the adjuster visit protects the homeowner's claim, and offer to be present at the adjuster appointment. Follow-up on insurance leads is longer and more touchpoint-heavy than retail replacement follow-up because the homeowner's decision timeline tracks the claims process, not just their own preference.

### How long does a homeowner wait before calling another roofing company after a storm?

Most homeowners call two to three roofing companies in the first 48 hours after noticing damage. If they can't reach one within an hour or two, they move to the next number on the list. After 72 hours, the urgency window for most homeowners starts to compress -- not because the damage is fixed, but because the acute stress of the discovery phase passes. Getting the inspection booked in the first 48 hours is materially more important for roofing than in almost any other trade.

### Should I add Angi leads during storm season?

Generally no. Storm season is a demand problem -- there are more homeowners with damaged roofs than you can handle. The constraint is phone capacity, not lead volume. Adding paid leads to an office running at 40% answer rate produces more missed leads at higher cost. Fix the answer rate first. Once you're consistently above 75% during surge, paid leads can add incremental volume. The breakdown of when Angi leads make sense for roofing is covered in the [Angi leads analysis](/insights/are-angi-leads-worth-it-for-roofing-companies).

### What should roofing companies do with missed calls from a storm event?

Work them the same day, not the next morning. Pull the missed call list from your phone system or call tracking platform. Any call that came in during the last four hours gets a callback attempt immediately. Anything older than four hours gets a text: "Hi [name], this is [company] -- we missed your call about roof damage. We're getting out to a lot of homes right now but wanted to make sure you didn't get left waiting. Are you still looking to get someone out?" That text, sent the same day, recovers 20 to 30% of calls that would otherwise go cold overnight.

### How do I handle the adjuster meeting for insurance roofing claims?

Show up. Roofing companies that attend adjuster meetings close at 65 to 75% on those jobs -- roughly double the rate for companies that don't. At the meeting, bring your own documentation: photos, measurements, and a written scope of the damage. Adjusters appreciate organized contractors and are more likely to approve full replacement scope when the documentation is complete. Brief the homeowner beforehand on what to expect so they're not surprised by anything the adjuster says.

### What's the difference in close rate between insurance and retail roofing leads?

Insurance replacement leads typically close at 45 to 60% for companies that are present at the adjuster meeting and maintain contact through the claims process. Retail replacement leads close at 30 to 45% depending on competitive density in the market and the company's follow-up consistency. The insurance close rate is higher because the homeowner's decision has essentially been made for them by the damage event -- the variable is which contractor they trust to execute the claim and the work.

### How many CSRs does a roofing company need during storm season?

A roofing company taking 40 to 70 calls per week in normal operations needs one dedicated CSR plus an overflow option during storm events. Companies taking 100+ calls per week in normal operations need two full-time CSRs plus overflow. The overflow option -- an answering service, a forwarding number to a second staffer, or a temporary hire -- is not optional if you want to maintain answer rates above 70% during the 48 to 72-hour surge window after a significant storm.
