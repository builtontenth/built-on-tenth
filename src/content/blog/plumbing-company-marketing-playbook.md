Most marketing advice for home service companies was written with HVAC in mind. The economics are different enough in plumbing that following HVAC playbooks without adjustment is one of the quieter ways plumbing companies overpay for calls that should cost less and under-invest in channels that would produce more.

The differences matter:

**Plumbing average ticket is lower.** A residential service call runs $280 to $650. A water heater replacement runs $1,200 to $2,800. A full repipe runs $4,000 to $12,000. Against an HVAC replacement at $9,000 to $18,000, the math on what you can afford to pay per booked job is tighter at the service-and-repair end.

**Plumbing decisions are faster.** A homeowner with a leaking pipe under their sink is not comparison shopping. They're calling the first plumber who answers. The urgency window is measured in hours, not days. That changes which channels matter and what your phone operation needs to do.

**Plumbing call types are more varied.** Emergency calls, planned replacement calls, and remodel-related calls all arrive with different urgency levels, different decision timelines, and different revenue profiles. Handling all three identically leaves money on the table.

This playbook covers what to spend, where to spend it, and what to measure at three revenue stages: $500K, $1M, and $3M. The math at each stage is different. The priorities shift as you grow.

## The Number That Runs Everything

Before the stage-by-stage breakdown, one number frames all of it: **cost per booked job.**

In plumbing, the target is 8 to 12% of your average ticket. Here's what that looks like across job types:

| Job Type | Avg Ticket | Max Cost/Booked Job (10%) |
|---|---|---|
| Emergency service call | $380 | $38 |
| Water heater replacement | $1,900 | $190 |
| Drain cleaning / repair | $280 | $28 |
| Full repipe | $7,500 | $750 |
| Remodel rough-in | $3,200 | $320 |

The emergency service call target of $38 per booked job is nearly impossible to hit with paid advertising at current click costs. That doesn't mean you don't take emergency calls -- it means you don't buy them with paid ads. Emergency plumbing calls come from GBP, map pack position, and direct calls from people who have used you before. Those channels produce calls at $8 to $25.

The water heater replacement at $190 per booked job works on Google Ads, LSAs, and even some aggregator spend if your answer rate and booking rate are solid.

The repipe at $750 per booked job has plenty of room for paid acquisition, targeted campaigns, and even direct mail to older neighborhoods.

## Stage 1: $500K Revenue

At this stage you're typically running one to three trucks, handling most service calls yourself or with one tech, and your marketing budget is somewhere between $1,500 and $4,000 per month. Every dollar matters because the margin for error is thin.

**What to prioritize:**

**Google Business Profile above everything else.** At $500K revenue, GBP optimization is the highest-ROI activity available to you. It costs nothing but time and produces emergency calls at $5 to $15 each -- the lowest cost per booked job of any channel. Getting to 40+ reviews with a 4.5+ star rating and consistent posting gets you into map pack position for your primary service keywords without paying for a single click.

Emergency plumbing searches -- "plumber near me," "emergency plumber [city]," "clogged drain [city]" -- have extremely high local intent. The homeowner is not reading blog posts. They're looking at the map pack and calling the first three results. Being in that top three, with strong reviews, is worth more than any paid campaign at this revenue stage.

**LSAs as your first paid channel.** Once your GBP is active and you have 25+ reviews, start the LSA verification process. At $500K revenue, LSA leads run $55 to $110 each for plumbing in most markets -- higher intent than aggregator leads, exclusive, and easier to answer quickly when you're a small operation that stays close to the phone. A budget of $600 to $1,000/week produces 6 to 15 leads per week depending on your market. At a 50% booking rate, that's 3 to 7 booked jobs per week from a single channel.

**What to skip at this stage:**

Google Ads require conversion tracking, landing page optimization, and ongoing campaign management to produce results at a cost per booked job that works. At $500K revenue you don't have the time or infrastructure to do it right. The clicks are expensive ($28 to $55 for plumbing keywords in most markets) and without proper tracking you're guessing. Wait until $1M+ to add Google Ads.

Aggregator platforms (Angi, Thumbtack, HomeAdvisor) produce shared leads at high cost per booked job. At a lower average ticket and thin margins, the math rarely works unless you have the phone ops to outcompete every other contractor on response time. Most $500K plumbing companies don't yet. Save that budget for GBP and LSAs.

**Benchmark for this stage:**

- Monthly marketing spend: $2,000 to $4,500
- Cost per booked job target: $65 to $140
- Primary channels: GBP (free), LSAs ($1,500 to $3,000/month)
- Answer rate target: 82%+
- Booking rate target: 55%+

## Stage 2: $1M Revenue

At $1M you're running three to six trucks, have a dedicated CSR or office manager handling phones, and your marketing budget is $4,000 to $9,000 per month. You have enough call volume to see patterns, enough data to make channel decisions, and enough margin to test.

**What to prioritize:**

**LSAs remain the core paid channel.** By this stage you should have 50+ Google reviews and a working LSA account. Increase LSA budget to $2,000 to $4,000/month and focus on maintaining the ranking signals that keep you in the top three positions: review velocity (6+ per month), response rate (80%+), and low dispute rate. The [HVAC LSA setup guide](/insights/how-to-set-up-google-local-service-ads-hvac) covers the ranking mechanics in detail -- the signals are identical for plumbing.

**Add Google Ads for planned work keywords.** Emergency calls come from GBP and LSAs. Planned work -- water heater replacement, water softener installation, repiping estimates -- comes from people who are searching with more consideration. These keywords are less competitive and cheaper per click than emergency terms. A tightly managed campaign targeting water heater and repipe keywords with a conversion-tracked landing page can produce booked jobs at $140 to $260 each. That works on a $1,900 water heater or a $7,500 repipe. It doesn't work on a $280 drain cleaning call.

Separate your campaigns by job type. Emergency keywords and planned work keywords should be in different campaigns with different bids and different landing pages. Mixing them blends your cost per booked job into a number that hides which job types are profitable on paid ads and which aren't.

**Build your review system.** Going from 50 to 120 reviews over 12 months requires a consistent ask process -- not a one-time push. The fastest system: a text from the tech immediately after the job closes, with a direct link. That alone produces 60 to 70% of your review volume if done consistently. Reviews compound map pack position, which compounds emergency call volume, which is your lowest-cost channel. Every review your team doesn't ask for is foregone free traffic.

**What to watch:**

At $1M revenue, the gap between your answer rate and your booking rate starts costing real money. A company taking 180 calls per month with a 65% answer rate and 48% booking rate on answered calls is booking 56 jobs per month. Improve answer rate to 85% and booking rate to 62% and that same call volume produces 95 booked jobs -- a 70% increase in output with zero added marketing spend.

The [phone revenue calculator](/tools/phone-calculator) shows what your specific answer rate and booking rate gap costs per month at your call volume and average ticket.

**Benchmark for this stage:**

- Monthly marketing spend: $5,000 to $9,000
- Cost per booked job target: $90 to $200
- Primary channels: LSAs ($2,500 to $4,500/month), Google Ads -- planned work ($1,200 to $2,500/month), GBP (free)
- Answer rate target: 85%+
- Booking rate target: 60%+

## Stage 3: $3M Revenue

At $3M you're running eight to eighteen trucks, have multiple CSRs, and your marketing budget is $12,000 to $22,000 per month. The constraint shifts from getting enough calls to converting them efficiently and allocating budget across channels without overpaying on any one of them.

**What to prioritize:**

**Channel-level cost per booked job tracking.** At this revenue, you cannot manage marketing by feel. You need cost per booked job broken out by channel -- LSAs, Google Ads by campaign type, GBP, direct, referral -- tracked monthly in your own system, not in your agency's report. Companies at $3M revenue that don't have this number are almost always overpaying on at least one channel by $150 to $300 per job and don't know it.

The [HVAC marketing benchmarks analysis](/insights/hvac-marketing-benchmarks-2026-what-top-performers-spend) shows what top-quartile tracking infrastructure looks like -- the same framework applies to plumbing. Only 14% of home service companies at this revenue level track cost per booked job by channel. That gap is where budget gets wasted without a clear signal to cut it.

**Expand LSAs to full coverage.** At $3M with 100+ reviews and established ranking signals, LSAs should be running at full budget during peak plumbing demand periods -- spring (frozen pipe aftermath, spring remodels), fall (pre-winter prep), and any time local weather creates surge demand. Scale LSA budget to $4,000 to $6,000/month during peak and pull back during slow windows when demand doesn't support it.

**Direct mail for planned replacement work.** Neighborhoods with homes built between 1975 and 1995 have significant water heater and galvanized pipe replacement demand coming. A direct mail campaign to those ZIP codes -- not a generic plumbing ad, but a specific "Is your water heater over 10 years old?" or "Homes built before 1990 in [city]: what you should know about your pipes" -- generates planned-work leads at $60 to $120 each. Lower volume than digital, higher average ticket per job, and zero competition from other plumbers who are all chasing the same emergency keywords online.

**Individual CSR performance tracking.** At $3M with multiple CSRs, the booking rate spread between your best and worst CSR is directly measurable and directly costly. The [CSR performance analysis](/insights/why-hvac-csrs-are-losing-you-300k-per-year) shows how a 25-point spread between top and bottom performer translates in dollar terms. The math is built on HVAC numbers but the mechanics are identical for plumbing -- substitute your average ticket and the output is the same. If you want to see the same issue from the field side, the [revenue per technician plumbing analysis](/insights/revenue-per-technician-plumbing-business-scaling) shows what weak dispatch, low average ticket, and low utilization do to technician output.

**Benchmark for this stage:**

- Monthly marketing spend: $13,000 to $22,000
- Cost per booked job target: $110 to $280 blended (lower for emergency channels, higher for planned work)
- Primary channels: LSAs ($4,000 to $6,500/month), Google Ads by job type ($3,000 to $5,000/month), GBP (free), direct mail ($1,500 to $3,000/month)
- Answer rate target: 90%+
- Booking rate target: 65%+

## Plumbing Phone Operations: Urgency Calls vs. Planned Work

Plumbing phone operations have a split that HVAC doesn't deal with at the same intensity: the difference between a homeowner with a burst pipe at 7pm and a homeowner who wants a water heater quote sometime next week.

These two call types require completely different handling.

**Urgency calls (emergency service, active leak, no hot water):**

The decision window is measured in minutes. The homeowner is calling two or three plumbers simultaneously. The one who answers and can commit to a same-day or next-morning arrival wins the job before the others finish listening to the voicemail.

Your CSR's only job on this call is to answer, confirm the issue, confirm the service area, give an arrival window, and get a name and number. Not a price quote. Not a detailed diagnostic. An arrival window.

> "We can have someone there between 2 and 4 this afternoon -- does that work? I'll have [tech name] call you 30 minutes before he heads over."

Two things that statement does: gives a specific window (not "sometime today"), and sets the expectation of a pre-arrival call so the homeowner doesn't feel like they're waiting blindly. Both reduce cancellations and increase the likelihood the homeowner doesn't call the next plumber while waiting.

After-hours urgency calls are where most plumbing companies lose the most revenue. A homeowner with a burst pipe at 9pm who gets voicemail will find someone. It just won't be you. An answering service or an on-call number that captures the lead and commits to a callback within 30 minutes recovers the majority of after-hours emergency calls. At $380 average service ticket, recovering three after-hours calls per week is $57,000 per year.

**Planned work calls (water heater replacement, repipe estimate, fixture installation):**

This caller is not in crisis. They're comparing options and will likely get two or three estimates before deciding. The booking ask is still important but the urgency framing is different.

> "We can usually get out within two to three days for an estimate -- and if it turns out it needs to be done sooner, we can often move faster. When are you generally home?"

The goal is the same -- get the appointment -- but the script acknowledges that this isn't an emergency without giving the homeowner a reason to feel like you're deprioritizing them.

Follow-up on planned work calls that don't book immediately follows the same structure as any estimate follow-up -- call within 2 hours, text within 4 if no answer, follow up at 48 hours, final touch at 7 days. The [4-touch follow-up sequence](/insights/the-follow-up-sequence-that-moved-close-rate-to-40-percent) has the scripts for each touch including trade-specific adaptations for plumbing.

## The Review Velocity Problem Specific to Plumbing

Plumbing has a structural review problem that HVAC doesn't: high job volume with low ask rate.

A plumbing company doing $1M in revenue might run 500 to 700 service calls per year. At a 3% organic review rate (customers who leave reviews without being asked), that's 15 to 21 reviews per year. At an 8% ask-and-convert rate, it's 40 to 56 reviews per year. That's the difference between a GBP profile that sits at 45 reviews after three years of operation and one that hits 150 reviews in the same window.

The problem is that most plumbing service calls feel transactional. The tech fixes the thing, gets paid, leaves. Nobody asks for a review because it feels awkward right after charging someone $380 to unclog a drain.

Two things that work:

**The text after the job, not during.** Asking for a review at the moment of payment feels like a condition of service. Asking 30 minutes after the tech leaves -- via a text from the company number -- feels like a genuine follow-up. "Hi [name], this is [company]. Hope everything is working well. If [tech name] did a good job, a quick Google review means a lot to us: [link]." That text, sent within an hour of job completion, produces a 15 to 22% review conversion rate.

**Tech-specific review velocity tracking.** Which techs are generating reviews and which aren't tells you whether the ask is actually happening. A tech averaging 0.3 reviews per job is asking. A tech averaging 0.05 is not. That data, shared in a monthly morning meeting, moves behavior faster than any training session because it's specific and visible.

## What to Do This Week

1. **Find your current cost per booked job.** Total marketing spend last month divided by total jobs booked from marketing. That's your blended number. Then try to split it by channel -- even rough estimates show you where to look. Use the [marketing cost calculator](/tools/marketing-calculator) to benchmark against the targets for your revenue stage above.
2. **Check your GBP review count and velocity.** How many reviews did you receive last month? If it's under 5 at $500K or under 8 at $1M, you don't have an ask process. Build the post-job text sequence this week.
3. **Separate your call types.** Pull last month's calls and tag them: emergency, planned work, after-hours. Calculate answer rate and booking rate for each type separately. You may find that you're converting emergency calls well and losing planned work calls to slow follow-up -- or the reverse. You can't fix what you're averaging together.
4. **If you're at $500K and not on LSAs, start verification today.** The process takes two to four weeks. Starting now means being live before your next busy period.
5. **If you're at $1M+, pull your channel-level cost per booked job.** Every channel you're spending on should have a number attached. Any channel above 15% of your average ticket gets cut or fixed before adding budget anywhere else.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much should a plumbing company spend on marketing?

At $500K revenue: $2,000 to $4,500/month (5 to 9% of revenue). At $1M: $5,000 to $9,000/month (5 to 8% of revenue). At $3M: $13,000 to $22,000/month (5 to 7% of revenue). The percentage tends to decrease as revenue grows because marketing efficiency improves with more reviews, stronger map pack position, and better referral volume. Cost per booked job -- not percentage of revenue -- is the number to manage against.

### What marketing channels work best for plumbing companies?

Google Business Profile optimization produces the lowest cost per booked job for emergency calls -- typically $8 to $25 -- because high-intent emergency searchers go to the map pack first. LSAs produce the best cost per booked job among paid channels, typically $75 to $160 for plumbing. Google Ads work for planned replacement work (water heaters, repiping) when campaigns are separated by job type and conversion tracking is in place. Aggregators like Angi and Thumbtack produce the highest cost per booked job and are worth deprioritizing once LSAs are live and ranking.

### How do I get more plumbing reviews on Google?

Send a text from the company number within one hour of job completion with a direct Google review link. Not an email, a text -- open rates are significantly higher. Track review volume by tech monthly. Techs who aren't generating reviews aren't asking. A 15 to 22% review conversion rate on sent texts is achievable and produces 6 to 12 new reviews per month for most $1M plumbing companies.

### Are Google LSAs worth it for plumbing?

Yes, for companies with 25+ Google reviews and completed verification. LSA leads for plumbing run $55 to $110 each in most markets -- exclusive, high intent, and charged per lead rather than per click. Companies with strong answer rates (85%+) and booking rates (60%+) produce booked jobs from LSAs at $95 to $180 each. That works at water heater and repipe ticket sizes. It's tight but workable on service call tickets if phone performance is strong.

### Should a plumbing company use Angi or Thumbtack?

Rarely as a primary channel. Shared leads at $60 to $120 each with a lower average ticket than HVAC produce cost per booked job numbers that are hard to make work. If you're going to test aggregators, Thumbtack's model -- where you choose which leads to bid on -- tends to work better than Angi's automatic shared lead model for plumbing, because you can select higher-ticket job types and skip the drain cleaning leads that don't cover acquisition cost. Test with a fixed monthly cap and track cost per booked job before scaling.

### How do I handle after-hours plumbing calls?

An on-call number or answering service that commits to a callback within 30 minutes captures the majority of after-hours emergency leads. Generic voicemail loses nearly all of them -- a homeowner with an active leak at 9pm finds someone within 15 minutes of starting to call. A plumbing-trained answering service ($300 to $600/month) answers, captures the problem and address, confirms someone will call back within 30 minutes, and texts you the lead. You or your on-call tech handles the callback. At $380 average emergency service ticket, recovering three additional after-hours calls per week covers the answering service cost for the year in the first month.

### What is a good booking rate for a plumbing CSR?

60 to 68% of answered calls resulting in a booked appointment is the top-quartile range for plumbing. Industry average is 42 to 50%. The gap on urgency calls is usually answer-rate related -- calls that get answered convert at high rates because the homeowner is already motivated. The gap on planned work calls is usually a follow-up problem -- homeowners who don't book on the first call rarely get a second attempt. Tracking booking rate separately for emergency and planned work calls shows you which problem you actually have.

### How does plumbing marketing differ from HVAC marketing?

Three key differences. First, average ticket is lower for most plumbing work, which tightens the cost per booked job ceiling on paid channels -- especially for service calls. Second, emergency call urgency is higher, which means phone answer rate matters more and the decision window is shorter. Third, plumbing has more job type variety (emergency service, planned replacement, remodel work) that requires different channels, different scripts, and different follow-up timelines. HVAC marketing playbooks work as a starting point but need adjustment for these differences before applying them to plumbing spend.
