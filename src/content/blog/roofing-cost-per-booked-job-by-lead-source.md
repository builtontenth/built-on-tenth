Most roofing companies compare marketing channels the same way: which one gives me the most leads for the least money per lead?

That framing produces bad decisions. It's the reason companies run Angi at $120/lead and think it's competitive with LSAs at $75/lead -- without ever calculating that their Angi leads book at 25% and their LSA leads book at 55%. The $120 lead costs $900 per booked job. The $75 lead costs $190. The "cheaper" source is almost five times more expensive when measured correctly.

The number that actually matters in roofing is **cost per booked job** -- what you pay per signed contract after factoring in lead price, contact rate, and close rate. That number changes the channel comparison completely. And in roofing specifically, two variables make the output wildly different from market to market: how fast you call new leads, and whether you offer financing at the estimate.

This article breaks down cost per booked job for every major roofing lead source in 2026, explains why phone speed and financing change every channel's output, and gives you the framework to build the comparison for your own operation.

---

## The Formula That Changes Everything

Before the channel breakdown, the math every roofing company should run monthly:

**Cost per booked job = Lead cost / (Contact rate x Close rate on contacted leads)**

At a 50% contact rate and a 40% close rate on contacts:

- A $75 lead costs **$375 per booked job**
- A $120 lead costs **$600 per booked job**
- A $180 lead costs **$900 per booked job**

At a 75% contact rate and a 55% close rate (top-quartile phone ops):

- A $75 lead costs **$182 per booked job**
- A $120 lead costs **$291 per booked job**
- A $180 lead costs **$436 per booked job**

Same channels. Same lead prices. The phone operations gap produces a 2 to 3x difference in actual cost per booked job across every source simultaneously.

This is the single most important insight in roofing lead economics: improving your contact rate and close rate is almost always worth more than switching channels. A roofing company that calls every lead within five minutes and runs a three-touch follow-up sequence will produce dramatically better numbers from every source on this list than a company with superior channel allocation but slow follow-up. The [roofing phone operations article](/insights/why-roofing-companies-miss-60-percent-inbound-calls-storm-season) covers what those gaps cost during storm season specifically.

## Source 1: Referrals

**CPL: $0 to $40 (referral incentive if any)**
**Close rate: 60 to 75% (highest of any source)**
**Cost per booked job: $0 to $115 (strong ops)**

Referrals are the most profitable lead type in roofing and the most underworked. A homeowner referred by a neighbor who used you last year already trusts you before the first call. They're comparing you to nobody. They're not waiting for three estimates. Close rates on referral leads run 60 to 75% -- more than double the industry average close rate on cold leads.

The cost is low to zero. A referral incentive program (gift card, discount, or cash reward per referred job) runs $25 to $50 per referral and still produces the lowest cost per booked job of any source once you account for the close rate differential.

Most roofing companies treat referrals as something that happens organically. Top performers systematize them: a text to every completed customer 30 days after the job asking if they know anyone with a similar roofing situation, a yard sign left for 30 days after every install, and a formal ask at the final walkthrough. Those three touches, done consistently, produce 2 to 4 additional referral leads per month on a company doing 15 to 25 jobs per month.

## Source 2: Google Business Profile (Organic Map Pack)

**CPL: $0 to $30 (time investment only)**
**Close rate: 55 to 65%**
**Cost per booked job: $0 to $86 (strong ops)**

Map pack calls for roofing produce the second-lowest cost per booked job after referrals. A homeowner who searched "roofing company near me" or "roof damage repair [city]" and called from the map pack has self-selected -- they found you, they're ready to talk, and they're not comparing you to two other contractors who just received the same contact information simultaneously.

The investment is in building and maintaining map pack position: review velocity, GBP activity, and website signals. Once established, the marginal cost per call is effectively zero.

The review dynamics specific to roofing -- what count and velocity you need by market size -- are covered in depth in the [roofing map pack review guide](/insights/how-many-google-reviews-roofing-company-map-pack). The short version: match or exceed your top competitor's monthly review add rate, respond to every review, and post weekly. Those three habits maintain position at close to zero ongoing cost.

## Source 3: Google LSAs

**CPL: $55 to $90 (most US roofing markets)**
**Close rate: 45 to 55% on contacted leads**
**Cost per booked job: $147 to $240 (strong ops), $314 to $514 (avg ops)**

LSAs are the best paid channel for roofing companies in most markets. Leads are exclusive -- the same homeowner contact does not go to three competitors. The Google Verified badge signals to homeowners that your company has been vetted. LSA leads arrive as phone calls over 90% of the time, which is the highest-intent format.

Roofing LSA leads average $71 per lead nationally based on LocaliQ data across 50,000+ service businesses. In competitive storm-prone markets during active weather periods, cost per lead can reach $150+. The variance is large -- your actual cost depends on market density and seasonal timing.

The close rate advantage over aggregator platforms is significant: roofing contractors report 20 to 30% close rates on LSA leads compared to 5 to 10% on shared directory leads, according to data from Improve & Grow. That gap explains why LSA cost per booked job outperforms Angi cost per booked job even when Angi leads cost less per contact -- the conversion chain on LSA is fundamentally stronger.

The one operational requirement that makes or breaks LSA economics for roofing: response time. Google tracks answer rate and response speed as LSA ranking signals. A roofing company missing storm-surge calls because they're understaffed gets penalized in LSA ranking at exactly the moment lead volume is highest and most valuable.

## Source 4: Google Ads (Search)

**CPL: $100 to $200 (varies heavily by campaign structure)**
**Close rate: 35 to 45% on contacted leads**
**Cost per booked job: $267 to $533 (strong ops), $571 to $1,143 (avg ops)**

Google Ads for roofing produce leads at $100 to $200 depending on market competitiveness and campaign structure. Roofing is one of the most expensive Google Ads categories -- clicks on emergency roofing keywords in competitive markets routinely run $40 to $80 each, and $100+ in dense metros.

The cost per booked job on Google Ads is workable at strong ops levels because the average roofing replacement ticket of $14,000 to $22,000 supports higher acquisition costs. A $350 cost per booked job on a $16,000 replacement is 2.2% of job revenue -- a reasonable marketing cost.

What makes Google Ads underperform for most roofing companies is campaign structure. A blended "roofing" campaign with mixed intent keywords (emergency repair, installation research, DIY queries) produces a higher CPL than segmented campaigns. The HVAC campaign structure article covers the segmentation logic -- the same principles apply to roofing. Separate campaigns for emergency repair, replacement, and inspection convert at meaningfully different rates and should have separate budgets, bids, and landing pages.

Without proper conversion tracking -- call tracking connected to booked jobs rather than just click volume -- Google Ads optimization is impossible. Most roofing companies running Google Ads without call tracking are optimizing toward the number their agency reports, not toward actual signed contracts.

## Source 5: Angi / HomeAdvisor

**CPL: $100 to $180 (shared leads)**
**Close rate: 20 to 30% on contacted leads (heavily speed-dependent)**
**Cost per booked job: $444 to $720 (strong ops), $952 to $1,543 (avg ops)**

Angi's cost per booked job math is the starkest example of why CPL comparisons mislead. The same contact goes to up to three roofing contractors simultaneously. The one who calls within five minutes contacts 70 to 80% of leads. The one who calls two hours later contacts 20 to 30%.

At a $130 average lead cost, a 65% contact rate, and a 30% close rate on contacts: **$666 per booked job.** That's a workable number on a $16,000 replacement if your operations support it. At a 35% contact rate and 25% close rate: **$1,486 per booked job.** Same channel, same lead price, five times the acquisition cost.

The full analysis of when Angi works and when it doesn't for roofing -- including the contact rate math and the speed-to-call requirement -- is in the [Angi leads breakdown](/insights/are-angi-leads-worth-it-for-roofing-companies). The short version: Angi is only defensible economics for roofing companies that call within five minutes, run a three-touch follow-up sequence, and are tracking cost per signed contract (not cost per lead) monthly.

## Source 6: Facebook / Meta Ads

**CPL: $40 to $150 (varies by offer type and targeting)**
**Close rate: 20 to 35% on contacted leads**
**Cost per booked job: $152 to $750 (strong ops), $325 to $1,607 (avg ops)**

Facebook leads in roofing behave differently from search-based leads. A homeowner on Facebook who sees your ad was not searching for roofing -- they were interrupted by it. That lower intent means lower contact rates and lower close rates than LSA or Google Ads on comparable follow-up.

Where Facebook performs for roofing:

**Storm damage awareness campaigns.** After a hail or wind event in your market, Facebook can efficiently target homeowners in affected zip codes with storm inspection offers. The audience is warm (they experienced the storm), the message is timely, and cost per lead runs $40 to $70 in the days following an event. These leads close at higher rates than cold Facebook traffic because the homeowner is aware of the damage context.

**Retargeting website visitors.** Homeowners who visited your website and didn't convert are already familiar with your company. Facebook retargeting puts your brand in front of them at $10 to $30 per lead in most markets and closes at 2 to 3x the rate of cold Facebook leads.

**Financing promotion campaigns.** "Get your new roof for $189/month" -- this offer converts better on Facebook than a generic service ad because the monthly payment framing addresses the liquidity objection directly. The connection between financing and close rate is covered in the [roofing financing article](/insights/how-offering-financing-closes-more-roofing-jobs). Facebook is one of the better distribution channels for that specific message.

Where Facebook underperforms: as a cold-outreach channel for $18,000 insurance replacements. The intent gap between a homeowner who found your ad in their feed and one who searched "storm damage roofer near me" is significant, and it shows up in the close rate.

## How Financing Changes Every Channel's Output

One variable not reflected in the CPL or close rate benchmarks above: whether you offer financing at the estimate.

Roofing contractors offering financing at the point of estimate close 30 to 50% more jobs than those who don't, according to data from FieldFuze and the Consumer Federation of America. That improvement applies equally across every lead source -- the channel that generated the lead doesn't change whether the homeowner can write a $16,000 check.

The practical impact on cost per booked job:

At a 27% close rate (industry average, no financing), a $130 lead costs $803 per booked job at average contact rates.

At a 38% close rate (with financing offered consistently), the same $130 lead costs $570 per booked job.

That's a $233 improvement per job across every source -- without changing your channel mix, your lead price, or your response time. Financing is a close-rate lever that works on Angi leads, LSA leads, Google Ads leads, and Facebook leads simultaneously. The mechanics of implementing it and the dealer fee math are in the [roofing financing guide](/insights/how-offering-financing-closes-more-roofing-jobs).

## Insurance vs. Retail: Different Economics by Source

One dimension the channel comparison above doesn't capture: insurance replacement jobs and retail replacement jobs have different economics by source.

Insurance replacement work -- storm damage claims, adjuster involvement -- produces the highest close rates of any job type when worked correctly. Roofing contractors present at adjuster meetings close 65 to 75% of those jobs, compared to 35 to 45% for retail replacement. The average ticket is also typically higher because the insurance scope includes code upgrades and components the homeowner wouldn't pay for out of pocket on a retail job.

Source performance on insurance work:

**Storm-based Facebook and Google Ads:** High value. A homeowner who searched "hail damage roof repair" or saw your post-storm Facebook ad is in the claims process. Lead quality is higher than retail Facebook or general Google Ads.

**Angi during storm events:** Sharply reduced value. Storm surge increases Angi call volume but also increases the speed-to-call competition. In a market that just took a hail event, every roofing company on Angi is calling the same leads. Response time becomes the only differentiator.

**LSAs during storm season:** Strong. Exclusive leads at maintained cost per lead even during surge. The constraint is lead volume -- LSAs don't produce the volume of Angi during a major event, but the conversion rate is significantly higher.

**Referrals and GBP after a storm:** Best value. Homeowners who call based on a neighbor's recommendation or map pack position have already decided to trust you before the first call. These are the contacts to prioritize during surge.

The insurance claim workflow -- what to do from initial contact through supplement filing -- is covered in the [insurance claim job guide](/insights/how-to-work-roofing-insurance-claim-job). The phone operations context for storm season is in the [storm season article](/insights/why-roofing-companies-miss-60-percent-inbound-calls-storm-season). Both affect cost per booked job by determining what happens to leads after they arrive.

## Building Your Own Channel Comparison

The benchmarks above are starting points. Your actual cost per booked job by source is more useful -- and building it requires three months of clean data.

**What to track for each source:**

1. Total leads received by source (call tracking, CRM source tags)
2. Total contacts made from those leads (calls answered, conversations had)
3. Total estimates set from contacts
4. Total signed contracts from estimates set
5. Total revenue from signed contracts
6. Total spend on that source

Divide total spend by signed contracts. That's your cost per booked job for that source.

If you don't have this data for the last 90 days, build the tracking today so you have it in 90 days. Even rough source attribution -- "how did you hear about us" at the first call -- produces directional insight that blended numbers hide.

## What to Do This Week

1. **Pull your last 90 days of signed contracts by source.** Rough attribution is fine. How many contracts came from Angi, from LSAs, from referrals, from Google Ads? Divide your spend on each source by the contracts it produced.
2. **Check your contact rate on the highest-volume paid source.** Of the last 50 leads from that source, how many did you actually speak with? If it's below 60%, your cost per booked job is being driven by phone ops more than channel economics.
3. **Check whether you're offering financing consistently.** On your last 20 estimates, how many times did the CSR or salesperson present a financing option before the homeowner asked about price? ## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the cheapest lead source for roofing companies?

By cost per lead, referrals and GBP organic map pack calls are the cheapest -- effectively zero marginal cost per lead once the system is in place. By cost per booked job, referrals also win because close rates are 60 to 75%. Among paid sources, LSAs typically produce the lowest cost per booked job because leads are exclusive and high-intent. Angi's lower per-lead cost is typically offset by the shared lead structure and lower close rates in most operations.

### What is a good cost per booked job for roofing?

The target is 8 to 12% of your average ticket. At a $16,000 average replacement: $1,280 to $1,920 per booked job is the acceptable ceiling. Top-performing roofing companies with strong phone operations and consistent follow-up typically achieve $200 to $600 per booked job from their best channels. Any channel consistently above 12% of your average ticket should either be fixed or cut before the budget gets renewed.

### Are Angi leads worth it for roofing companies?

Only with strong phone operations. At average response times and follow-up rates, Angi leads for roofing produce cost per booked job in the $900 to $1,500 range on a $130 average lead cost -- which doesn't pencil out on most job types. With five-minute response times and a three-touch follow-up sequence, Angi can produce booked jobs in the $400 to $700 range, which is workable on replacement tickets. The full math and operational requirements are in the [Angi leads analysis](/insights/are-angi-leads-worth-it-for-roofing-companies).

### How do Google LSAs compare to Google Ads for roofing?

LSAs produce lower cost per booked job in most scenarios because leads are exclusive and arrive as phone calls rather than website clicks. LSA leads close at 20 to 30% compared to 5 to 10% on shared directory leads, according to contractor-level data. Google Ads costs $100 to $200 per lead and requires precise campaign structure to perform well. Both channels have a role -- LSAs for high-intent immediate service searches, Google Ads for planned replacement research keywords. Running both produces lower blended cost per booked job than either alone.

### Does response time really change roofing lead economics that much?

Yes, more than almost any other variable. A 75% contact rate vs. a 35% contact rate on the same lead at the same price produces a 2x difference in cost per booked job. On Angi's shared leads, a five-minute response contacts 70 to 80% of leads and a two-hour response contacts 20 to 30% -- making the effective cost per booked job five times higher from identical spend. The math applies across all paid channels: every lead you don't contact is a lead you paid for and lost.

### How does offering financing affect cost per booked job?

It improves close rate by 30 to 50% when presented consistently at estimates, which directly reduces cost per booked job from every source proportionally. At a 27% close rate vs. 38% close rate, cost per booked job drops by roughly 30% across every channel simultaneously -- no change to lead volume, lead price, or response time required. Financing is one of the few levers that improves the output of all channels at once.

### What are the best lead sources for roofing during storm season?

Referrals and GBP map pack calls are the best value during storm season because homeowners who chose you specifically convert at much higher rates than shared leads during a surge. Among paid channels, LSAs maintain quality during storms because they're exclusive. Angi's economics deteriorate during storm events because competition intensifies and speed-to-call becomes even more critical. Facebook can work well for post-storm targeted campaigns in affected zip codes. The storm season phone ops context is in the [roofing storm season article](/insights/why-roofing-companies-miss-60-percent-inbound-calls-storm-season).
