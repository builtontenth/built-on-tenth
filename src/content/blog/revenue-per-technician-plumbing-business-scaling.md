A plumbing company doing $1.2M with six technicians and a company doing $1.2M with four technicians are not the same business.

The first is producing $200,000 per tech. The second is producing $300,000 per tech. Same total revenue. Completely different operational picture. The first company is either carrying two underperforming technicians or running a dispatch and job mix that doesn't generate enough revenue per field hour. Either way, adding a seventh truck won't fix it -- it will dilute the number further.

Most plumbing owners track total revenue. Few track revenue per technician. That gap lets inefficiency compound invisibly while the owner assumes the business is growing because the top line is moving.

**Revenue per technician is the number that tells you whether your business is actually scaling or just getting bigger.**

Top-performing plumbing companies generate **$200,000 to $300,000 in annual revenue per technician**, according to benchmarks from Brentwood Growth's 2025 KPI analysis of plumbing operations. Companies in the bottom half of the industry generate $95,000 to $140,000 per tech. On a five-person team, that spread represents **$300,000 to $800,000 in annual revenue difference** from the same headcount -- before adding a single truck.

This article covers how to calculate the number, what drives the gap between top and bottom performers, how to find which technicians are pulling it down, and what the number tells you about when to actually hire.

## How to Calculate Revenue Per Technician

The calculation is straightforward. The discipline to run it monthly is where most companies fall short.

**Annual revenue per technician:**

Total revenue for the period divided by the number of field technicians (not including office staff, dispatchers, or the owner if they're not running calls).

Example: $1.4M revenue / 6 technicians = $233,333 per tech annually.

**Monthly revenue per technician:**

Total revenue for the month divided by field tech headcount. Track this every month, not just annually. Monthly tracking shows you seasonal patterns, the impact of technician changes, and whether dispatch or job mix decisions are moving the number.

**Per-tech by individual:**

Total revenue attributable to each technician for the period divided by the number of working days in that period. This is the number that reveals your spread -- the gap between your highest and lowest producers.

Most field service platforms -- ServiceTitan, Housecall Pro, Jobber -- generate this report automatically. If yours doesn't, pull job revenue by assigned technician from your invoicing system and run the math in a spreadsheet. It takes 20 minutes the first time and five minutes every month after that.

**What to do with the number:**

Compare it to the $200,000 to $300,000 benchmark. If your blended number is below $160,000, something structural is wrong -- job mix, scheduling density, average ticket, or billable utilization. If your blended number is above $250,000 and you have capacity, that's a green light to add a truck. If your blended number is at $180,000 and you're considering adding headcount, adding a technician at the same productivity level makes the problem worse, not better.

One of the fastest ways to raise the number on the emergency-service side is to improve your local visibility before adding more spend. The [plumbing GBP optimization guide](/insights/plumbing-google-business-profile-optimization) covers how stronger map pack presence increases the volume of high-intent calls that usually produce the best same-day technician output.

## The Five Variables That Drive the Number

Revenue per technician is a product of five variables. Understanding which ones are pulling your number down is the only way to fix it deliberately rather than by luck.

### Variable 1: Average Ticket

A technician running four jobs per day at $280 average generates $1,120 in daily revenue. A technician running four jobs per day at $450 average generates $1,800. Same job count. Same hours. $58,240 difference per year from one technician.

Average ticket in plumbing is driven by job mix (emergency calls command 1.5 to 2x standard rates), flat-rate pricing discipline (technicians who quote from memory underprice compared to ones using a price book), and upsell behavior (a tech who flags a corroded shutoff valve on a water heater call and books the repair adds revenue to the same truck roll).

The highest-margin job types in plumbing -- emergency calls, water heater replacements, repipes, and maintenance agreements -- produce gross margins of 60 to 70%. Basic repairs and drain cleaning typically run 50 to 60%. A technician whose job mix skews toward basic repairs will produce lower revenue per day than one whose mix includes more installation and emergency work, even at identical job counts.

**The diagnostic question:** Pull average ticket per technician for the last 90 days. If your spread is more than $80 between highest and lowest, job mix or pricing is the primary driver.

### Variable 2: Jobs Per Day

Top-performing plumbing technicians complete 3.5 to 5.5 jobs per day depending on job type and market density. Companies averaging 2.5 to 3 jobs per tech per day have a scheduling problem, a drive-time problem, or both.

Drive time is the most commonly underestimated drag on jobs per day. According to ServiceTitan's plumbing benchmarks, technicians spend 28% of their workday driving. On a 9-hour day, that's 2.5 hours of non-billable windshield time. In a tightly scheduled urban operation with jobs clustered by geography, that number drops to 18 to 20%. In a sprawling service area with poor dispatch routing, it runs 32 to 35%.

Cutting drive time by 25% on a five-tech operation running 8 hours each at $150 fully-loaded cost doesn't just save money -- it frees capacity that turns directly into additional jobs. ServiceTitan estimates a 50-tech operation loses $2.38M annually to windshield time. Scaled to a five-tech plumbing company, that's roughly $238,000 in recoverable capacity from better dispatch routing alone.

**The diagnostic question:** How many jobs per day per technician did you average last month? If it's below 3.5 on service and repair work, map the geographic spread of your jobs and look at whether dispatch is clustering by neighborhood or randomizing across your service area.

### Variable 3: Billable Utilization Rate

Billable utilization rate is the percentage of a technician's paid hours that are spent on revenue-generating work. Efficient plumbing operations target 65 to 75% billable utilization. Lower rates signal scheduling gaps, excessive travel, administrative time, or callbacks eating into productive hours.

A technician on the clock for 8 hours at 68% utilization is billing 5.4 hours. At $150/hour billed rate, that's $810 in billable output. The same technician at 75% utilization is billing 6 hours -- $900 in daily output, $90 per day more, $22,500 per year more from one technician.

Utilization drops below 65% for three specific reasons: too many callbacks (returning to fix work that should have been done on the first visit), scheduling gaps between jobs (dispatched with 45-minute windows between jobs rather than back-to-back), and jobs that run longer than booked because estimates were wrong.

First-call resolution rate -- the percentage of jobs resolved completely on the initial visit -- directly supports utilization. The target is 85% or above. Companies below 75% first-call resolution are generating callbacks that fill tech calendars with non-revenue-generating repeat visits, which reduces the number of new jobs each tech can handle per day.

**The diagnostic question:** What percentage of your technician hours last month were billable? If you can't answer this, you don't have utilization tracking. That's the first gap to close.

### Variable 4: Upsell Rate

A technician who goes to a job, fixes the stated problem, and leaves is generating the minimum revenue from that truck roll. A technician who fixes the problem, checks the adjacent equipment, and presents one additional repair or replacement opportunity when it's genuinely warranted is generating 15 to 35% more revenue per visit.

The upsell rate in plumbing isn't about pressure tactics. It's about techs being trained to look at the whole system, not just the symptom. A tech replacing a water heater who notices corroded supply lines and mentions it is doing the homeowner a favor -- and potentially saving a callback in six months. A tech who clears a drain without checking the P-trap condition or the drain slope is leaving information on the table.

The effective mechanism is a simple checklist: at the end of each job, the tech confirms they've checked the three most common secondary issues for that job type and documented findings in the work order. That documentation serves two purposes: it protects the company if a related issue appears later, and it creates a natural follow-up call if the homeowner declines the repair now but wants it addressed later.

**The diagnostic question:** What percentage of jobs last month included an additional repair or service recommendation beyond the original call? If you don't know, you're not tracking upsell behavior at all.

### Variable 5: Non-Billable Time

Non-billable time includes drive time (covered above), parts runs, administrative tasks, and waiting. The biggest controllable piece in most plumbing operations is parts runs -- technicians leaving a job to pick up a part that wasn't on the truck.

A parts run typically costs 45 to 90 minutes of non-billable time. On a technician running four jobs per day, one parts run drops that day to three and a half jobs. On a 250-working-day year, if a technician averages one parts run every three days, that's 83 runs at 60 minutes each -- roughly $12,450 in non-billable labor at $150/hour fully loaded, plus the opportunity cost of the job that didn't get done.

Truck stock standardization -- every truck carries the same high-frequency parts in the same quantities -- reduces parts runs dramatically. It's an operational decision, not a technician performance issue, but it shows up in individual revenue per tech numbers because the techs who need parts most often have the most interrupted days.

**The diagnostic question:** How many parts runs did each technician make last month? If your field service platform tracks this, pull it. If not, ask your techs at the next morning meeting. The ones who are honest will tell you it's more than you think.

## The Spread Between Your Best and Worst Technician

Every plumbing company with three or more technicians has a spread between its highest and lowest producer. The size of that spread tells you how much of your revenue problem is a hiring problem vs. a systems problem.

A 20 to 25% spread between top and bottom is normal and largely explained by experience level, job mix assignment, and market conditions. A 40 to 50% spread indicates a systems failure -- either your dispatch is routing easier jobs to certain technicians, your pricing varies by who quotes the job, or one or more technicians have a specific gap that hasn't been diagnosed.

**Running the per-tech analysis:**

Pull the last 90 days of invoiced revenue by assigned technician. For each technician calculate: total revenue, days worked, average daily revenue, jobs per day, and average ticket.

The pattern almost always shows one of three things:

Low average ticket with normal job count: the technician is underpricing or has a job mix skewed toward lower-value work. Check if they're using the price book consistently and whether their callbacks are being assigned back to them (which would inflate job count while suppressing net revenue).

Low job count with normal average ticket: a scheduling or drive-time problem. Map their jobs geographically and check dispatch routing.

Low job count and low average ticket: a training and accountability issue. This technician needs direct observation, call listening, and coaching before any operational changes will help.

## What the Number Tells You About Hiring

The most common mistake in plumbing business growth is hiring a new technician to solve a revenue problem that's actually a productivity problem.

If your current technicians are averaging $140,000 per year and you add a fifth at the same productivity level, you now have five technicians averaging $140,000 instead of four. You've added a salary, a truck payment, insurance, and benefits -- and generated the same revenue per head you had before.

**The hiring signal:**

Add a technician when two conditions are both true: your current technicians are running at or above 72% billable utilization consistently, AND you're turning away or delaying calls because you don't have available field capacity.

If only one of those is true, you don't have a capacity problem -- you have a productivity problem or a demand problem. Adding headcount to a productivity problem makes margins worse. Adding headcount to a demand problem makes capacity worse without solving the underlying issue.

**The capacity calculator:**

At 72% billable utilization, a technician working 250 days generates approximately 1,440 billable hours per year. At $150/hour billed rate, that's $216,000 in annual revenue potential. If your technicians are averaging $160,000, they're running at 74% utilization -- near the top of efficient range. Adding a technician at that productivity level adds $160,000 in revenue at whatever your current margin is on that revenue. That math works if demand supports it.

If your technicians are averaging $110,000, they're running at roughly 51% utilization -- well below efficient range. Adding a technician doesn't generate $110,000 in new revenue. It creates another truck running at 51% utilization while your overhead increases.

## How This Connects to Your Marketing Spend

Revenue per technician is the downstream number that validates or invalidates marketing spend.

Every marketing dollar's purpose is to put a technician on a job. If that technician generates $280 per job and runs 3.5 jobs per day, marketing is working through a productive system. If that technician generates $180 per job and runs 2.8 jobs per day, you're paying to fill a leaky bucket.

The [plumbing marketing playbook](/insights/plumbing-company-marketing-playbook) covers cost per booked job by channel and what to spend at each revenue stage. The channel mix only makes sense if the technician side of the equation is healthy. A company with a $150/call LSA cost and a 60% booking rate is generating jobs at $250 each. If those jobs go to technicians averaging $140 per job in revenue, the marketing ROI is negative before you account for labor, parts, or overhead.

Fix revenue per technician before scaling marketing spend. The order matters.

And if planned-work estimates are stalling after the first visit, that drags down technician output just as much as poor dispatch does. ## What to Do This Week

1. **Calculate your revenue per technician for last month.** Total invoiced revenue divided by number of field technicians. That's your blended number. Compare it to the $200,000 to $300,000 annual benchmark -- divide monthly by roughly 21 working days and multiply by 250 to get an annual run rate.
2. **Pull it by individual technician.** Same math, filtered by who was assigned the job. Find your spread -- the gap between your highest and lowest producer over the last 90 days.
3. **Identify which variable is driving the gap.** Use the diagnostic questions above for average ticket, jobs per day, utilization rate, upsell rate, and parts runs. You're looking for the single biggest lever, not a list of 10 things to fix simultaneously.
4. **Check your billable utilization rate.** If your field service platform tracks hours by category, pull billable vs. total hours for last month. If it doesn't, start tracking it manually -- it's the most important operational metric for a field service business.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is a good revenue per technician for a plumbing company?

Top-performing plumbing companies generate $200,000 to $300,000 in annual revenue per technician. Companies in the middle of the industry run $140,000 to $180,000. Below $120,000 per tech indicates a structural problem in job mix, scheduling, pricing, or billable utilization that won't be solved by adding more calls. Calculate your number monthly and track the trend -- a rising number indicates operational improvement; a flat or declining number means something in the system is degrading.

### How do I calculate revenue per technician?

Divide total revenue for the period by the number of field technicians who worked during that period. For monthly tracking: total monthly invoiced revenue / field tech headcount = monthly revenue per tech. Multiply by 12 for an annual run rate, or compare directly against other months to see the trend. For individual tracking, filter invoiced jobs by assigned technician and run the same calculation for each person separately.

### What causes low revenue per technician in plumbing?

The five most common causes in order of frequency: low average ticket (underpricing or low-value job mix), low jobs per day (excessive drive time, scheduling gaps, or callbacks), low billable utilization (parts runs, administrative time, waiting), no upsell behavior (techs completing the minimum scope and leaving), and high first-visit failure rate (callbacks eating future capacity). Most companies have more than one contributing factor. Pull the per-tech breakdown first to identify whether the problem is universal or concentrated in specific technicians.

### How many jobs per day should a plumber complete?

For service and repair work, 3.5 to 5.5 jobs per day is the top-quartile range depending on market density and average job duration. Emergency calls typically take 60 to 90 minutes including drive time. Water heater replacements run 2 to 3 hours. Drain cleaning runs 45 to 75 minutes. A technician averaging 2.5 jobs per day has a scheduling or drive-time problem unless they're running exclusively high-ticket installation work that justifies the lower job count with a higher average ticket.

### Should I add a technician or improve existing technician productivity first?

Improve productivity first unless your existing technicians are at or above 72% billable utilization and you're actively turning away calls. Adding a technician to a team running at 50% utilization generates new overhead without proportional revenue. Adding a technician to a team running at 73% utilization with a full dispatch board generates close to full productivity output from day one. The utilization rate is the gate check before hiring.

### How does dispatch routing affect revenue per technician?

Significantly. Technicians spending 28% of their day driving -- the industry average from ServiceTitan's benchmarks -- are generating about 72% of the revenue they could produce with tighter routing. Clustering jobs geographically by day (all Tuesday jobs in the northeast quadrant of your service area, all Wednesday jobs in the southeast) reduces drive time per job, increases jobs per day, and improves billable utilization without changing anything about how individual technicians perform. It's a dispatch decision, not a technician performance intervention, but it shows up directly in per-tech revenue.

### What billable utilization rate should I target for plumbing technicians?

65 to 75% is the efficient range for residential plumbing service and repair. Below 65% indicates scheduling gaps, excessive parts runs, or high callback rates eating into productive time. Above 75% is achievable with tight dispatch and strong first-call resolution but is difficult to sustain without burning out technicians. Target 70% as a steady-state goal and investigate any technician consistently below 62%.

### How do I improve a technician's average ticket without pressuring customers?

Three approaches that work without pressure: train techs to check adjacent equipment on every job type and document findings in the work order, use a flat-rate price book consistently so prices don't vary by who quotes the job, and build a job-type checklist that prompts techs to look at the three most common secondary issues for each call type. A tech who notices a 12-year-old water heater while fixing a drain is giving the homeowner useful information. Whether the homeowner acts on it is their decision -- but the tech who never mentions it isn't serving the customer fully either.
