The plumbing owner I reviewed last quarter was spending $4,200/month on marketing.

He thought it was working. The phone rang. Jobs got booked. His Google Ads agency sent a monthly report showing impressions, click-through rates, and something labeled "conversions."

When we traced it back -- what did he actually spend, what calls came in, what booked, what closed -- his cost per booked job was $610.

His average service ticket: $380.

He was spending $610 to acquire a job that generated $380. Every marketing dollar was actively destroying margin. He kept running it because the activity felt like growth.

Plumbing marketing math is different from HVAC marketing math. Lower average tickets, higher urgency, faster decisions, more varied call types. The same percentage-of-revenue benchmarks get recycled across both trades, but the economics that determine whether your spend is working are trade-specific.

This article gives you the plumbing-specific version: benchmarks by revenue stage, a five-step budget formula, and the three places plumbing companies most commonly leak marketing budget without a number attached to the loss.

---

## The Number Everyone Gets Wrong

Most articles about marketing budgets say "spend 7 to 10% of revenue." For plumbing, that's a starting point at best and dangerously misleading at worst.

Here's why: a plumbing company doing $1M in revenue with a $320 average ticket has completely different marketing economics than one doing $1M with a $650 average ticket. The same 8% spend -- $80,000 per year -- produces a completely different cost per booked job at different ticket sizes, which changes everything about how much you can afford to spend per channel.

ServiceTitan's industry guidance puts the minimum for growth at **10% of revenue** and suggests 5% as a "maintenance" budget that "yields minimal results." That's directionally right for plumbing companies trying to grow, but the real number is what you're paying per booked job -- and whether that number makes sense given your average ticket.

The right question isn't "what percentage of revenue should I spend?"

The right question is: **"What am I paying to get a booked job, and does that number work at my average ticket?"**

That's the one metric that tells you whether your marketing is working or bleeding you.

**Target cost per booked job** is the column that actually matters. It's calculated as 8 to 12% of your average ticket. A company doing significant water heater and repipe work at $1,800+ average has more room on the paid channel side.

The companies hitting the left side of these ranges are running strong GBP programs, consistent review velocity, and well-optimized LSA campaigns. The ones on the right side are usually running blended Google Ads campaigns without segmentation, paying for shared aggregator leads, or missing calls that cost them on every channel simultaneously.

## The Budget Formula

### Step 1: Find your average ticket by job type

Total revenue for the last 12 months divided by total jobs completed gives you a blended average. But in plumbing, the blend hides the real story. Break it out:

- Emergency service calls (leak, clog, no hot water)
- Water heater replacement/installation
- Drain cleaning
- Repipe / full service
- Remodel / new construction rough-in

Each job type has a different average ticket and a different marketing cost tolerance. A $280 drain cleaning call cannot support a $150 paid lead cost. A $3,200 repipe can support $350 without breaking the math.

### Step 2: Set your maximum cost per booked job by job type

The formula: 8 to 12% of average ticket per job type.

| Job Type | Avg Ticket | Max Cost/Booked Job |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Emergency service | $380 | $30 to $46 |
| Water heater replacement | $1,850 | $148 to $222 |
| Drain cleaning | $280 | $22 to $34 |
| Repipe | $6,500 | $520 to $780 |
| Remodel rough-in | $3,200 | $256 to $384 |

This table tells you which channels can work for which job types. Emergency service calls can only be acquired cheaply through GBP and referrals. Water heater and repipe work has room for LSAs. Remodel work has room for Google Ads and even targeted direct mail.

### Step 3: Find your current close rate by call type

Of every call that comes in for each job type, what percentage becomes a booked, completed job? Most plumbing companies believe their close rate is higher than it is. Track it for 30 days from call-tracking data before estimating.

Industry data shows home services close rates averaging 12 to 15% on total leads at the channel level -- which accounts for the full chain from impression to booked job. Within your answered and contacted calls, close rates on emergency plumbing work typically run 65 to 80% because urgency drives the decision. Planned work (water heaters, repiping) runs 35 to 55%.

### Step 4: Calculate calls needed

How many jobs do you want to book per month? Divide by your close rate on the call type.

Example: 60 emergency service calls per month needed, 70% close rate = 86 inbound calls needed. 15 water heater jobs per month needed, 45% close rate = 33 inbound calls needed.

### Step 5: Calculate your maximum monthly budget

Multiply calls needed by your maximum cost per call threshold (cost per booked job x close rate).

Emergency calls: 86 calls needed x $30 max cost per call = **$2,580/month maximum** for emergency call acquisition. If you're spending more than that on channels generating emergency calls, you're above your threshold.

## The 3 Places Plumbing Companies Leak Marketing Budget

### Leak 1: Buying Paid Leads for Job Types That Can't Support Them

Emergency plumbing calls -- burst pipe at 9pm, no hot water Monday morning -- are the most urgent leads in the trade. They're also the lowest average ticket in the job mix. A homeowner who needs a leak fixed is not comparison shopping. They're calling the first result that looks legitimate.

That urgency makes them cheap to acquire through GBP: $5 to $25 per call from the map pack. It makes them expensive to acquire through paid channels: a $55 LSA lead on a $280 drain cleaning job is already at 20% of ticket before you've touched a wrench.

Most plumbing companies running Google Ads don't separate emergency repair keywords from planned replacement keywords. They're paying $80 to $150 per click for "drain cleaning" and "pipe repair" searches on jobs that average $280. The math doesn't work.

The fix: GBP handles emergency calls. LSAs handle higher-ticket urgency work (water heater failure, major leak). Google Ads handles planned replacement and remodel intent only, with service-line segmentation matching the campaign type to the ticket size that supports the spend.

**What this costs without the fix:** A company generating 40 emergency service calls per month through Google Ads at $120/call is spending $4,800/month on calls that should cost $400 through GBP. That $4,400 difference is pure waste.

### Leak 2: Missed Calls on Every Channel

The average plumbing company answers **68% of inbound calls**. That means 32 out of every 100 calls -- regardless of which channel generated them -- go unanswered. At $55 cost per call and 200 calls per month, that's **$3,520/month** spent generating calls nobody answers.

Plumbing has a specific version of this problem: emergency calls don't wait. A homeowner with a burst pipe calls two or three plumbers simultaneously. The one who answers wins the job before the others finish hearing the voicemail message. Missing an emergency call isn't just a missed lead -- it's a guaranteed loss to whoever answered.

The [phone revenue calculator](/tools/phone-calculator) shows the dollar cost of your current answer rate at your actual call volume and average ticket. Most plumbing owners who run it for the first time find the number is larger than their entire monthly marketing budget.

**The fix:** Track answer rate by hour. Identify the windows where calls most commonly go unanswered -- early morning (before the office opens), lunch, and after 5pm are the most common gaps. An answering service covering those windows at $300 to $500/month recovers missed calls that represent multiples of that cost in revenue.

### Leak 3: No Tracking From Channel to Booked Job

If you can't answer these four questions right now, you have a measurement problem:

1. What's my cost per booked job this month, by channel?
2. Which channel produced the most booked jobs last month?
3. What's my average ticket by channel?
4. What's my close rate on calls from LSAs vs. GBP vs. Google Ads?

Most plumbing companies can't. They know what they spent on marketing. They don't know what each channel produced. That means every budget decision -- whether to add to Google Ads, whether to cut Angi, whether to scale LSAs -- is a guess.

Agencies that can't show you cost per booked job by channel are reporting on their activity, not your results. The data gap gives any underperforming channel unlimited cover: if you can't show that it's not working, the invoice keeps getting paid.

CallRail ($45/month) solves the call-tracking problem. ServiceTitan, Housecall Pro, and Jobber all have channel attribution built in. The infrastructure cost is low. The discipline to review it weekly is the harder part.

## How Urgent vs. Planned Work Changes the Budget Formula

This is the dimension most plumbing marketing advice skips: your job mix determines what channels make sense, not just your revenue.

A plumbing company doing 70% emergency service calls and 30% planned replacement work needs a completely different channel strategy than one doing 40% emergency and 60% water heater and repipe work.

**Emergency-heavy operations:**

- Primary channel: GBP (free, high intent, emergency searcher)
- Secondary: LSAs (exclusive, call-based, fast)
- Budget: 5 to 7% of revenue -- emergency calls don't require heavy paid acquisition if GBP is working
- Phone ops: critical. Miss the call, lose the job. Period.

**Planned-work-heavy operations:**

- Primary channels: Google Ads (segmented by job type), LSAs, direct mail to older neighborhoods
- GBP still critical for emergency coverage
- Budget: 7 to 10% of revenue -- planned work justifies paid channel investment because average ticket supports the acquisition cost
- Follow-up: essential. Planned-work leads don't decide immediately. A [three-touch follow-up sequence](/insights/the-follow-up-sequence-that-moved-close-rate-to-40-percent) within 48 hours of an estimate recovers a meaningful percentage of the leads that don't book on the first call.

Knowing which type of business you are -- and allocating budget accordingly -- is worth more than any channel optimization.

## The Real Answer to "How Much Should I Spend?"

Spend exactly as much as produces booked jobs at or below your cost-per-job threshold for each job type. Not a dollar more.

If GBP is producing emergency calls at $15 each and your threshold is $46, GBP deserves more investment -- in review velocity, profile activity, photo uploads, and the website signals that reinforce map pack position.

If LSAs are producing water heater leads at $180 each and your threshold is $222, LSAs are working. Scale the budget.

If Google Ads are producing drain cleaning leads at $340 each and your threshold is $34, Google Ads is the wrong channel for that job type regardless of what your agency's report shows.

The threshold by job type, calculated in Step 2 above, is your ceiling. Every channel gets measured against it monthly. Channels above it get cut or restructured. Channels below it get more budget.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What percentage of revenue should a plumbing company spend on marketing?

Industry guidance puts 5 to 10% of revenue as the standard range for plumbing, with 10% recommended for companies actively trying to grow. ServiceTitan's data suggests 5% is a "maintenance" budget and under-delivers on growth. The percentage matters less than cost per booked job by channel -- a company spending 6% efficiently and measuring everything will outperform one spending 10% on unmeasured channels.

### What is a good cost per booked job for a plumbing company?

The target is 8 to 12% of your average ticket per job type. On emergency service calls averaging $380: $30 to $46 per booked job. On water heater replacements averaging $1,850: $148 to $222. On repiping jobs averaging $6,500: $520 to $780. Emergency calls can only hit the low targets through GBP and referrals -- not through paid channels where lead costs start at $40 to $70.

### What marketing channels work best for plumbing companies?

Google Business Profile optimization produces the lowest cost per booked job for emergency calls -- effectively free once the profile is built correctly and review velocity is established. LSAs produce the best cost per booked job among paid channels for plumbing at $35 to $65 per lead. Google Ads works for planned replacement work (water heaters, repiping) when campaigns are separated by job type. Aggregators like Angi rarely pencil out for plumbing because the lower average ticket on service calls doesn't support the shared lead cost.

### How do I know if my plumbing marketing is working?

Track cost per booked job by channel monthly. If you can't do this because you don't have call tracking connected to your scheduling system, that's the first gap to close. A company generating 80 emergency calls per month from GBP at zero marginal cost and 20 water heater calls per month from LSAs at $180 per booked job is running a tight, measurable operation. A company spending $6,000/month without knowing what each channel produces is guessing with a significant checkbook.

### Should plumbing companies use Angi or Thumbtack for leads?

Rarely as a primary channel. Shared leads at $60 to $120 each on an average plumbing ticket of $280 to $380 produce cost per booked job that doesn't support the math once you account for contact rate and close rate. If you test aggregators, use a fixed monthly cap and measure cost per booked job weekly. Cut any aggregator channel running above 15% of your average ticket on booked jobs. For higher-ticket work (water heater, repipe), aggregators can occasionally pencil out -- but exclusive channels (LSAs, GBP) almost always outperform them at the same spend level.

### How much should a new plumbing company spend on marketing?

Under $500K revenue, start with $1,500 to $3,500/month concentrated on two channels: GBP optimization (nearly free, just time investment) and LSAs once you have 25+ reviews. Avoid Google Ads until you have enough call volume to generate the conversion data the platform needs to optimize. The priority at early stage is building reviews and establishing map pack position -- both of which compound over time and lower your cost per booked job across every future paid channel.

### What's the ROI on plumbing marketing?

Top-performing plumbing companies generate $9 to $14 in revenue for every $1 spent on marketing. The range across the industry is wide -- $4 to $20+ depending on job mix, phone performance, and channel allocation. Companies running mostly emergency service work at low ticket will naturally show lower revenue-per-marketing-dollar than those with significant water heater and repipe volume. Track revenue per marketing dollar month over month as a trend indicator alongside cost per booked job by channel.
