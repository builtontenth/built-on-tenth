The plumbing company spending $4,200/month on Google Ads and the one spending $4,200/month on LSAs and GBP are not running equivalent marketing programs.

The first is probably generating 35 to 50 leads at $85 to $120 each, booking at 35 to 45%, producing 12 to 23 booked jobs at $183 to $350 per booked job.

The second is generating 60 to 90 leads at $40 to $70 each, booking at 55 to 65%, producing 33 to 59 booked jobs at $71 to $127 per booked job.

Same monthly spend. The difference in output is not marginal -- it's 2 to 3x more booked jobs from the same budget. The gap lives entirely in how each channel produces leads and what type of homeowner responds to each.

This article breaks down cost per booked job for every major plumbing lead channel. Not cost per lead -- the number that makes expensive channels look cheap -- but cost per booked job, which is the only metric that connects marketing spend to revenue.

Plumbing has economics that are distinct from HVAC and roofing, and the channel comparison has to account for them: lower average tickets on service calls, higher urgency driving faster decisions, and the split between emergency calls (cheap to acquire organically, expensive to acquire through paid channels) and planned replacement work (worth paying for through targeted channels).

## The Formula Behind Every Number in This Article

Before the channel breakdown:

**Cost per booked job = Cost per lead / (Contact rate x Booking rate on contacted leads)**

Two scenarios applied consistently across all channels below:

**Strong ops:** 85% contact rate, 60% booking rate on contacts. Reflects fast response, consistent follow-up, and a CSR who makes the booking ask on every planned-work call.

**Average ops:** 62% contact rate, 40% booking rate. Reflects industry-average answer rate and booking rate from the plumbing benchmarks established across the Built on Tenth dataset.

Every cost per booked job figure in the tables below uses these two scenarios. The channel determines the lead cost and baseline intent. Your phone performance determines which scenario you're actually running.

The single most important takeaway before reviewing any channel: improving your contact rate and booking rate from average to strong ops halves your cost per booked job on every source simultaneously. ## The Emergency vs. Planned Work Split

Before evaluating channels, know your job mix. Plumbing has a wider ticket spread than HVAC or roofing, and the channel economics are job-type specific.

| Job Type | Avg Ticket | Max Cost/Booked Job (10%) | Channel that works |
|---|---|---|---|
| Emergency service | $380 | $38 | GBP, referrals only |
| Drain cleaning | $290 | $29 | GBP, referrals only |
| Water heater replacement | $1,850 | $185 | LSAs, Google Ads |
| Repipe | $6,500 | $650 | Google Ads, direct mail |
| Remodel rough-in | $3,200 | $320 | Google Ads, direct mail |

The emergency and drain cleaning rows are not a typo. At $38 and $29 max cost per booked job, the only channels that work economically for those job types are GBP organic calls and referrals -- both of which produce calls at effectively zero marginal cost. Any paid channel charging $40+ per lead on a $290 drain cleaning call is mathematically unprofitable before you've answered the phone.

This is the core insight that separates plumbing channel economics from HVAC: your lowest-volume paid channel (LSAs) is the right entry point for paid acquisition, but only for job types with average tickets above $1,000. For everything below that, the money is in owning the map pack and generating calls for free.

## Channel 1: Google Business Profile (Organic Map Pack)

**CPL: $0 to $25**
**Contact rate: 80 to 90% (homeowner chose to call you specifically)**
**Booking rate: 65 to 80% (emergency) / 45 to 60% (planned)**
**Cost per booked job: $0 to $55**

GBP organic calls are the most valuable lead type in plumbing by a significant margin. A homeowner who searched "emergency plumber near me" or "water heater repair [city]" and called from the map pack is already qualified: they're local, they have a specific problem, and they chose your listing over two others on the same screen.

The contact rate on a GBP call that gets answered is effectively 100% -- you already have them. The question is only booking rate, which runs high because the caller is motivated.

Plumbing companies ranking in the top three map pack positions generate 20 to 50 organic calls per month in most markets, at $0 marginal cost per call. That's the comparison point for every paid channel.

The investment is in building and maintaining position: review velocity, weekly GBP posts, consistent photo uploads, and website signals. The [plumbing GBP optimization guide](/insights/plumbing-google-business-profile-optimization) covers the complete setup. The cost of that investment -- primarily time -- produces the lowest cost per booked job of any channel by a factor of 3 to 10x vs. paid alternatives.

**What keeps plumbing companies from capturing this:** most have a partially optimized GBP sitting at position 4 or 5 in the map pack because their review velocity is 1 to 2 per month when their top competitors are adding 6 to 8. That's not a setup problem -- it's an operations problem with a clear fix.

## Channel 2: Referrals

**CPL: $0 to $40 (if you run a referral incentive program)**
**Contact rate: 95%+ (warm intro)**
**Booking rate: 70 to 80%**
**Cost per booked job: $0 to $57**

Referrals produce the highest close rates of any lead type in plumbing -- 70 to 80% -- because the homeowner has pre-qualified you through someone they trust. They're not comparison shopping. They may not even ask for your price before saying yes.

Most plumbing companies treat referrals as organic luck. Top performers systematize them with three specific behaviors: asking every completed customer at job close ("if you know anyone who needs plumbing work, we'd really appreciate the referral"), leaving a business card or magnet at every job site, and following up 30 days post-job with a text that includes an explicit ask.

A formal referral incentive -- $25 to $50 per referred job -- is optional but increases referral rate meaningfully. The economics work at any ticket size: a $50 incentive on a $380 emergency call is 13% of ticket and still produces a booked job for less than any paid channel.

## Channel 3: Google LSAs

**CPL: $35 to $65 (most US plumbing markets)**
**Contact rate: 75 to 85% (calls direct to your phone)**
**Booking rate: 55 to 65% on contacted leads**
**Cost per booked job: $63 to $157 (strong ops), $130 to $262 (avg ops)**

LSAs are the correct entry point for paid plumbing lead generation. Leads are exclusive -- the same contact doesn't go to three competitors simultaneously. Over 90% of LSA leads arrive as phone calls, which is the highest-intent format available in paid advertising. The Google Verified badge (formerly Google Guaranteed) signals to homeowners that your company has passed background and insurance verification.

Plumbing LSA leads average $40 to $75 per lead nationally, with The Media Captain's aggregated data showing approximately $69 as a median. Pipeline On's 2026 data shows $35 to $65 for plumbing across most markets, with water heater and drain cleaning queries at the higher end. BestPPC's analysis confirms LSAs consistently produce lower cost per booked job than Google Ads for plumbing even when the lead cost appears higher -- because the booking rate advantage offsets the price difference.

The practical scenario from Home Service Direct's LSA data: 50 plumbing LSA leads at $55 each ($2,750 spend), closing 18 customers at $425 average job = $7,650 revenue and a 2.8:1 ROAS. At strong ops performance (60% booking rate vs. their example's 36%), the same $2,750 produces 33 booked jobs at $1,650 more in revenue.

**The one setting that changes LSA economics:** response time. Google tracks how quickly you answer calls and uses it as a ranking signal. A plumbing company that answers within 60 seconds ranks higher, receives more lead volume, and pays lower effective cost per lead than one that frequently misses calls. The operational requirement and the marketing requirement are the same action.

## Channel 4: Google Ads (Search -- Segmented)

**CPL: $80 to $180 (varies by job type and market)**
**Contact rate: 65 to 78% (landing page to call)**
**Booking rate: 45 to 55% on contacted leads**
**Cost per booked job: $187 to $615 (strong ops), $296 to $972 (avg ops)**

Google Ads produce plumbing leads at $80 to $180 depending on market density and campaign structure. The range is wide because campaign structure matters more than any other variable: a company running one blended "plumber" campaign will pay $150 to $180 per lead while a company running separate campaigns for water heater replacement, repipe, and emergency repair will pay $80 to $120 on the high-intent job-type keywords.

At strong ops and $1,850 average water heater ticket, Google Ads can produce booked jobs at $187 to $400 -- within the 10% threshold on that job type. At $380 emergency ticket, the math breaks immediately: $187 per booked job is 49% of ticket. Google Ads should not be used for emergency or low-ticket plumbing search terms.

The segmentation discipline that makes Google Ads viable for plumbing:

- Campaign 1: Water heater replacement keywords ("water heater replacement cost," "install new water heater [city]") -- highest ticket, supports paid acquisition
- Campaign 2: Repipe and major service keywords ("whole house repipe," "sewer line replacement") -- highest ticket, most room for acquisition cost
- Campaign 3: Branded defense -- your company name and common misspellings. CPL at $20 to $35. Non-negotiable.
- No campaign: Emergency service, drain cleaning, or repair keywords at ticket sizes below $800

The same campaign structure principles from the [HVAC Google Ads article](/insights/hvac-google-ads-campaign-structure) apply directly to plumbing -- the segmentation logic is trade-agnostic. What differs is which keywords belong in which campaign based on plumbing's specific ticket distribution.

## Channel 5: Angi / HomeAdvisor

**CPL: $55 to $120 (shared leads)**
**Contact rate: 40 to 60% (speed-to-call dependent)**
**Booking rate: 25 to 40% on contacted leads**
**Cost per booked job: $229 to $480 (strong ops), $458 to $960 (avg ops)**

Angi leads for plumbing present the same structural problem they present for HVAC and roofing: the same contact goes to up to three contractors at once, making contact rate and response speed the primary determinants of whether you recover any value from the lead.

The math on a $90 shared plumbing lead:

- Strong ops (75% contact, 40% booking): $300 per booked job
- Average ops (50% contact, 30% booking): $600 per booked job

At a $380 emergency service ticket, $300 per booked job is 79% of ticket. That doesn't work. At a $1,850 water heater ticket, $300 per booked job is 16% -- workable but expensive relative to LSAs.

Plumbing has less justification for Angi than HVAC because the ticket distribution is lower. The share of calls that have tickets high enough to support shared-lead acquisition costs is smaller. Most plumbing companies running Angi at scale are generating emergency and service calls that can't support the economics, mixed with occasional higher-ticket calls that can -- and the blended number looks acceptable while the per-job-type breakdown shows the channel only makes sense on a fraction of the work it's generating.

**When Angi is defensible for plumbing:** exclusively for higher-ticket planned work (water heater, repipe, remodel), with five-minute response time, three-touch follow-up, and monthly cost-per-booked-job tracking by source. If any of those conditions aren't in place, Angi spend should be redirected to LSAs.

## Channel 6: Facebook / Meta Ads

**CPL: $30 to $80 (varies by offer)**
**Contact rate: 50 to 65%**
**Booking rate: 25 to 35% on contacted leads**
**Cost per booked job: $131 to $320 (strong ops), $208 to $508 (avg ops)**

Facebook leads in plumbing behave differently from Google-based leads. The homeowner wasn't searching -- they were interrupted. That lower intent shows up in contact rate and booking rate, even when the lead cost appears attractive.

Where Facebook works for plumbing:

**Water heater promotion campaigns.** "Is your water heater over 10 years old? Get a free inspection." Targeted at homeowners in zip codes with older housing stock, this offer generates planned-work leads at $40 to $60 each. The homeowner is somewhat warm -- they're aware of the potential issue -- and the ticket ($1,850+) supports the acquisition cost even at moderate booking rates.

**Financing promotion campaigns.** "$0 down, 0% interest for 12 months on water heater installation." This converts better on Facebook than a service ad because the monthly payment framing addresses the purchase hesitation directly. The connection between financing offers and close rate is covered in detail in the context of the broader plumbing marketing playbook.

**Retargeting website visitors.** Homeowners who visited your website and didn't convert already know your company. Facebook retargeting reaches them at $10 to $30 per lead and books at 2 to 3x the rate of cold Facebook traffic.

Where Facebook doesn't work for plumbing: emergency and repair calls. The homeowner with an active leak is on Google, not scrolling Facebook. Cold-outreach Facebook campaigns for emergency plumbing services produce leads that are low-intent, slow to respond, and difficult to contact -- exactly the wrong match for a category where speed-to-call determines everything.

## Channel 7: Direct Mail

**CPL: $60 to $150 (highly variable)**
**Contact rate: 70 to 85% (inbound calls, high intent)**
**Booking rate: 55 to 70%**
**Cost per booked job: $101 to $390 (strong ops), $160 to $617 (avg ops)**

Direct mail in plumbing performs best for planned replacement campaigns targeting homeowners with aging equipment. Homes built between 1975 and 1990 have water heaters and galvanized piping approaching end-of-life. A mailer to those zip codes that says "Is your water heater over 12 years old? Most fail between years 10 and 15. Get a free inspection before it fails" generates inbound calls from homeowners who are ready to have the conversation.

The contact rate on direct mail is high because the homeowner initiates the call -- there's no lead form or intermediary, just a phone number they dialed themselves. Booking rate is also strong for the same reason.

The cost economics only work at higher ticket sizes. At $60 to $150 per lead and typical response rates of 0.5 to 1.5% on direct mail, the cost per contact is high enough that it needs to be applied to water heater and repipe work, not emergency service or drain cleaning.

Companies at $1M+ revenue in markets with significant aging housing stock find direct mail producing water heater leads at $180 to $350 per booked job -- competitive with Google Ads at a lower management burden, and reaching homeowners who aren't actively searching but have latent need.

## How Phone Performance Changes Every Row

The "strong ops" vs. "average ops" gap in the table above produces a 2 to 2.5x cost difference across every channel. That multiplier applies simultaneously to every source you're running.

A company moving from average ops (62% contact, 40% booking) to strong ops (85% contact, 60% booking) on its existing channel mix doesn't just improve one source's economics -- it improves all of them. The same LSA budget that was producing booked jobs at $220 starts producing them at $100. The Google Ads budget that was producing water heater jobs at $600 starts producing them at $280.

That's why improving phone performance is almost always a higher-ROI investment than switching channels. Switching channels optimizes one part of the equation. Improving phone performance multiplies the output of every source simultaneously.

## Building Your Own Channel Comparison

The benchmarks above are industry ranges. Your actual cost per booked job by source is the number that drives budget decisions for your specific operation.

**Three months of clean data is enough to build a reliable comparison:**

For each channel, track monthly:
1. Total leads / contacts received
2. Total contacts made (conversations had)
3. Total estimates set or jobs quoted
4. Total signed or booked jobs
5. Total revenue from those jobs
6. Total spend on that source

Divide total spend by signed jobs. That's your cost per booked job for that source.

If you don't have this data for the last 90 days, build the tracking today. CallRail ($45/month) handles call attribution automatically if your CRM isn't doing it. Even rough source attribution -- "how did you hear about us" at the first call -- produces directional insight that the blended number hides completely.

## The Budget Sequencing That Works

Given the channel economics above, here's how plumbing companies should sequence channel investment as they grow:

**Under $500K:** GBP optimization + LSAs once 25+ reviews are established. Nothing else. Emergency calls should be free (GBP). Higher-ticket planned work can be paid (LSAs). The budget for everything else goes toward building review velocity that makes GBP more productive.

**$500K to $1.5M:** GBP + LSAs at 30 to 40% of paid budget + segmented Google Ads for water heater and repipe keywords only. No broad HVAC-style campaigns. No emergency keywords in paid.

**$1.5M to $5M:** GBP + LSAs + segmented Google Ads + direct mail to aging housing zip codes for water heater campaigns. Channel-level cost per booked job tracking is non-negotiable at this stage. Every channel gets measured monthly. Channels above 15% of average ticket get cut or restructured before budget renewal.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the cheapest lead source for plumbing companies?

By cost per booked job, Google Business Profile organic calls and referrals are the cheapest -- effectively zero marginal cost once the system is running. Among paid channels, Google LSAs produce the lowest cost per booked job for plumbing at $63 to $157 for operations with strong phone performance. Angi and Google Ads produce similar or higher costs per booked job at significantly less favorable booking rate dynamics.

### Are Angi leads worth it for plumbing?

Only on higher-ticket planned work (water heater replacement, repipe) and only with five-minute response time and three-touch follow-up. On emergency service and drain cleaning calls with average tickets below $400, shared lead costs at $55 to $120 per lead produce cost per booked job numbers that exceed 20 to 30% of ticket -- unprofitable at any booking rate. The full math is in the [plumbing marketing playbook](/insights/plumbing-company-marketing-playbook).

### How do Google LSAs compare to Google Ads for plumbing?

LSAs produce lower cost per booked job in most scenarios because leads arrive as direct phone calls (90%+ of LSA contacts), are exclusive to your company, and carry the Google Verified trust signal. Google Ads produce higher raw CPL and lower booking rate but allow more specific keyword targeting for planned replacement work. The right answer for most plumbing companies above $1M revenue is both: LSAs as the primary paid channel, Google Ads segmented exclusively to water heater and repipe keywords.

### Why doesn't paid advertising work well for emergency plumbing calls?

Emergency plumbing tickets ($280 to $450) are too low to support paid lead costs. Any paid channel charging $40+ per lead on a $320 drain cleaning call produces a cost per booked job above 10% of ticket -- the maximum sustainable threshold. Emergency calls are acquired most economically through GBP organic (map pack position) where marginal cost per call is near zero. Paying for emergency leads through Google Ads, LSAs, or Angi at scale destroys margin on the jobs that represent the highest call volume in most plumbing operations.

### What is a good cost per booked job for plumbing?

The target is 8 to 12% of your average ticket by job type. Emergency service at $380 average: $30 to $46. Water heater replacement at $1,850 average: $148 to $222. Repipe at $6,500 average: $520 to $780. These thresholds determine which channels are defensible for which job types -- and make clear why paid advertising only works in plumbing when it's targeted at higher-ticket planned replacement work rather than the full service menu.

### How does phone performance affect cost per booked job?

Dramatically. Moving from average ops (62% contact rate, 40% booking rate) to strong ops (85% contact rate, 60% booking rate) cuts cost per booked job by 50 to 60% on every channel simultaneously. A $65 LSA lead costs $262 per booked job at average ops and $125 at strong ops. The same improvement applied across a $5,000/month multi-channel spend produces 20 to 30 additional booked jobs per month -- more than any channel reallocation would generate.

### Should I track cost per booked job or cost per lead?

Cost per booked job. Cost per lead is the number agencies and platforms report because it makes their performance look better. A $35 lead that books at 20% costs $175 per booked job. A $65 lead that books at 60% costs $108 per booked job. The $35 lead looks cheaper and produces worse results. Cost per booked job is the only metric that connects marketing spend to revenue, and it's the only basis for a sound channel allocation decision.
