Most plumbing owners think their phone problem is a volume problem. Not enough calls coming in. The season is slow. The ads aren't working.

But pull the actual call data -- answer rates, booking rates, call types, follow-up rates -- and the picture almost always looks different. The calls are coming in. They're just not converting.

The industry average booking rate for plumbing companies is **40%**, according to data from ServiceTitan across thousands of home service businesses, with other sources citing a real-world average closer to 40% despite most owners expecting 70%. The average answer rate across plumbing operations runs around **68%**.

Run those numbers on a company taking 250 calls a month at a $380 average ticket:

- 250 calls x 68% answer rate = **170 answered calls**
- 170 x 40% booking rate = **68 booked jobs**
- 68 x $380 = **$25,840/month**

Now improve both numbers to top-quartile performance -- 88% answer rate, 62% booking rate:

- 250 x 88% = 220 answered calls
- 220 x 62% = **137 booked jobs**
- 137 x $380 = **$52,060/month**

That's a **$26,220/month difference** -- over $314,000 annually -- from the same call volume, the same market, and the same marketing spend. No additional leads purchased. No new channels. Just phone performance moving from industry average to top quartile.

This article covers where that gap comes from in plumbing specifically, why the dynamics are different from HVAC, and the three fixes that recover the most revenue.

## Why Plumbing Phone Performance Is Its Own Problem

The [HVAC CSR performance analysis](/insights/why-hvac-csrs-are-losing-you-300k-per-year) covers the call conversion mechanics that apply across home service trades. The numbers look similar -- average booking rates in the 40 to 45% range, missed calls running 32% on average. The structural causes in plumbing are different enough to warrant separate treatment.

**Plumbing has two fundamentally different call types that require different handling.**

An emergency plumbing call -- pipe burst at 9pm, no hot water Monday morning, active leak -- is a homeowner in crisis. They're calling two or three plumbers at the same time. The one who answers and can commit to an arrival window wins the job in the first 60 seconds. The booking rate on an emergency call that gets answered promptly runs 75 to 85%. The urgency does most of the selling.

A planned work call -- water heater quote, estimate for a repipe, fixture replacement -- is a different call entirely. The homeowner is not in crisis. They're comparing options. They may call back tomorrow. The booking rate on a first-touch planned work call runs 35 to 50%, and that's where follow-up and the booking ask matter most.

Most plumbing CSRs handle both call types identically. That's the primary training gap in plumbing phone operations -- treating every call like either urgent or non-urgent, rather than reading the situation and adjusting the approach.

**The emergency call tax on your metrics.**

A plumbing company taking 60% emergency calls and 40% planned work calls has a blended booking rate that looks acceptable -- maybe 48% -- while hiding the fact that emergency call answer rate is 55% (many go to voicemail because nobody's answering at 8pm) and planned work booking rate is 35% (nobody's making the ask or following up).

Both problems are real. Both cost money. They require different solutions. But if you're only looking at the blended number, you can't see either one clearly.

## The Three Failures That Cost Plumbing Companies the Most

### Failure 1: After-Hours Calls Go to Voicemail

Plumbing has a problem that HVAC doesn't face at the same intensity: a significant percentage of high-value emergency calls arrive outside business hours.

A homeowner discovers a burst pipe at 9pm. A water heater stops working on Saturday morning. A sewer backs up on Sunday afternoon. These are the calls with the highest urgency, the fastest decision windows, and the highest willingness to pay emergency rates -- and they're the calls most plumbing companies route to voicemail.

The math is brutal. An emergency plumbing call at 9pm goes to voicemail. The homeowner calls the next company on the list. That company answers. They get the job. Your voicemail gets a "never mind" message in the morning.

At a $380 average emergency ticket and a booking rate of 80% on answered urgent calls, each after-hours missed call represents approximately $304 in lost revenue. A company taking 15 after-hours emergency calls per week and answering zero of them is leaving **$4,560/week** -- **$237,000/year** -- on calls they never heard ring.

An answering service covering after-hours costs $300 to $600/month. At $380 average ticket and 80% booking rate on captured calls, recovering three additional after-hours calls per week covers the annual answering service cost in the first month.

**What top-performing plumbing companies do:** An on-call number or answering service that captures name, address, problem description, and confirms a callback window within 30 minutes. The on-call tech handles the callback. Jobs get dispatched. Voicemail doesn't compete.

### Failure 2: The Booking Ask Doesn't Happen on Planned Work Calls

Emergency plumbing calls mostly book themselves. The urgency handles the selling. Planned work calls are where CSR skill determines whether the call becomes a job or becomes a lost lead.

The typical pattern on a planned work call:

The homeowner describes needing a water heater replaced. The CSR confirms the service area. Answers a few questions about timing and price range. The homeowner says "okay I'll think about it and call back." The CSR says "sounds good, we're here whenever you're ready."

Nobody asked for the appointment. The homeowner called two other plumbers. One of them asked "can we get someone out Tuesday to take a look -- morning or afternoon works better for you?" and got the job.

This is the same failure documented in the HVAC CSR article -- the CSR is helping, not selling. The difference in plumbing is that planned work calls are the ones most likely to produce higher-ticket jobs (water heaters, repiping, installation work) that justify the marketing spend to generate them in the first place.

**The script difference:**

Undertrained CSR on a water heater call:
> Caller: "My water heater isn't working and I think I need a new one."
> CSR: "Sure, we can definitely help with that. We do water heater replacements. Depending on the unit, it usually runs somewhere between $1,200 and $2,500."
> Caller: "Okay, let me get a couple quotes and I'll call you back."
> CSR: "Sounds good, just give us a call."

Trained CSR on the same call:
> Caller: "My water heater isn't working and I think I need a new one."
> CSR: "We can definitely get that sorted out. Our tech will come out, confirm what's going on, and give you a firm price before anything gets done. Are mornings or afternoons better for you this week?"

One sentence. A direct move toward scheduling instead of providing a price range that invites comparison. The difference between a call that books and one that comparison-shops.

### Failure 3: Planned Work Leads Get Zero Follow-Up

A homeowner calls about repiping their house, doesn't book immediately, and says they'll think about it. In most plumbing companies, that's the last contact. The CSR logs the call. Nobody follows up. The homeowner chooses whoever called back.

Data from across home service companies shows that most businesses make **fewer than one follow-up attempt** on unbooked calls. In plumbing, where the average planned-work job runs $1,800 to $6,500, each unworked follow-up represents significant foregone revenue.

The mechanics are straightforward: a call that doesn't book immediately gets a follow-up text within two hours and a return call within 24 hours. That sequence alone recovers 20 to 30% of calls that didn't book on first contact, according to observed results across home service companies.

The [4-touch follow-up sequence](/insights/the-follow-up-sequence-that-moved-close-rate-to-40-percent) covers the exact timing, scripts, and tracking setup in full. The plumbing-specific adjustment is the urgency framing on Touch 3 for higher-ticket jobs -- a water heater that's already showing failure signs genuinely needs to be addressed before it fails completely, and saying so is factual information rather than pressure.

## The Specific Cost of the After-Hours Gap

Most plumbing company owners have never quantified what their after-hours gap actually costs. Here's how to calculate it for your operation:

**Step 1:** Pull your last 30 days of call data from your phone system or call tracking. Filter for calls that arrived after 5pm and before 8am, plus weekends.

**Step 2:** Count how many of those calls went to voicemail or were unanswered.

**Step 3:** Multiply by your emergency ticket average and your booking rate on answered emergency calls.

Example: 40 after-hours calls last month, 28 went to voicemail, $380 emergency average, 78% booking rate on answered calls.

28 missed after-hours calls x 78% x $380 = **$8,294 in monthly lost revenue** from after-hours alone.

Most plumbing owners who run this calculation for the first time find it's $5,000 to $15,000 per month depending on call volume and service area. An answering service at $400/month that captures 70% of those contacts and converts at 70% booking rate produces $2,400 to $5,600 in recovered revenue per month. The ROI is typically 4:1 or better within the first 30 days.

## How to Track CSR Performance by Individual

Team-level booking rates hide the most important data point: the spread between your best and worst CSR.

In most plumbing companies with two or more people handling calls, the top performer books 60 to 68% of answered calls. The bottom performer books 28 to 38%. The blended average looks acceptable -- maybe 48% -- while one person is generating jobs at nearly double the cost per booked call of the other.

**What to track by individual CSR, monthly:**

- Total calls handled
- Calls resulting in a booked appointment
- Individual booking rate
- Calls by type (emergency vs. planned work) if your system supports it
- Unbooked calls that received a follow-up attempt

Most field service platforms -- ServiceTitan, Housecall Pro, Jobber -- surface this data with a CSR scorecard. ServiceTitan specifically includes call recording alongside booking rate by CSR, which lets you listen to the exact moment where the call loses direction.

**What to do with the data:**

A 20-point spread between best and worst CSR is normal and largely fixable with targeted training. A 35-point spread indicates a systemic gap -- either scripts are missing, the booking ask has never been trained explicitly, or the low performer is handling the wrong call mix.

Listen to five recorded calls from the bottom performer. In most cases, you'll find one or two repeating patterns: answering the price question and stopping, letting the homeowner lead to "I'll call back," or not adjusting approach between urgent and non-urgent calls. Those specific patterns have specific fixes.

## The Three Fixes, In Order

### Fix 1: Cover after-hours emergency calls

The fastest money in plumbing phone performance. An answering service or on-call number that captures name, address, issue, and commits to a callback window within 30 minutes. Not "someone will call you in the morning." A callback window tonight.

Options: a plumbing-specific answering service ($300 to $600/month), an automated text response that captures the lead and promises a 30-minute callback, or a rotating on-call tech who handles the callback himself. All three work. Voicemail doesn't.

### Fix 2: Separate your call scripts by type

Write two scripts: one for emergency calls, one for planned work.

Emergency script: confirm the problem, confirm the service area, commit to an arrival window. That's the whole script. Urgency does the rest.

Planned work script: confirm the job scope, confirm the service area, move to scheduling. Not "let me give you some pricing information." Move to scheduling. "Our tech will come out, take a look, and give you a firm number before anything gets started. Are mornings or afternoons better for you this week?"

Post both scripts in the office. Practice them. Listen to recorded calls weekly and identify where the scripts are breaking down.

### Fix 3: Build the two-touch follow-up

Every call that doesn't book immediately gets a follow-up text within two hours and a callback within 24 hours if the text doesn't produce a response. That's it. Two touches. Build the tracking -- a column in your CRM or a simple spreadsheet -- and assign one person to work it daily.

Most plumbing companies that implement this see 20 to 25% recovery on unbooked planned-work calls within the first 30 days. On a company generating 60 planned-work calls per month with 35% first-touch booking rate, that's 39 unbooked calls. Recovering 20% of those is 8 additional jobs per month. At $1,850 average water heater ticket, that's $14,800/month from a two-text follow-up system.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is a good booking rate for a plumbing CSR?

Top-performing plumbing companies book 60 to 68% of answered calls overall. Industry average is around 40% to 42%, according to ServiceTitan data across home service companies. Emergency calls book at higher rates (75 to 85% when answered promptly) because urgency drives the decision. Planned work calls book at lower rates (40 to 58% for well-trained CSRs) because the homeowner is comparing options. Track the two rates separately -- the blended number hides where the training gap is.

### What is a good phone answer rate for a plumbing company?

Top-quartile plumbing companies answer 90%+ of inbound calls, including after-hours emergency coverage. Industry average runs approximately 68%. The after-hours component is the biggest differentiator -- companies with after-hours coverage answer a materially higher percentage of their total weekly call volume than those routing everything to voicemail after 5pm. A company with 88% business-hours answer rate and no after-hours coverage has a real blended answer rate significantly below 88%.

### How much revenue do missed plumbing calls cost?

At $380 average emergency ticket, 78% booking rate on answered emergency calls, and industry-average 32% missed call rate on 250 calls/month: approximately **$23,400/month in potential missed revenue** from calls that ring and don't get answered. After-hours calls specifically contribute disproportionately -- they're high-urgency, high-intent callers who will immediately call the next plumber. Recovering after-hours calls through an answering service typically produces 4:1 ROI or better within the first month.

### How do I train my plumbing CSR to book more planned-work calls?

Write an explicit booking ask into the call script and practice it until it sounds natural. The move from answering the homeowner's questions to asking for the appointment needs to be a single sentence: "Our tech will come out, take a look, and give you a firm number before anything gets started -- are mornings or afternoons better for you this week?" Then listen to recorded calls weekly and identify the specific moment where calls break down. Most CSRs have one or two repeating patterns where calls go sideways. Those specific moments have specific fixes.

### Should plumbing companies have a separate CSR or have the dispatcher handle calls?

At $500K to $1M revenue, the dispatcher and CSR role are often the same person. Above $1M with call volume exceeding 150 calls/month, separating the roles pays for itself. A CSR focused on inbound calls and follow-up, with a separate dispatcher managing scheduling and tech communication, prevents the most common cause of missed calls: one person being on with a tech while a homeowner calls in and goes to voicemail.

### What's the biggest difference between plumbing and HVAC phone performance challenges?

Two things. First, the emergency/planned split in plumbing requires different scripts and different handling for each call type in a way that HVAC doesn't face as sharply -- most HVAC calls have similar urgency profiles. Second, the after-hours problem is more acute in plumbing because plumbing emergencies (burst pipes, sewage backups, no hot water) happen at high frequency outside business hours in ways that non-emergency HVAC calls don't. Plumbing phone performance can't be solved without after-hours coverage. HVAC can function adequately without it.

### How do I find which CSR is dragging down my booking rate?

Pull booking rate by individual from your field service platform monthly. Compare each CSR's booked appointments against their total calls handled for the same period. A 20-point spread between top and bottom performer is common. A 35-point spread indicates a systemic training gap. Listen to five recorded calls from the bottom performer and identify the repeating pattern -- most CSRs have one or two specific moments where calls consistently break down. Address those specific moments rather than general retraining.
