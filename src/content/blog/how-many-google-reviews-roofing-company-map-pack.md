There is no universal review count that gets a roofing company into the map pack.

That might be the most useful thing to say upfront, because the question "how many reviews do I need?" sends most roofers chasing the wrong number. They count the reviews of whoever's in position one and decide that's the target. Sometimes they're right. More often they're measuring the wrong thing and still not ranking after hitting it.

Here's what actually determines whether your review profile is competitive enough to rank:

Not your total count. Your total count **relative to your specific competitors**, combined with how fast you're adding reviews right now, and whether your overall rating clears the threshold homeowners actually click on.

A company with 48 reviews adding 9 per month will outrank a company with 84 reviews adding 1 per month within six months in most markets. That's the dynamic most roofers don't see because they're watching the total number and not the rate.

This article gives you the framework to find your actual target, the three signals Google weights most, and the review system that generates the right velocity without depending on customers to volunteer it.

---

## Why Total Review Count Is the Wrong Starting Point

Most articles on this topic pick a number -- "get to 50 reviews," "you need 100 to compete" -- and present it as a universal threshold. That's not how Google local ranking works.

Google ranks your roofing company against the businesses competing for the same searches in the same geographic area. It doesn't grade you against a national standard. It grades you against the companies showing up in your specific market right now.

Search Atlas's 2025 study of 3,269 local businesses found that review count is the **second most influential ranking factor** in local search, carrying 19.2% weight overall and 26% weight for top 10 map pack positions. Only proximity to the searcher ranked higher at 55.2%.

That weighting confirms reviews matter significantly. It also confirms that the review signal Google uses is relative -- it's comparing your count, velocity, and rating against the businesses around you. In some markets, 40 reviews puts you in position one. In others, 300 reviews is barely average.

The right starting point isn't a number. It's a competitive audit of your market.

## The Two-Minute Competitive Audit

Before targeting a review count, do this:

Open an incognito browser window. Search "roofing company [your city]" or "roof replacement [your city]." Look at the three companies in the map pack. For each one, note:

1. Total review count
2. Star rating
3. Most recent review date (click on the profile to see)

That tells you three things: the count floor to be competitive, the rating standard homeowners are choosing between, and whether the top companies are actively collecting or coasting on an old burst.

If the top three have 180, 140, and 95 reviews and the most recent review dates are 3 days ago, 1 week ago, and 3 months ago -- you now know two things. The 95-review company is vulnerable because they've stopped collecting. And the 140-review company is the real target, not the 180.

This audit takes two minutes. Most roofing owners have never done it.

## Review Benchmarks by Market Type

While there's no universal number, markets fall into rough tiers. These are starting points for setting a target -- your actual target comes from the competitive audit above.

### Small Markets (population under 100,000)

**Typical top-3 review count: 30 to 75**
**Velocity to maintain position: 3 to 5 per month**

The bar is lower in small markets but not absent. If the leading company has 48 reviews and you have 12, you're still at a significant disadvantage. The mistake roofers make in small markets is assuming reviews don't matter because the numbers look small. They matter -- you just don't need hundreds to compete.

One caution: small markets with dominant regional companies can punch above their weight on reviews because a single committed company can create a large gap quickly. A company in a small market with 150 reviews at 4.8 stars isn't just ranking well locally -- they're functionally immune to new competitors for as long as they maintain velocity.

### Mid-Size Markets (population 100,000 to 500,000)

**Typical top-3 review count: 75 to 200**
**Velocity to maintain position: 5 to 10 per month**

This is where most roofing companies are competing, and where the velocity gap creates the most visible ranking movement. The stronger players in these markets are typically in the 100 to 150 range and collecting consistently. One burst of 20 reviews won't carry you -- if your competitors are adding 8 per month and you're adding 1, you'll lose ground even if your total looks similar today.

Companies stuck between 40 and 70 reviews in mid-size markets often feel like their review strategy is "working" because they're getting some reviews. They're usually losing position slowly to a competitor who treats review collection as an operational system rather than an occasional push.

### Large Metro Markets (population 500,000+)

**Typical top-3 review count: 200 to 500+**
**Velocity to maintain position: 10 to 20 per month**

In competitive metros -- the Atlantas, Houstons, Phoenixes -- 200 reviews is table stakes, not a goal. Top-three positions often belong to companies with 350 to 600 reviews, and they're adding 12 to 18 per month. Velocity matters more here than anywhere because the total count gap between competitors is smaller in percentage terms and movement is driven by who's adding reviews faster right now.

New entrants in large metros face a genuine chicken-and-egg challenge: you need reviews to rank, and you need calls to complete jobs that generate reviews. The path through this is concentrating effort on three to five specific zip codes rather than the whole metro initially -- get to dominant review velocity in a tight geography, rank there first, then expand.

## The Three Signals Google Actually Weights

Review count is one input. Google weights three signals together to determine whether your review profile supports your map pack position.

### Signal 1: Review Velocity

How many new reviews you're getting per month is more important than your total count past a certain baseline. A profile adding 10 reviews per month signals an active, busy business. A profile that hit 100 reviews two years ago and has added 3 since signals a company that either slowed down or isn't asking.

Google's algorithm weights recency because it reflects current relevance. A homeowner searching for a roofer today wants to see that other homeowners chose this company recently -- not that they were popular two years ago.

The practical target: your monthly velocity should match or exceed your top competitor's. If the #1 company is adding 8 reviews per month and you're adding 2, their position is compounding against you even if you're ahead on total count today.

### Signal 2: Star Rating Threshold

The data is clear on minimum rating: roofing businesses with a **4.0+ star rating get 35% more clicks** than those with lower ratings. The practical minimum to compete is 4.3 -- below that, homeowners who receive multiple results are actively choosing competitors with higher ratings before they've read a word of the review content.

The distinction between 4.3, 4.5, and 4.8 is less important for ranking than most roofers think. A 4.4 star company with strong velocity will outrank a 4.7 star company that's stopped collecting. Where star rating matters most is conversion -- once a homeowner is choosing between the three map pack results, a 4.8 vs. 4.3 comparison influences who they call.

If your rating is below 4.3, the priority before anything else is response rate on existing reviews -- especially the negative ones. Owner responses on negative reviews that acknowledge the issue and offer resolution measurably improve click-through on profiles. A 4.2 star profile with thoughtful owner responses reads differently than a 4.2 star profile with unanswered complaints.

### Signal 3: Review Content and Keywords

Reviews that mention specific services -- "roof replacement," "hail damage repair," "installed a new metal roof" -- add semantic relevance signals that help Google understand what your business does and match it to specific search queries. A profile where reviewers use service-specific language ranks better for those specific searches than a profile full of generic "great company!" reviews.

You can't write reviews for your customers. But you can influence review content by the way you ask. "Would you mind mentioning the type of work we did?" in a text after a job produces more specific reviews than "please leave us a review." Techs who hand a specific leave-behind that says "If you have a moment, please mention what brought you to us and what we did" get reviews that include service names and location context naturally.

## What Keeps Most Roofing Companies Stuck Under 50 Reviews

The companies stuck at 40 to 60 reviews after two or three years in business almost always share the same problem: they ask inconsistently.

A few jobs generate reviews because the homeowner was especially happy and volunteered one. The rest don't because nobody asked at the right moment. The review count grows slowly and randomly -- a few per quarter -- and every time the owner checks, they're roughly where they were.

The pattern has three specific causes:

**The ask is verbal, not sent.** A tech saying "please leave us a review" at the end of a job generates a fraction of the reviews that a direct text with a clickable link generates. The homeowner means to do it. Life gets in the way. The link removes the friction.

**The ask comes too late.** Asking a week after a job, via an automated email that lands in the promotions tab, produces minimal results. The moment of highest satisfaction is immediately after the job is completed and the tech is leaving. That's the window. Texts sent within one hour of job completion produce significantly higher review conversion rates than messages sent the next day.

**There's no accountability.** If review collection isn't tracked by technician and reviewed monthly, nobody knows which techs are asking and which aren't. The company's review count reflects the habits of whoever happens to remember, rather than a consistent process.

## The Review System That Actually Produces Velocity

This is the minimum viable system that most roofing companies can implement in a week:

**Step 1: Build the review request text.**

Create a text message template stored on every tech's phone. It should be short, personal, and include a direct link to your Google review page. Something like:

> "Hi [name], it was great to get your roof sorted out today. If you have two minutes, a Google review means the world to a small business. Here's the link: [direct URL]. Thank you."

The direct URL is key. Don't send them to your website to find the review link. Generate a direct Google review link from your GBP and use that.

**Step 2: Send it within one hour of job completion.**

Make this a stated expectation, not a suggestion. The tech sends the text before they leave the property or immediately after. Not at the end of the day. Not from the office later. Within the hour.

**Step 3: Track by technician monthly.**

Pull review count by month. Ask each tech how many jobs they completed that month. Calculate reviews generated per job. Share the number in a morning meeting. The techs who are asking show up clearly in the data. The ones who aren't also show up clearly.

**Step 4: Owner responds to every review within 48 hours.**

Every review -- positive and negative. For positive reviews, a two-sentence response that mentions the specific work done and thanks the homeowner. For negative reviews, an acknowledgment and an invitation to resolve it offline. This takes 10 to 15 minutes per week and is a visible signal to every homeowner who reads your profile before calling.

GBP profiles with 100 or more photos also get significantly more calls than those with fewer images, according to local SEO benchmarks. While this isn't a review-specific signal, it compounds with review velocity -- an active, well-photographed profile with consistent reviews signals the same thing: a busy, legitimate, current operation.

## How Review Velocity Compounds Over Time

Here's the math that makes velocity more important than current count in most markets:

**Scenario A:** Company at 85 reviews, adding 2 per month.
**Scenario B:** Company at 55 reviews, adding 8 per month.

At 12 months: Company A has 109. Company B has 151.
At 24 months: Company A has 133. Company B has 247.

Company B overtook Company A in total count around month 5 and never looked back. Every month the gap widened. By year two, Company B's profile reads as dramatically more active and current.

This is the dynamic that makes review velocity the most important metric for a roofing company stuck under 50 reviews competing against companies with larger counts. You don't need to catch up on total reviews. You need to outrun your competitors on monthly addition rate. Over 6 to 12 months, that position change is visible in the map pack.

The [HVAC marketing benchmarks analysis](/insights/hvac-marketing-benchmarks-2026-what-top-performers-spend) shows top-quartile home service companies adding 8 to 15 reviews per month vs. 2 to 4 for the industry average. The roofing benchmark is consistent with this -- and the compounding math is the same regardless of trade.

## Reviews as a Conversion Tool, Not Just a Ranking Signal

Map pack position is the visibility goal. But once a homeowner sees three results, reviews are the primary filter they use to decide who to call.

After storm season, homeowners searching for roofing companies are often receiving three simultaneous calls from contractors who bought the same Angi lead. The ones who got to the homeowner first are calling within minutes of each other. The homeowner who gets three calls in 20 minutes immediately pulls up all three company profiles to decide who to call back.

Your review profile is your close rate on every lead you've already paid for. A company with 45 reviews at 4.7 stars and recent responses loses that call to a company with 160 reviews at 4.5 stars and similar activity -- because volume and recency read as more legitimate to a homeowner under stress who doesn't have time to evaluate thoroughly.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many Google reviews does a roofing company need to rank?

There is no universal number. Review count is measured relative to your local competitors, not against a national standard. In small markets, 40 to 75 reviews may be competitive. In mid-size markets, 75 to 200. In large metros, 200 to 500+. The right target is the average of the top three companies in your specific market, plus a velocity that matches or exceeds theirs. Run the competitive audit above before setting a target.

### Does review velocity or total count matter more for roofing map pack ranking?

Velocity matters more past the early stages. A company with fewer total reviews that's adding 8 to 10 per month will outrank a company with more reviews adding 1 to 2 per month over a 6 to 12 month window in most markets. Search Atlas's 2025 local ranking study found review count carries 26% weight for top map pack positions -- and recency is embedded in how that signal is scored.

### What star rating do roofing companies need to rank?

The functional minimum to compete is 4.3 stars. Roofing businesses with 4.0+ star ratings get 35% more clicks than those with lower ratings. The difference between 4.4 and 4.8 matters more for conversion than for ranking -- a 4.4 star company with strong velocity will typically outrank a 4.7 star company that has stopped collecting. If your rating is below 4.3, respond to every existing negative review before focusing on volume.

### What is the fastest way to get more Google reviews for a roofing company?

Send a direct text with a clickable Google review link within one hour of job completion. Not an email, not a verbal ask, not a message the next day -- a text within the hour while the homeowner is still satisfied and the job is fresh. Track which techs are sending it. The companies that implement this consistently typically see monthly review velocity increase by 3 to 5x within 60 days.

### Do the keywords in reviews help roofing companies rank?

Yes. Reviews that mention specific services -- "roof replacement," "hail damage," "metal roof installation" -- add semantic relevance that helps Google match your profile to specific search queries beyond just "roofer near me." You can't write reviews for customers, but asking them to mention what type of work was done produces more useful review content naturally. Over time, a profile where dozens of reviews mention "storm damage repair" or "shingle replacement" ranks more specifically for those searches.

### Should roofing companies respond to every Google review?

Yes. Both positive and negative. Owner response rate is a visible GBP activity signal. Profiles with high response rates read as actively managed -- which correlates with real ranking signals like recency and completeness. For negative reviews specifically, a professional, non-defensive response that acknowledges the issue and offers resolution changes how other homeowners read the review. An unanswered complaint is significantly more damaging than a responded-to one.

### How long does it take for new reviews to improve map pack ranking?

Results vary by market and how large the starting gap is. Companies in mid-size markets starting at 30 to 40 reviews with a new 8 to 10 per month system typically see measurable position improvement in 60 to 90 days. Companies in large metros with larger gaps should expect 4 to 6 months of consistent velocity before significant movement. Technical fixes to GBP completeness and website mobile speed often produce faster initial movement than reviews alone -- combine both simultaneously rather than sequentially.
